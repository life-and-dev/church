# Rapture

The concept of the "Rapture" is a central theme in modern Christian eschatology,
often depicted as a sudden event where believers are gathered to meet Jesus
Christ in the air. While widely popularized in the 20th century, the origins of
this specific interpretation and the linguistic nuances of the scriptures used
to support it reveal a complex history and a variety of perspectives.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture and convinced many other Christians of this view. His perspective was
notably influenced by a period of convalescence in early 1827 following a horse
accident in October 1826. Darby's leadership and teachings attracted a growing
number of followers. The movement spread to England and other parts of the
world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with his 1970 book, "The Late Great Planet Earth."
Chapter 12, titled "The Ultimate Trip," states:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This suggests a belief that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to meet Jesus
in the air, while those remaining would face distress.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 (ESV) and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV) to suggest two
separate comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation
event to gather believers, followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish his reign on Earth.

## Scriptural analysis

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is Matthew 24:31 (ESV):

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some arguments suggest that verse 31 might be a later addition, but major
uncials like Sinaiticus and Vaticanus include it. Regardless of its textual
history, the angels gather the elect **after** the Tribulation and the return of
the Son of Man. This sequence challenges the pre-tribulation theory that the
rapture occurs before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and **they were unaware** until the flood came and swept them
> all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) . Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) . Therefore, stay awake, for
> **you do not know** on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if
> the master of the house **had known** in what part of the night the thief was
> coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken
> into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an
> hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison between the Rapture and the days of Noah centers on the element
of surprise. Jesus does not explicitly state that Noah escaped the flood in this
context. Instead, the emphasis remains on the unexpected nature of the "coming
of the Son of Man," paralleling the surprise of the flood.

The recurring theme of an unexpected event, coupled with the phrase "one will be
taken and one left," leads to the interpretation that believers will be removed
from the Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) . This
term combines ["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) , meaning "from
close" or "alongside," with ["ambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm) ,
which means "to receive," "admit," or "acknowledge." This can be understood as
being accepted into a specific position. The same root is used in other
scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife." — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is
[ἀφίεται (aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) . It means to send away
or leave alone, carrying connotations of rejection or being left unforgiven. A
related usage appears in this verse:

> but if you ***do not forgive*** others their trespasses, neither will your
> Father ***forgive*** your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

From a human perspective, two individuals might appear identical, yet one is
accepted while the other is rejected. This parallels the acceptance of a bride.
The theme of readiness for an unexpected return is further illustrated by the
three parables following this passage.

### Defense of the interpretation

Critiques of this interpretation often point out that in the days of Noah, those
"taken" were the ones swept away in judgment. However, the Greek text uses two
different words. In Matthew 24:39 (ESV), the flood "swept away" (*airo*) the
people. This word is often associated with removal in judgment. In contrast,
verses 40 and 41 use *paralambano*. This means to receive alongside or to take
as a companion.

This distinction is supported by the description of the meeting in the air in 1
Thessalonians 4:17 (ESV). The Greek word for "meeting" is *apantesis*.
Historically, this referred to a formal delegation leaving a city to meet a
visiting dignitary and escort them back into the city. This suggests that the
"taken" are received by Christ to accompany Him in His return to Earth. They are
not removed from it permanently.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory, while popularized by
[John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) and
[Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory) , faces challenges when compared to the
timing of the [gathering of the elect](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) described in
Matthew 24. A linguistic analysis of the [Greek terms](#like-the-days-of-noah)
suggests that being "taken" refers to acceptance into the Lord's presence and
service rather than an escape from tribulation. Ultimately, the scriptures and
subsequent parables emphasize that the primary call for believers is
[readiness and faithful stewardship](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) in
anticipation of His unexpected return.
