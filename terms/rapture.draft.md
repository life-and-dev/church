# The Rapture

The concept of a "secret" return of Christ has fascinated and divided believers
for centuries. While many anticipate a sudden disappearance of the faithful,
others question the historical and biblical foundations of this popular
theology. This article explores where these ideas originated and what the
original Greek texts actually reveal about being "taken" or "left."

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view during his period of
convalescence following a horse accident in late 1826. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers, and the movement spread from
Ireland to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian,
popularized Rapture theology. His 1970 book, *The Late Great Planet Earth*,
became a bestseller. In it, he stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are
> ready. But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock
> and sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at
> a time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late
> Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12

Lindsey described a scenario where Christians would be unexpectedly
"teleported" to a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and
sorrow would fall upon those left behind.

In addition, some pretribulationists interpret biblical passages such as
1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 (ESV) and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV) to suggest two
separate comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation
event to gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming
to establish his reign on Earth.

## Biblical Examination

### The Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses often quoted to support the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some critics have questioned the authenticity of this verse, suggesting it was
a later addition. However, Matthew 24:31 (ESV) is well-supported by primary
textual evidence, including major uncials like Codex Sinaiticus and Codex
Vaticanus.

Regardless of its textual history, the passage indicates that the angels gather
the elect **after** the Tribulation and the visible return of the "Son of Man."
This sequence provides a significant challenge to the theory that the rapture
occurs before the Tribulation.

### The Days of Noah

Jesus compared the coming of the Son of Man to the days of Noah:

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them
> all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in
> the field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the
> mill; one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not
> know on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of
> the house had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would
> have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore
> you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not
> expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus repeatedly emphasized that his coming would occur unexpectedly. Because
he used the words "one will be taken and one left," some believe this refers to
believers being suddenly removed from the Earth.

### Analysis of Greek Terms

A closer look at the original Greek provides further insight. The word
translated as "taken" is [*paralambanetai*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm)
(Matthew 24:40,41). This is a combination of
[*para*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) (alongside) and
[*lambano*](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm) (to take or receive). It often
conveys being "received near" or associated with oneself in an intimate act.

For example, the same root word is used when Joseph is told:

> Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife.
> — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

And when he obeys:

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

In contrast, the Greek word for "left" is
[*aphietai*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away, leave
alone, or forgive. In this context, it may imply being "rejected" or
"unforgiven." The same word is used in Matthew 6:15 (ESV):

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

These linguistic choices suggest that while two people may appear equal from a
human perspective, one is accepted like a bride being received. The other is
rejected. This interpretation is supported by the fact that *airo* (to sweep
away) is used for the judgment in the days of Noah (Matthew 24:39), whereas
*paralambano* is used for those "taken" in verses 40,41.

### Supporting Parables

This theme of being ready for an unexpected return to be accepted as stewards
is confirmed by three parables that follow:

1. **The Faithful and Wise Servant** (Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)): The master
   returns unexpectedly to reward the faithful servant with authority but
   punishes the wicked servant.
2. **The Ten Virgins** (Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)): Those who were ready went in
   with the bridegroom to the marriage feast, while the door was shut on the
   others.
3. **The Talents** (Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)): The master returns after a long
   time to settle accounts, inviting faithful servants to "enter into the joy"
   of their master while casting the worthless servant into "outer darkness."

## Defense of the Interpretation

Critics of this view often argue that being "taken" refers to being taken away
in judgment, similar to how the flood "swept away" the people in Noah's time.
They point to the immediate context of judgment as evidence.

However, a strong defense for the "accepted vs. rejected" view lies in the
specific Greek terminology. Jesus chose *paralambano* for those taken, a word
typically used for receiving someone into a positive relationship. If he meant
taking them away in judgment, he could have used *airo*, which he used just a
verse earlier to describe the flood. Furthermore, the parables that follow
consistently depict a separation where the "ready" are received into a feast or
reward, while the "unready" are left out or cast away.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory, while popularized by [John Nelson Darby](#historical-origins)
and [Hal Lindsey](#historical-origins), faces scrutiny when examined through
the lens of [Greek terminology](#analysis-of-greek-terms) and [biblical
context](#biblical-examination). The scriptures emphasize readiness for an
[unexpected return](#the-days-of-noah) and a [final
separation](#analysis-of-greek-terms) based on faithfulness, rather than a
secret escape before tribulation.
