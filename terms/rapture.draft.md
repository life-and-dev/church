# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture is a significant topic in modern Christian
eschatology, often sparking intense discussion about the end times. This
article explores how this theory developed over the centuries and examines the
biblical passages frequently cited in its support. By looking at historical
origins and linguistic nuances, one can better understand the different
perspectives held by believers today.

## Origin

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers, and the movement spread
to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian,
made the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
**a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This interpretation suggests that Christians will be unexpectedly "teleported"
to a meeting with Jesus in the air. In this view, shock and sorrow will fall
upon those who remain on Earth.

In addition, some pretribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 (ESV) and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV) to suggest two
separate comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret event occurring
before the tribulation. This event gathers believers. It is followed by a
later, visible second coming to establish his reign on Earth.

## Evidence

## Elect Gathering

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
>
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major early
manuscripts. These manuscripts include Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus.
While [some
sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textual_variants_in_the_New_Testament)
discuss minor textual variations in the New Testament, the core of this verse
is not considered a later addition to the biblical canon.

Even with the authenticity of the scripture established, the timing of the
event remains a point of debate. The passage indicates that the angels gather
the elect **after** the Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man". This
sequence of events contrasts with the pretribulational theory that the rapture
will occur before the Tribulation begins.

## Noah Analogy

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
For as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying
and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they
were unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the
coming of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
[*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
[*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
[*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
[*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is the *"Rapture"* like "the days of Noah"?

Not because Noah "escaped the flood", otherwise Jesus would have said so, but
he does not.  Instead, he repeatedly echoed that the "the coming of the Son of
Man" will be unexpected like the flood in the days of Noah.

However, because Jesus repeatedly says this event will occur unexpectedly in
combination with the words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some
people believe this means that the elect would unexpectedly be teleported from
Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This term combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning "alongside," and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning "to take" or
"receive." Lexical studies often define it as "receiving near" or "taking to
oneself." This implies a welcoming or an acceptance into a close relationship.

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife.” — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
he ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It carries the meaning of
sending away or leaving alone. It can also imply rejection or being left
without forgiveness. This usage is seen in other contexts where individuals are
released or abandoned.

For example the same word was also used in the scripture:

> But if you ***do not forgive*** others, then your Father will ***not
forgive*** your transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB)

This linguistic evidence suggests that from a human perspective, two
individuals might appear similar. However, one is accepted into fellowship
while the other is rejected or left alone. Critics of this view might argue
that "taken" could also imply being taken in judgment. They might compare it to
the flood sweeping people away. However, the use of *paralambano* in other New
Testament passages consistently points toward a positive reception rather than
a forced removal.

This theme, of being ready at any time for the Lord's unexpected return to be
accepted as stewards of his kingdom, is also confirmed by the 3 parables that
immediately follow this passage:

## Faithful Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
Truly, I say to you, he will **set him over all his possessions**.
> * But if that wicked servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and
begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
master of that servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him and
at an hour he does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him with the
hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.
>
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

## Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise. For
when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the wise
took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they all
became drowsy and slept.
>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet
him.’
>
> Then all those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps.
>
> And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for our lamps
are going out.’
> But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not be enough for us and
for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.’
>
> And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and **those who were
ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the door was shut.
>
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’  
> But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, **I do not know you**.’
>
> Watch therefore, for **you know neither the day nor the hour**.
>
> — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

## The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to
another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents
more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid
his master’s money.
>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled
accounts with them.
>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made
five talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of
your master**.’
>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you
delivered to me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of
your master**.’
>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I
knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where
you scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the
ground. Here, you have what is yours.’
> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew
that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then
you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should
have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from him and
give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will more be
given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not, even what
he has will be taken away. And **cast the worthless servant into the outer
darkness**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.’
>
> — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has evolved from its [19th-century origins](#origin) into a
widely recognized belief through popular literature. Scriptural
[evidence](#evidence) suggests that the gathering of the elect occurs after the
Tribulation. The [analogy of Noah](#noah-analogy) emphasizes the unexpected
nature of Christ's return rather than a secret disappearance. Parables like the
[Faithful Servant](#faithful-servant), [Ten Virgins](#ten-virgins), and [The
Talents](#the-talents) reinforce the theme of readiness and accountability for
all believers.
