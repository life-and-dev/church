# Rapture

The concept of a rapture has become a cornerstone of modern eschatology for
many. This event involves believers being suddenly taken up to meet Christ in
the air. But where did this idea originate? Does it align with the original
meaning of the biblical texts often cited in its defense? Exploring the
development of this theory reveals a fascinating journey. It moves through
19th-century theology and 20th-century popular culture.

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
*The Late Great Planet Earth* that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, *The Late Great
> Planet Earth*, Chapter 12.

Hal Lindsey seemed to think that Christians would be unexpectedly teleported to
a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow would fall
upon those left behind.

Some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1 Thessalonians
4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings of Christ.
They see the rapture as a secret event to gather believers before the
tribulation. This is followed by a later visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical Examination

### Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

However, textual scholarship provides a different perspective on this verse. The
Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus include this verse. Some sources suggest
variants, but the core verse is well-supported in major uncials.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the context shows that the angels gather the
elect after the tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This sequence
challenges the theory that the rapture happens before the tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
> away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the mill;
> one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on
> what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of the house
> had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed
> awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must
> be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect. — Matthew
> 24:35-44 (ESV)

The rapture is compared to the days of Noah. This comparison is not because Noah
escaped the flood. Jesus does not state that. Instead, he repeatedly echoed that
the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It will be like the flood in
the days of Noah.

Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly. He uses the words "one will be
taken and one left". Consequently, some people believe this means the elect
would be unexpectedly teleported from Earth.

Critics often point out that those who were taken in the days of Noah were the
ones swept away by the flood in judgment. However, a closer look at the Greek
text reveals an important distinction. The word for "swept away" in verse 39 is
*airo*, while the word for "taken" in verse 40 is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm).

The word *paralambanetai* combines [παρά
(para)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning alongside, and [λαμβάνω
(lambano)](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or take to
oneself. It often refers to taking someone into close association or protection.
It can be explained as being accepted into a position. For example, the same
word appears in these scriptures:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.” —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. It can imply rejection or being left in an unforgiven state. For example,
the same word appears in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus likely meant that two persons would appear equal from a human perspective.
However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other will be rejected.
This interpretation is supported by [textual
analysis](https://www.ministrymagazine.org/archive/2018/12/Taken-or-left) of the
relationship between these Greek terms.

### Parables of Readiness

The theme of being ready for an unexpected return is confirmed by three
parables. These follow the passage in Matthew.

#### Faithful and Wise Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to provide them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that
> servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you,
> he will set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to
> himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and
> eats and drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day
> when he does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him
> in pieces and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping
> and gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

#### Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not
> be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, ‘Lord, lord, open to us.’ But
> he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for
> you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

#### Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five
> talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who had the
> two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two talents;
> here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well done, good
> and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you
> over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had received the
> one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping
> where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was
> afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is
> yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You
> knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed?
> Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I
> should have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from
> him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will
> more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not,
> even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant into the
> outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. —
> Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The rapture theory emerged primarily through the work of [John Nelson
Darby](#historical-origins). It later gained widespread popularity through
[modern media](#historical-origins). A closer look at the [Greek
terms](#days-of-noah) used in the Gospels suggests a focus on divine acceptance
and rejection rather than physical teleportation. The [parables of
Jesus](#parables-of-readiness) emphasize readiness for an unexpected return.
This readiness leads to being [accepted into the
kingdom](#parables-of-readiness) or being cast out based on faithfulness.
