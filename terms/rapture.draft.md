# Rapture

The concept of the "Rapture" has become a central theme in many modern
theological discussions. This belief suggests that Christians will be suddenly
taken from Earth to meet Jesus in the air. While it is a widely recognized term
today, its historical roots and the specific interpretations of the scriptures
used to support it are subjects of significant interest. This article examines
the origins of this theory and explores how various biblical passages are
understood by different groups.

## Origin of the Theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture, and he convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers, and the movement spread
to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

In other words, it seems like he thought that Christians would be unexpectedly
"teleported" to a secret place somewhere in the air to meet Jesus, while shock
and sorrow will be upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists interpret biblical passages such as 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 as indicating two separate
comings of Christ. They view the rapture as a secret event occurring before the
Tribulation to gather believers. This event is followed by a later, visible
second coming to establish a reign on Earth.

## Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:
> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

While some discussions on [textual
variants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textual_variants_in_the_New_Testament)
exist, verse 31 is well-supported by major ancient manuscripts such as Codex
Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus.

Regardless of these textual considerations, it is noted that the angels gather
the elect **after** the Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man". This
sequence challenges the Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture will occur
before the Tribulation.

## Days of Noah
>
> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The Rapture is often compared to "the days of Noah". This comparison does not
necessarily imply that believers will escape a period of trial. Jesus did not
state that Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he repeatedly emphasized that the
coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected, similar to how the flood occurred
in the days of Noah.

Because Jesus stated that this event will occur unexpectedly in combination with
the words "one will be taken and one left," some people believe this means that
the elect will be unexpectedly taken from Earth.

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This
word is a combination of [παρά (para)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm),
which means from close or alongside, and [λαμβάνω
(lambano)](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), which means to receive, admit,
or acknowledge. In other words, it could be explained as being "accepted into
position."

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:
> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word means to send away or
leave alone, which can imply being rejected or remaining unforgiven.

For example, the same word was also used in this scripture:
> But if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus likely meant that from a human perspective, two persons would be
considered equal, but one will be accepted like a bride while the other would be
rejected.

This theme of being ready at any time for the Lord's unexpected return to be
accepted as stewards of his kingdom is also confirmed by the three parables that
immediately follow this passage.

>
> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory, popularized in the 19th and 20th centuries by figures such
as [John Nelson Darby](#origin-of-the-theory) and [Hal
Lindsey](#origin-of-the-theory), suggests a secret return of Christ to gather
his church before the Tribulation. However, a closer look at the [Gathering of
the Elect](#gathering-of-the-elect) in Matthew 24:31 indicates that this
gathering occurs after the Tribulation. Furthermore, the [Days of
Noah](#days-of-noah) passage and the subsequent parables of the [Faithful and
Wise Servant](#faithful-and-wise-servant), the [Ten Virgins](#ten-virgins), and
the [Talents](#talents) emphasize readiness for an unexpected return rather than
a secret escape, with linguistic roots for "taken" and "left" suggesting
acceptance or rejection based on a person's spiritual state.
