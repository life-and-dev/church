# Rapture

The concept of the rapture has become a cornerstone of modern end-times theology
for many believers. It suggests a secret event where the faithful are suddenly
removed from the world before a period of global tribulation. While widely
popular today, the history and biblical basis of this theory offer a complex
landscape for exploration.

## Theory Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. In 1970, he published a book titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth." The book stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are
> ready. But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible
> shock and sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take
> place at a time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
>
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Lindsey appeared to think that Christians would be unexpectedly teleported to a
secret place. They would meet Jesus in the air. Shock and sorrow would then fall
upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical Analysis

### Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is Matthew 24:31.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
>
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major uncials such
as Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. While Codex Bezae contains additional
phrases, the core verse is not a later addition to the canon.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the angels gather the elect after the
Tribulation. This occurs at the return of the "Son of Man." This sequence
challenges the Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture happens before the
Tribulation.

### Days of Noah

The comparison between the rapture and the days of Noah is often discussed.

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be
> the coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they
> were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day
> when Noah entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and
> swept them all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men
> will be in the field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be
> grinding at the mill; one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake,
> for you do not know on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if
> the master of the house had known in what part of the night the thief was
> coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be
> broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming
> at an hour you do not expect.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The rapture is compared to the days of Noah not because Noah escaped the flood.
Jesus does not make that point. Instead, he repeatedly emphasizes that the
coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It will be like the flood in the
days of Noah.

Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly. He uses the words "one will be
taken and one left." Many people believe this means the elect will be physically
removed or teleported from Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It means "to receive
near" or "take to oneself." It can be explained as being accepted into position.
The same word was used in these scriptures:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.
>
> — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife.
>
> — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This means "to send away" or
"leave alone." In this context, it may imply being rejected or remaining
unforgiven. The same word was also used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses.
>
> — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

It is likely that Jesus meant two persons would appear equal from a human
perspective. However, one will be accepted like a bride. The other will be
rejected.

Critiques of this view argue that being "taken" could refer to being taken in
judgment, similar to how the flood "swept them all away." However, the context
of the Greek word *paralambano* usually denotes a positive reception. The three
parables that follow this passage confirm this theme. They emphasize being ready
for the Lord's unexpected return to be accepted as stewards.

#### Faithful and Wise Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that
> servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to
> you, he will set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant
> says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow
> servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will
> come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he does not know
> and will cut him in pieces and put him with the hypocrites. In that place
> there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
>
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

#### Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed,
> they all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is
> the bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and
> trimmed their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your
> oil, for our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since
> there will not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and
> buy for yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came,
> and those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the
> door was shut. Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, ‘Lord, lord,
> open to us.’ But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’
> Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour.
>
> — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

#### Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid
> his master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came
> and settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents
> came forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to
> me five talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to
> him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a
> little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he
> also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to
> me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him,
> ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
> I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who
> had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be
> a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered
> no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground.
> Here, you have what is yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and
> slothful servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather
> where I scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the
> bankers, and at my coming I should have received what was my own with
> interest. So take the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten
> talents. For to everyone who has will more be given, and he will have an
> abundance. But from the one who has not, even what he has will be taken
> away. And cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness. In that place
> there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
>
> — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The rapture theory, which originated with [John Nelson Darby](#theory-origins),
suggests a secret return of Christ before the Tribulation. However, scriptural
analysis of the gathering of the elect in [Matthew 24:31](#gathering-of-the-elect)
indicates it occurs after the Tribulation. Furthermore, the selection of
those "taken" and "left" described in the [days of Noah](#days-of-noah) and the
subsequent [parables](#faithful-and-wise-servant) suggests a focus on spiritual
readiness and stewardship rather than a physical teleportation event.
