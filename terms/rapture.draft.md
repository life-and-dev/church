# Rapture

Many people have heard stories of a sudden event where individuals vanish from
Earth, leaving only their belongings behind. This concept, often called the
Rapture, remains a central theme in many modern discussions about the future.
Exploring where this idea came from and how specific ancient texts are
understood can provide a deeper understanding of this influential theory.

## Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made Rapture theology popular. He was an American
author and Christian theologian. In 1970, he published a book titled "The Late
Great Planet Earth" which stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are
> ready. But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock
> and sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place
> at a time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late
> Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This suggests that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a secret location
in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, those remaining on Earth would experience
shock and sorrow.

Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages such as 1 Thessalonians
4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 as suggesting two separate comings of
Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical arguments

### Gathering of the elect

One verse frequently quoted to support the Rapture theory is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some discussions mention textual variants in the New Testament. However,
textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major ancient
manuscripts. These include Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. Some
manuscripts like Codex Bezae contain additional phrases. The core verse remains
a well-attested part of the biblical canon.

The text indicates that the angels gather the elect after the Tribulation. This
occurs following the return of the "Son of Man". This timing contrasts with the
theory that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
> For as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking,
> marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark,
> and **they were unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so
> will be the coming of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison to the days of Noah is often used in these discussions. Jesus
did not state that Noah escaped the flood in this context. Instead, he
emphasized that the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. This follows
the pattern of the flood in the days of Noah.

Jesus stated that this event will occur unexpectedly. He also used the words
"one will be taken and one left". Consequently, some people believe this means
the elect will be removed from Earth.

Critics of this view sometimes argue that being "taken" refers to being taken
away in judgment. This would be similar to how the flood "swept them all away"
in verse 39. However, the Greek word used for being swept away in verse 39 is
`airō`. This word often carries a negative connotation of removal in judgment.
In contrast, the word used in verses 40 and 41 is `paralambanō`.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" in these verses is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)][1]. This word combines
["pará"][2], meaning alongside, and ["ambánō"][3], meaning to receive or
acknowledge. It literally means "to receive near" or "take to oneself". This
word is used in John 14:3 where Jesus promises to take his disciples to
himself. It could be interpreted as being "accepted into position" or received
in close fellowship.

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται (aphietai)][4].
This means to send away or leave alone. It can imply being rejected or
remaining unforgiven.

The same word was also used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

One interpretation suggests that while two people might appear equal from a
human perspective, one will be accepted while the other is rejected. This
theme of being ready for an unexpected return is also supported by three
parables that follow this passage.

#### Faithful servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
> Truly, I say to you, he will **set him over all his possessions**.
> * But if that wicked servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and
> begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
> master of that servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him
> and at an hour he does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him
> with the hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing
> of teeth**. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

#### Ten virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed,
> they all became drowsy and slept.
>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet
> him.’
>
> Then all those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps.
>
> And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for our lamps
> are going out.’
> But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not be enough for us and
> for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.’
>
> And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and
> **those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the
> door was shut.
>
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’  
> But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, **I do not know you**.’
>
> Watch therefore, for **you know neither the day nor the hour**.
> — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

#### Parable of talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
> he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two
> talents more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the
> ground and hid his master’s money.
>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled
> accounts with them.
>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five
> talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I
> have made five talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
> been faithful over a little; I will set you over much.
> **Enter into the joy of your master**.’
>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you
> delivered to me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
> been faithful over a little; I will set you over much.
> **Enter into the joy of your master**.’
>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I
> knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering
> where you scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your
> talent in the ground. Here, you have what is yours.’
> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew
> that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed?
> Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my
> coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take the
> talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone
> who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one
> who has not, even what he has will be taken away. And
> **cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness**. In that place there
> will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.’ — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The modern Rapture theory has [historical origins](#origins) in the 19th and
20th centuries. Its biblical basis is often linked to interpretations of the
[gathering of the elect](#gathering-of-the-elect) and the description of
individuals being [taken or left](#days-of-noah) in the days of Noah. This
theme of readiness is further developed through the parables of the
[faithful servant](#faithful-servant), the [ten virgins](#ten-virgins), and
the [talents](#parable-of-talents).
