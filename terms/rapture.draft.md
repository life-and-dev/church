# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture, a sudden event where believers are "caught up" to
meet Jesus Christ, remains one of the most discussed topics in modern Christian
eschatology. While many today consider it a foundational belief, its history and
biblical basis are subjects of significant debate. This article explores where
this theory originated, how it became popularized, and what the original Greek
texts reveal about the passages often used to support it.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He eventually convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's
leadership and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement
spread to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
>
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This perspective suggests that Christians would be unexpectedly removed from
Earth to a secret location in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow
would fall upon those left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers, followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his reign on
Earth.

## Scriptural analysis of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses often cited to support the Rapture is Matthew 24:31.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

This scripture indicates that the angels gather the elect after the Tribulation
and the return of the "Son of Man". This sequence challenges the
pretribulational theory that the Rapture occurs before the Tribulation.

## Comparison to the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus compared the coming of the Son of Man to the days of Noah. He emphasized
that the event will be unexpected, much like the flood was to those in Noah's
time. Because Jesus stated this event occurs unexpectedly alongside the words
"one will be taken and one left," some believe this refers to believers being
removed from Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This is a combination of
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning "from alongside," and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning "to receive" or
"acknowledge." This term can be understood as being "accepted" or "received
alongside."

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This term means to send away or
leave alone, often implying rejection. For example, the same word is used in
Matthew 6:15:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

The context suggests that while two people might appear similar from a human
perspective, one is accepted while the other is rejected. This theme of
remaining ready for the Lord's unexpected return is further illustrated by the
three parables that follow this passage.

## Theological defense and counter-arguments

Critics of the post-tribulational view often point to 1 Thessalonians 4:17,
which describes believers being "caught up together with them in the clouds to
meet the Lord in the air." They argue that this "meeting" implies a sudden
removal from Earth to heaven to escape the Tribulation.

> Then we who are alive, who are left, shall be caught up together with them in
> the clouds to meet the Lord in the air, and so we will always be with the
> Lord. — 1 Thessalonians 4:17 (ESV)

However, the Greek word for "meeting" ([ἀπάντησιν,
apantesin](https://biblehub.com/greek/529.htm)) was often used in ancient times
to describe a delegation of citizens who left their city to meet a visiting
dignitary and escort them back into the city. In this light, the passage can be
interpreted not as an escape from Earth, but as believers welcoming the
returning King and escorting him to his reign on Earth. This interpretation
aligns with the sequence found in Matthew 24, where the gathering of the elect
occurs after the Tribulation.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory [originated in the 19th century](#the-origin-of-the-theory)
and was later popularized through modern media. However, a closer look at
[scriptural analysis](#scriptural-analysis-of-the-theory) and the original Greek
terminology suggests that these passages may instead describe a welcoming of
Christ after the Tribulation. While [theological
interpretations](#theological-defense-and-counter-arguments) vary, the emphasis
remains on being ready for the Lord's return.
