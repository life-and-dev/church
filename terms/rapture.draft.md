# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has fascinated and divided Christians for centuries.
While many modern believers view it as a central tenet of faith, others question
its biblical foundations and historical pedigree. This article explores where
the theory originated and examines the primary scriptures used to support and
challenge various interpretations of this event.

## Origin of the theory

John Nelson Darby began to develop his thinking on a pretribulational rapture
during a period of convalescence between December 1826 and January 1827. He had
suffered a horse accident in October 1826. During his recovery, he argued that
the Bible taught a secret return of Christ before the Tribulation. The
Tribulation is a predicted period of great suffering and global distress. Darby
convinced many other Christians of this view. His leadership and teachings
attracted a growing number of followers. The movement eventually spread from
Ireland to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular. He published a book in 1970 titled "The Late Great
Planet Earth" which stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be a
> secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him. It will
> be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready. But
> for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and sorrow.
> The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a time
> unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet
> Earth, Chapter 12

Hal Lindsey described a scenario where believers are unexpectedly removed from
the earth to meet Jesus in the air. This event would leave those remaining in a
state of shock and sorrow.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Evidence for the theory

### Gathering of the elect

The term "elect" refers to those whom God has chosen. One of the verses quoted
to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some scholars have discussed textual variants in the New Testament. However,
Matthew 24:31 is well-attested in major ancient manuscripts like Codex
Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus.

Regardless of its manuscript history, the context of the verse is important. The
angels gather the elect after the Tribulation and the return of the Son of Man.
This sequence challenges the Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture occurs
before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
> away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the mill;
> one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on
> what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of the house
> had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed
> awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must
> be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect. — Matthew
> 24:35-44 (ESV)

The "Rapture" is compared to the days of Noah. Jesus repeatedly emphasized that
the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It will be like the flood in
the days of Noah.

Some people believe the phrase "one will be taken and one left" refers to an
unexpected teleportation of believers from Earth. They combine this with the
unexpected nature of the event.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is παραλαμβάνεται
([paralambanetai](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm)). It is a combination of
[pará](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close or alongside,
and [lambánō](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive, admit,
or acknowledge. It can be explained as being accepted into a position.

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> “Joseph, son of David, do not fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife...” —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is ἀφίεται
([aphietai](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm)). It means to send away or leave
alone. This implies being rejected or left unforgiven.

The same word is also used in this scripture:

> “For if you forgive others their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also
> forgive you, but if you ***do not forgive*** others their trespasses, neither
> will your Father forgive your trespasses.” — Matthew 6:14-15 (ESV)

It is likely that Jesus meant two persons might appear equal from a human
perspective. However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other will be
rejected.

This theme of being ready for an unexpected return is confirmed by the three
parables that immediately follow this passage.

### Faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
> whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
> set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
> ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
> drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
> does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
> and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
> gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not
> be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But he
> answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for you
> know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### Parable of the talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five
> talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who had the
> two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two talents;
> here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well done, good
> and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you
> over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had received the
> one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping
> where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was
> afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is
> yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You
> knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed?
> Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I
> should have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from
> him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will
> more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not,
> even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant into the
> outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. —
> Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Defense against counter-arguments

Critics often argue that being "taken" in Matthew 24:40-41 must refer to the
Rapture because being "left" would imply staying on Earth during the
Tribulation. However, the context of the days of Noah suggests that those who
were "swept away" by the flood were the ones taken in judgment. Noah and his
family were "left" to inherit the earth. Therefore, being "taken" can be
interpreted as removal in judgment rather than a rescue.

Another critique focuses on 1 Thessalonians 4:17 (ESV), where believers are
"caught up" in the clouds. While this describes a meeting with the Lord in the
air, the text does not explicitly state that this occurs seven years before the
final return. Many scholars suggest this meeting is a royal welcome. In this
view, the subjects meet the returning King to escort Him back to Earth.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has evolved from its
[19th-century origins](#origin-of-the-theory) into a widely held belief today.
However, a close examination of the
[biblical evidence](#evidence-for-the-theory), including the
[gathering of the elect](#gathering-of-the-elect) and the
[analogy of Noah](#like-the-days-of-noah), suggests a different sequence of
events. The parables of the [faithful servant](#faithful-and-wise-servant), the
[ten virgins](#ten-virgins), and the [talents](#parable-of-the-talents) all
emphasize the necessity of being ready for an unexpected return and the
subsequent judgment or acceptance.
