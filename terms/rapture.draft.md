# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has fascinated and divided Christians for nearly two
centuries. While some see it as a central hope of the faith, others question
its biblical and historical foundations. This article examines where the theory
came from and how it interprets key passages of scripture.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. Lindsey was an
American author and Christian theologian who published "The Late Great Planet
Earth" in 1970. The book stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This suggests an interpretation where Christians are unexpectedly teleported to
a location in the air to meet Jesus, while shock and sorrow fall upon those
remaining.

In addition, some pretribulationists interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 as suggesting two separate
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pretribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish a reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One verse frequently cited in discussions about the return of Christ is Matthew
24:31 (ESV):

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
> Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

While some debate the textual history of certain phrases in this chapter,
Matthew 24:31 is well-attested in major early manuscripts such as Codex
Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. Even if the authenticity is accepted, the text
indicates that the angels gather the elect after the Tribulation and the return
of the "Son of Man." This sequence contradicts the pretribulational theory that
the rapture occurs before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
> away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Therefore, stay awake, for you
> do not know on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master
> of the house had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he
> would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken into.
> Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you
> do not expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison of the Rapture to the "days of Noah" focuses on the unexpected
nature of the event. Jesus did not state that Noah escaped the flood; rather,
he emphasized that the "coming of the Son of Man" would be as unexpected as the
flood was for those who were swept away.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This word combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning alongside, and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to take or receive.
Lexicographers often define this as receiving someone into association or
[associating with oneself](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This suggests
being "accepted" or "received."

The same word appears in other contexts:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear to take Mary as your wife.” — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

In contrast, the Greek word for "swept away" in verse 39 is
[*airō*](https://biblehub.com/greek/142.htm), which means to lift up or carry
away. Jesus uses a different word, *paralambanō*, in verses 40-41 to describe
those who are taken.

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away, leave
alone, or permit. It often carries the connotation of rejection or being left
unforgiven. For example, the same word is used in Matthew 6:15 (ESV):

> But if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

The context suggests that while two people may appear similar from a human
perspective, one will be accepted while the other is rejected. This theme of
being prepared for the Lord's unexpected return to be received as a steward of
his kingdom is further illustrated by the three parables that follow this
passage.

## Defense

Some critics argue that being "taken" refers to judgment, citing the parallel
in Luke 17:37 where the disciples ask "Where, Lord?" and Jesus responds with an
image of vultures gathering around a corpse. In this view, those "taken" are
taken away to judgment, similar to how the flood "swept away" (*airō*) the
people in the days of Noah.

However, the use of *paralambanō* elsewhere in the New Testament frequently
has a positive connotation of receiving or welcoming. For example, in John 14:3
(ESV), Jesus uses the same verb when he says, "I will come again and will take
you to myself." Furthermore, the parables of the faithful servant, the ten
virgins, and the talents all emphasize the reward of those who are found ready
and the rejection of those who are not. In these stories, the ones who are
"taken" in or "enter" the joy of the master are the faithful, while the ones
"left" outside are the ones who face judgment.

### The faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
> whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
> set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
> ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
> drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
> does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
> and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
> gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise. For
> when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the wise
> took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they all
> became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will
> not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But
> he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for
> you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to
> another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who had
> received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made five
> talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more. But he
> who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me
> five talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him,
> ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
> I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who
> had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two
> talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had
> received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a
> hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no
> seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you
> have what is yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful
> servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I
> scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers,
> and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take
> the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone
> who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who
> has not, even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant
> into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of
> teeth. — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory is a relatively modern development in Christian history,
popularized by [John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) and authors like
[Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory). While proponents cite verses like
Matthew 24:31 to support a pretribulational event, the context often places
these events [after the Tribulation](#the-gathering-of-the-elect). Furthermore,
linguistic studies of terms like "taken" and "left" suggest a focus on
[readiness and divine acceptance](#like-the-days-of-noah) rather than a secret
disappearance. These themes are reinforced by the subsequent parables, which
emphasize being [found faithful](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) and prepared
for the [master's return](#the-ten-virgins). Despite [competing
interpretations](#defense), the core message remains one of vigilance and
stewardship.
