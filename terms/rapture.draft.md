# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has captured the imagination of many, often depicted
as a sudden disappearance of believers from the Earth. While widely popularized
in modern media and certain theological circles, its historical roots and
biblical basis invite a closer examination. This article explores where the
theory originated and how original Greek texts shape the understanding of being
"taken" or "left" in the context of Christ's return.

## Origins of the rapture theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture, and he convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers, and the movement spread
to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Lindsey's interpretation suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly removed
to a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, those remaining on Earth
would experience shock and sorrow.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV) to suggest two separate
comings of Christ. They view the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish his reign on Earth.

## Biblical examination of the theory

## The gathering of the elect

One of the verses often cited to prove the Rapture is Matthew 24:31:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some suggest that verse 31 might be a later addition to the text. However,
textual scholarship confirms its presence in major early manuscripts such as
[Codex Sinaiticus](https://codexsinaiticus.org/en/manuscript.aspx?book=33&chapter=24&lid=en&side=r&verse=31)
and [Codex Vaticanus](https://digi.vatlib.it/view/MSS_Vat.gr.1209/p/1266).

Regardless of its textual history, the angels gather the elect after the
Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man". This sequence challenges the
Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture occurs before the Tribulation.

## Like the days of Noah

> “Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. 36 But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. 37 For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. 38 For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, 39 and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them
> all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. 40 Then two men will be in
> the field; one will be taken and one left. 41 Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be taken and one left. 42 Therefore, stay awake, for you do
> not know on what day your Lord is coming. 43 But know this, that if the master
> of the house had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he
> would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken into. 44
> Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you
> do not expect.” — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison of the Rapture to "the days of Noah" is significant. Jesus did
not state that Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he repeatedly emphasized that
the "coming of the Son of Man" will be as unexpected as the flood was for those
in Noah's time.

Jesus repeatedly stated this event will occur unexpectedly. Combined with the
words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some interpret this to mean
believers will be removed from the Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It is a
combination of ["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close
or alongside, and ["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to
receive or take. This term carries the sense of receiving someone near or taking
them to oneself.

For example, the same word is used in these scriptures:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is
[ἀφίεται (aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word means to
send away or leave alone. It can imply a sense of rejection or being left
unforgiven.

For example the same word was also used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus most likely meant that from a human perspective two persons would be
considered equal. However, one will be accepted while the other is rejected.

This theme of being ready at any time for an unexpected return is also supported
by subsequent parables. These stories emphasize the importance of being accepted
as stewards of the kingdom.

### The faithful and wise servant

> servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes. 47 Truly, I say to

### The Ten Virgins

> us.’ 12 But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ 13 Watch
> therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour.” — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The theory of a pre-tribulation Rapture originated in the 19th century and
gained widespread popularity through modern literature. [Historical
evidence](#origins-of-the-rapture-theory) shows its development by figures like
John Nelson Darby and Hal Lindsey. However, [biblical
examination](#biblical-examination-of-the-theory) indicates that the gathering
of the elect occurs after the Tribulation. Furthermore, the [Greek
terminology](#like-the-days-of-noah) used by Jesus suggests that being "taken"
or "left" refers to acceptance or rejection upon his return, rather than a
physical removal from the Earth. [Subsequent
parables](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) reinforce this theme of readiness and
stewardship.
