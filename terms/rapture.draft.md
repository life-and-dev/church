# The Rapture Theory

The concept of the Rapture has fascinated and divided Christians for centuries.
Many believers anticipate a sudden event where they will be caught up to meet
Christ. This article explores the history of this doctrine and examines its
biblical foundations to understand its place in modern theology.

## Origins of the Doctrine

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view during his recovery
from a horse accident. Darby's leadership and teachings attracted a growing
number of followers. The movement spread to England and other parts of the
world.

## Modern Popularization

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. His book
published in 1970, titled "The Late Great Planet Earth", stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be a
secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him. It will
be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready. But for
those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and sorrow. The
Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a time unknown to
anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth,
Chapter 12

Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a meeting
place in the air with Jesus. This event would bring shock and sorrow to those
remaining on Earth.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17; 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Examining Scriptural Proofs

The gathering of the elect is often cited as proof of the Rapture.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the
other. — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some arguments suggest this verse was a later addition. However, major ancient
manuscripts like Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus include this verse. It is
widely accepted as authentic by scholars.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the text indicates the angels gather the
elect after the Tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This challenges
the theory that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

## The Context of Noah's Days

Jesus compared his coming to the days of Noah.

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the mill;
one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on
what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of the house
had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed
awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must
be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect. —
Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus emphasized that the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. He did
not focus on Noah's escape from the flood. Instead, he highlighted the sudden
nature of the event.

Some interpret the phrases "one will be taken and one left" as an unexpected
teleportation of believers from Earth. However, the original Greek words
provide further context.

## Linguistic Insights into Being Taken

The Greek word translated as "taken" is
[paralambanetai](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This is a form of
[paralambano](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It combines
[para](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) (alongside) and
[lambano](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm) (to take or receive). It often
implies receiving someone into a close relationship or position.

For example, the same word appears in these verses:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. —
Matthew 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The word translated as "left" is
[aphietai](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This is a form of
[aphiemi](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away, leave
alone, or forgive. In the context of judgment, it can imply being left behind
or rejected.

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

The passage may suggest that two people appear similar from a human
perspective. Yet one is accepted while the other is rejected. This aligns with
the theme of being prepared for the Lord's return.

The use of paralambano in a positive sense of receiving someone is further
supported by Jesus' promise to his disciples in the Gospel of John.

> And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again and will take you
to myself, that where I am you may be also. — John 14:3 (ESV)

In this context, the word "take" (paralempsomai) clearly refers to Jesus
receiving his followers into his presence.

## Defense of the Interpretation

Critics often argue that the context of Noah's days suggests that being "taken"
is a negative event of judgment. They point out that those "swept away" by the
flood were the ones judged. However, Jesus used a different Greek word for the
flood sweeping people away: airo. This word implies a forceful removal or
carrying away in judgment.

In contrast, he used paralambano for the men in the field and the women at the
mill. This choice of words suggests a distinction between the destruction of the
wicked and the receiving of the faithful. The focus remains on the suddenness of
the event and the necessity of being ready for the Master's return.

## Parables of Readiness

Three parables in Matthew follow this passage to reinforce the theme of
readiness.

### The Faithful and Wise Servant

Jesus described a servant who remains faithful while the master is away.

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

The parable of the ten virgins highlights the importance of being prepared for
the bridegroom's arrival.

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise. For
when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the wise
took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they all
became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will
not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, ‘Lord, lord, open to us.’
But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore,
for you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

The parable of the talents emphasizes being productive with what the Master has
entrusted to his servants.

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to
another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who had
received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made five
talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more. But he
who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me
five talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him,
‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who
had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two
talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had
received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a
hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no
seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you
have what is yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful
servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I
scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers,
and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take
the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone
who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who
has not, even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant
into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of
teeth. — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The [Rapture theory](#origins-of-the-doctrine) has its roots in 19th-century
interpretations and gained [widespread popularity](#modern-popularization) in
the 20th century. While it relies on specific understandings of being
[taken](#linguistic-insights-into-being-taken) or
[gathered](#examining-scriptural-proofs), others point to the [context of
Noah's days](#the-context-of-noahs-days) and [parables of
readiness](#parables-of-readiness) to suggest a focus on moral preparedness
rather than a secret escape. A [linguistic
defense](#defense-of-the-interpretation) of these terms supports the view that
the event centers on acceptance or rejection by the Lord. The debate highlights
the different ways believers [interpret scriptural
proofs](#examining-scriptural-proofs) regarding the return of Christ.
