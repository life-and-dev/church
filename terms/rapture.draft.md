# Rapture

Many Christians today anticipate a sudden, secret event where believers are
whisked away from Earth before a time of great trouble. This concept, known as
the rapture, has become a cornerstone of modern prophecy teaching. But where did
this idea originate, and does it accurately reflect the biblical text? Exploring
the historical development and the original Greek meaning of key passages
reveals a perspective that might challenge common assumptions about the end of
the age.

## Origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His
> church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be a secret,
> invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him. It will be a
> moment of
> unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready. But for those who are
> not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and sorrow. The Bible calls
> this
> event the 'rapture' which will take place at a time unknown to anyone except
> God the
> Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12

This teaching suggests that Christians would be unexpectedly "teleported" to a
secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow would then fall upon
those who were left behind.

In addition, some pretribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pretribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Examining the evidence

### Gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from
> the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

The author previously suggested that this verse was a later addition. However,
textual scholarship confirms its presence in major early manuscripts like Codex
Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. It is widely accepted as an authentic part of
the Gospel of Matthew.

Even as an authentic scripture, the text indicates that the angels gather the
elect after the tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This sequence
appears to contradict the pretribulationist theory that the rapture occurs
before the tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and
> hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor the Son, but the Father
> only. For
> as were the days of Noah, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For as in
> those days
> before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in
> marriage, until
> the day when Noah entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came
> and
> swept them all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men
> will be
> in the field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at
> the mill;
> one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on
> what
> day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of the house had
> known in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have
> let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of
> Man
> is coming at an hour you do not expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The "coming of the Son of Man" is compared to the days of Noah. This comparison
emphasizes the unexpected nature of the event. Just as the flood surprised those
who were unaware, the return of Christ will occur at an hour people do not
expect.

Some interpret the words "one will be taken and one left" to mean a sudden
disappearance from Earth. However, the original Greek words provide a different
nuance. The word translated as "taken" is *paralambano*. It means "to receive
near" or "to take to oneself." It is used in Matthew 1:20 and Matthew 1:24 when
Joseph took Mary as his wife. This suggests being accepted or received into a
close relationship.

The Greek word translated as "left" is *aphiemi*. It means "to send away" or
"to leave alone." It is often used in the context of rejection or abandonment.
For example, it appears in Matthew 6:15 regarding the failure to forgive.

This suggests that two people might appear equal from a human perspective. One
is accepted like a bride, while the other is rejected. This theme of readiness
for an unexpected return is further explored in the parables that follow.

## Conclusion

The pretribulational rapture theory is a relatively modern development in
Christian theology, primarily rooted in the 19th-century teachings of [John
Nelson Darby](#origin-of-the-theory). While popularized by modern authors like
[Hal Lindsey](#origin-of-the-theory), the biblical evidence often points to a
single, visible return of Christ rather than a [secret
event](#examining-the-evidence). Passages such as [Matthew
24:31](#gathering-of-the-elect) place the gathering of the elect after the
tribulation. Furthermore, the Greek terminology in [Matthew
24:40-41](#days-of-noah) emphasizes being accepted or rejected in judgment
rather than a physical removal from Earth. Ultimately, the parables of the
[faithful servant](#faithful-and-wise-servant), the [ten virgins](#ten-virgins),
and the [talents](#talents) highlight the importance of spiritual readiness for
Christ's certain but unexpected return.
