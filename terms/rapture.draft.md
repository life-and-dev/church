# The Rapture Theory

The concept of a secret, sudden disappearance of believers from the Earth has
fascinated and divided Christians for generations. This event is commonly known
as the Rapture. Many people wonder whether this doctrine is a foundational
biblical truth or a relatively modern theological development. By exploring its
history and examining the scriptures often used to support it, one can gain a
clearer understanding of what the Bible actually teaches about the return of
Jesus Christ.

## The Origin of the Theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He eventually convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's
leadership and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement
subsequently spread to England and other parts of the world. His development of
these views was notably influenced by a period of convalescence in early 1827.
This followed a horse accident in October 1826.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth." In Chapter 12, titled "The Ultimate Trip," he
stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

This perspective suggests that Christians would be unexpectedly "teleported" to
a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow would fall
upon those left behind.

In addition, pretribulationists often interpret biblical passages like
1 Thessalonians 4:13-17; 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV) to suggest different
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pretribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish His reign on Earth.

## Analysis of the Theory

### The Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses quoted to support the Rapture is Matthew 24:31 (ESV):

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some sources have suggested that verse 31 was added to the scripture or is
missing from some texts. However, major uncials like Sinaiticus and Vaticanus
include this verse. Claims that it is missing or a later addition are not
supported by primary textual evidence.

Regardless of its textual history, the context of the verse is significant. The
angels gather the elect **after** the Tribulation and the return of the "Son of
Man." This sequence challenges the pretribulationist theory that the rapture
occurs before the Tribulation.

### Like the Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44
> (ESV)

The comparison to the "days of Noah" highlights the unexpected nature of the
event. It does not necessarily imply that Noah escaped the flood in a way that
mirrors a secret rapture. Instead, it emphasizes that the "coming of the Son of
Man" will occur without warning, much like the flood caught the people of
Noah's day off guard.

The Greek word translated as "taken" is *paralambanetai*. It is the singular
passive form of *paralambano*. This word combines *para* (alongside) and
*lambano* (to take or receive). It can imply being received or accepted. For
instance, the same root is used in Matthew 1:20,24 (ESV). There, Joseph is told
"to take" Mary as his wife and subsequently "took" her.

In contrast, the word translated as "left" is *aphietai*. It is the singular
passive form of *aphiemi*. This word means to send away or leave alone. In
theological contexts, it can refer to rejection or being left unforgiven. This
is seen in Matthew 6:15 (ESV) regarding forgiveness.

The text suggests that two people might appear similar from a human perspective.
However, one will be **accepted** like a bride, while the other will be
**rejected**. This theme of being ready for an unexpected return is also present
in several parables.

## Theological Defense and Perspectives

While the pretribulational rapture is a popular view, many theologians point to
alternative interpretations. For instance, the Greek word *apantesin* in
1 Thessalonians 4:17 refers to a formal delegation meeting a visiting
dignitary. In the Greco-Roman world, such a group would meet the official to
escort them into the city. This supports a view where believers meet the
returning King to escort Him to Earth.

Critiques often argue that the "secret" nature of the rapture contradicts the
"loud trumpet call" mentioned in both Matthew 24:31; 1 Thessalonians 4:16
(ESV). Furthermore, the use of *airo* (to sweep away) in Matthew 24:39 (ESV)
describes those taken in judgment during the flood. This contrast with
*paralambano* suggests that being "taken" in verses 40-41 refers to being
received by Christ rather than being taken in judgment.

Some proponents of pretribulationism argue that the warnings in Matthew 24 are
directed specifically at the Jewish people and not the Church. They suggest
that the Church will have already been removed before these events. However,
defenders of a post-tribulational view point out that the term "elect" is
frequently used throughout the New Testament to describe all believers in
Christ, regardless of their background (Colossians 3:12 (ESV)). This suggests
that the exhortation to remain watchful and faithful is applicable to the
entire body of believers.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has roots in the 19th-century teachings of [John Nelson
Darby](rapture.draft.md#the-origin-of-the-theory). It was further popularized
in the 20th century by [Hal Lindsey](rapture.draft.md#the-origin-of-the-theory).
Analysis of scriptures like [Matthew 24:31](rapture.draft.md#the-gathering-of-the-elect)
and the [days of Noah](rapture.draft.md#like-the-days-of-noah) suggests that the
focus is on being ready for an unexpected return. The use of specific Greek
terms like [*paralambanetai* and
*aphietai*](rapture.draft.md#like-the-days-of-noah) points toward a message of
acceptance or rejection. Ultimately, parables such as the [Faithful
Servant](rapture.draft.md#the-faithful-and-wise-servant), the [Ten
Virgins](rapture.draft.md#the-ten-virgins), and the
[Talents](rapture.draft.md#the-talents) emphasize the importance of
faithfulness and readiness for the return of the Son of Man.
