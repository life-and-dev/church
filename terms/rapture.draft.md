# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture remains one of the most discussed and debated topics
in modern eschatology. While many believers consider it a foundational truth,
its historical development and scriptural foundations reveal a more complex
story. This article explores where this theory originated and how specific
biblical passages are interpreted by different theological schools.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

It seems he thought that Christians would be unexpectedly "teleported" to a
secret place somewhere in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow would then be
upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some pretribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pretribulation event to gather
believers. This would be followed by a later, visible second coming to establish
his reign on Earth. Critiques of this view argue that these passages describe a
single, public event rather than two separate comings.

## Evidence for the Rapture Theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that this verse is present in major early
manuscripts like [Codex
Sinaiticus](https://codexsinaiticus.org/en/manuscript.aspx?book=33&chapter=24&lid=en&side=r&verse=31)
and Codex Vaticanus. While some later manuscripts like Codex Bezae contain
additional phrases, the core verse is widely recognized as part of the original
canon rather than a later addition.

Even with its authenticity established, the context shows that the angels gather
the elect after the tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This sequence
challenges the pretribulational theory which places the gathering before the
tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be taken and one left.
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be taken and one left.
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is the Rapture like "the days of Noah"?

Jesus did not say that Noah "escaped the flood". He repeatedly echoed that the
coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected like the flood in the days of Noah.

Because Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly in combination with the
words "one will be taken and one left", some people believe this means that the
elect would unexpectedly be teleported from Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is paralambanetai. This word is a
combination of para, which means from alongside, and lambano, which means to
receive or take. It carries the sense of receiving someone near or taking them
to oneself.

For example, the same word appears in these scriptures:

> Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which is
> conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is aphietai. This word means to
send away or leave alone. In certain contexts, it can imply rejection or being
left in an unforgiven state.

For example, the same word is used in this scripture:

> But if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus more likely meant that from a human perspective two persons would be
considered equal. One will be accepted like a bride while the other will be
rejected.

This theme, of being ready at any time for the Lord's unexpected return to be
accepted as stewards of his kingdom, is also confirmed by the 3 parables that
immediately follow this passage:

## Conclusion

The theory of a secret pretribulational rapture is a relatively recent
development in Christian eschatology. It was first formalized by [John Nelson
Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) in the 19th century and later popularized by
[Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory) in the 20th century. Scriptural
analysis reveals that key passages used to support this view may have
alternative interpretations. The gathering of the elect in [Matthew
24:31](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) occurs after the tribulation. Furthermore,
the linguistic analysis of the Greek words [paralambano and
aphiemi](#like-the-days-of-noah) suggests that being "taken" and "left" refers
to acceptance and rejection at the Lord's return. This theme is consistent with
the [subsequent parables](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) emphasizing readiness
and faithful stewardship rather than a secret escape.
