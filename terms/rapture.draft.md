# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture is a widely discussed topic in Christian eschatology.
It involves different interpretations of how and when believers will meet Jesus
Christ. This article examines the historical development of the theory and the
biblical passages often used as evidence.

## Theory origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He is an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth." The book stated the following information.

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This perspective suggests that Christians will be unexpectedly "teleported" to a
secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow will fall upon those
left behind.

Some pre-tribulationists interpret certain biblical passages to suggest two
separate comings of Christ. These passages include 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1
Corinthians 15:51-52. They view the Rapture as a secret event to gather
believers before the Tribulation. They believe a visible second coming will
happen later to establish His reign on Earth. Critiques of this view often point
out that these scriptures do not explicitly describe a secret return. They argue
that the gathering of believers happens at the same time as the visible second
coming.

## Elect gathering

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is Matthew 24:31.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some argue that this verse is a later addition. However, textual scholarship
confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major ancient manuscripts such as
Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. Codex Bezae contains additional phrases,
but the core verse is established in the biblical canon.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the context shows that the angels gather the
elect after the Tribulation. This occurs during the return of the Son of Man.
This sequence challenges the theory that the Rapture happens before the
Tribulation.

## Noah comparison

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44
> (ESV)

The comparison to the days of Noah is significant. Noah did not escape the flood
in the sense of being removed from the earth. Instead, he was protected through
it. Jesus emphasized that the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It
will be similar to the suddenness of the flood.

Some people believe that the phrase "one will be taken and one left" indicates
that the elect will be teleported from Earth. However, the original Greek words
provide further insight. The word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This word is a
combination of [pará](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning "from close
or alongside," and [lambanō](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning "to
receive, admit, or acknowledge." It can be understood as being "accepted into a
position" or "received near."

This same Greek word appears in other scriptures.

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear to take Mary as your wife." — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. This can imply rejection or being left unforgiven.

The same word is used in Matthew 6:15 (ESV).

> But if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

These word choices suggest that two people might appear equal from a human
perspective. However, one is accepted while the other is rejected.

This theme, of being ready at any time for the Lord's unexpected return to be
accepted as stewards of his kingdom, is also confirmed by the 3 parables that
immediately follow this passage:

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory is a relatively modern development in Christian thought. It
emerged in the 19th century and gained widespread popularity in the 20th century
([Theory origins](#theory-origins)). Biblical analysis suggests that the
gathering of the elect follows the period of Tribulation ([Elect
gathering](#elect-gathering)). Interpretations of people being "taken" or "left"
focus on spiritual readiness and divine acceptance rather than physical removal
from Earth ([Noah comparison](#noah-comparison)). The accompanying parables
emphasize the importance of being faithful and prepared for the unexpected
return of the Lord ([Faithful servant](#faithful-servant), [Ten
virgins](#ten-virgins), [Talent stewardship](#talent-stewardship)).
