# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has become a central theme in modern Christian
eschatology. It is often depicted as a sudden, secret event where believers are
whisked away from the Earth. This article examines the historical origins of
this theory and its popularization in the 20th century. It also explores the
biblical passages frequently cited in its support. By examining the original
Greek terminology and the surrounding parables, we can gain a deeper
understanding of the message Jesus intended to convey about being ready for His
return.

## Historical Origin

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement eventually
spread to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, American author Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology
popular.
He published a book in 1970 titled "The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

It seems that Lindsey thought Christians would be unexpectedly teleported to a
secret place in the air to meet Jesus. He suggested that shock and sorrow would
fall upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish His reign on Earth. However, the Greek word for "meeting" in 1
Thessalonians 4:17 is *apantesin* (Strong's 529), which was commonly used to
describe a formal delegation meeting a visiting dignitary to escort them into a
city. This suggests an event where believers meet the returning King to escort
Him to Earth, rather than being whisked away to a secret location.

## Scriptural Analysis

### Gathering the Elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
> Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some people claim verse 31 was added to the scripture and is missing from some
texts. While there are minor textual variants in the New Testament, Matthew
24:31 is well-attested in the most ancient and reliable manuscripts. These
include Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the angels gather the elect **after** the
Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man". This contradicts the theory
that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only. **For as were the days of Noah**, so will
> be the coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they
> were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day
> when Noah entered the ark, and **they were unaware** until the flood came
> and swept them all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two
> men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Therefore, stay awake, for
> **you do not know** on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if
> the master of the house **had known** in what part of the night the thief
> was coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be
> broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming
> at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is the return of Christ compared to "the days of Noah"? Noah did not escape
the flood. Instead, Jesus repeatedly echoed that the "coming of the Son of Man"
will be unexpected like the flood.

Because Jesus repeatedly says this event will occur unexpectedly, some people
believe the phrase "one will be taken and one left" means believers will be
teleported from Earth. The original Greek word translated as "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It
combines ["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close or
alongside, and ["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to
receive or acknowledge. It can be explained as being accepted into a position.

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which
> is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife, — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This means to send away or
leave alone. It implies being rejected or remaining unforgiven. The same root
word was also used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus likely meant that two people might appear equal from a human perspective.
However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other is rejected.
Critics of this interpretation often point to the parallel with the flood.
They suggest that being "taken" refers to being swept away in judgment.
However, in Matthew 24:39, the flood "swept away" the people using the Greek
word *airo* (Strong's 142). In contrast, verses 40-41 use *paralambano* for
being "taken," which is consistently used for receiving someone into a close
relationship or position. In this context, those "left" are described with
*aphietai*, which can imply being sent away or rejected.

This theme of being ready for the Lord's unexpected return is confirmed by the
three parables that immediately follow this passage.

### Faithful Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
> Truly, I say to you, he will set him over all his possessions.
>
> * But if that wicked servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and
> begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
> master of that servant will come on a day when he does not expect him and at
> an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces and put him with the
> hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. —
> Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will
> not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and
> **those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the door
> was shut. Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to
> us.’ But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch
> therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13
> (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me
> five talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him,
> ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
> I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also
> who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me
> two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him,
> ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
> I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who
> had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a
> hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no
> seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here,
> you have what is yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and
> slothful servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where
> I scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the
> bankers, and at my coming I should have received what was my own with
> interest. So take the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten
> talents. For to everyone who has will more be given, and he will have an
> abundance. But from the one who has not, even what he has will be taken away.
> And cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness. In that place there
> will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.’ — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The theory of a pre-tribulation Rapture originated in the [19th
century](#historical-origin). It was popularized in modern times through
influential literature. However, biblical evidence such as the [gathering of
the elect](#gathering-the-elect) suggests this event occurs after the
Tribulation. Furthermore, the context of being [taken or
left](#days-of-noah) indicates a selection based on readiness and
stewardship. The parables of the [faithful
servant](#faithful-servant), the [ten virgins](#ten-virgins),
and the [talents](#the-talents) emphasize the necessity of being prepared for
the Lord's unexpected return.
