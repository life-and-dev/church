# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has captured the imagination of millions, often
depicted in popular culture as a sudden, mysterious disappearance of people
across the globe. This event is frequently associated with the end of the world
and the return of Jesus Christ. But where did this idea originate, and what does
the biblical text actually say about being taken or left behind? This article
examines the development of Rapture theology and explores the linguistic and
contextual clues found in the scriptures to understand the intended message of
these prophetic warnings.

## Origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
*The Late Great Planet Earth*. It stated the following:
>
> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, *The Late Great
> Planet Earth*, Chapter 12

Hal Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly taken to a secret
place in the air to meet Jesus. He believed that those left behind would
experience shock and sorrow.

Pre-tribulationists often interpret certain biblical passages to suggest two
separate comings of Christ. These passages include 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 (ESV)
and 1 Corinthians 15:51,52 (ESV). They view the Rapture as a secret event to
gather believers before the tribulation. This is followed by a later visible
second coming to establish a reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### Gathering of the elect

One verse used to support the Rapture theory describes the gathering of the
elect.
>
> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 (ESV) is present in major early
manuscripts. These include Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. The verse is
not a later addition to the canon.

The text indicates that the angels gather the elect after the tribulation. This
gathering follows the return of the Son of Man. This sequence of events
contrasts with the pretribulational theory that the Rapture occurs before the
tribulation.

### Days of Noah
>
> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> For as were the days of Noah, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For as
> in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and they were
> unaware until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming of
> the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be taken and one left.
>
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be taken and one left.
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on what day your Lord is coming.
> But know this, that if the master of the house had known in what part of the
> night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let
> his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man
> is coming at an hour you do not expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison to the days of Noah focuses on the unexpected nature of the
flood. Jesus emphasized that his coming would occur at an hour when people do
not expect him.

The words "one will be taken and one left" are often used to suggest a secret
removal of believers. However, the Greek word for "taken" is *paralambanetai*
(Strong's 3880). It is a combination of *para* (alongside) and *lambano* (to
take or receive). This term implies receiving someone near or taking them into a
close relationship. Jesus used the same word when promising to receive his
disciples to himself.
>
> And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again and will take you
> to myself, that where I am you may be also. — John 14:3 (ESV)

The same word appears in the account of Joseph and Mary.
>
> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.” —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The Greek word translated as "left" is *aphietai* (Strong's 863). It means to
send away or leave alone. This word is also used in the context of forgiveness.
>
> But if you do not forgive others, then your Father will not forgive your
> transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus used the term *aphiemi* in Matthew 6:15 (ESV) regarding forgiveness. This
supports its interpretation in Matthew 24:41 (ESV) as "to send away" or "leave
alone". Those who are left are rejected or unforgiven. They are not simply left
behind in a physical sense.

From a human perspective, two people might appear the same. However, one is
accepted like a bride while the other is rejected. This theme of readiness is
confirmed by the three parables that immediately follow this passage. They
emphasize being prepared for the unexpected return of the Lord to be accepted as
stewards of his kingdom.

### Faithful and wise servant
>
> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
> whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
> set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
> ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
> drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
> does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
> and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
> gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### Ten Virgins
>
> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not
> be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But he
> answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for you
> know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### Talents
>
> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five
> talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who had the
> two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two talents;
> here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well done, good
> and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you
> over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had received the
> one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping
> where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was
> afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is
> yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You
> knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed?
> Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I
> should have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from
> him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will
> more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not,
> even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant into the
> outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. —
> Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has developed over time from the teachings of [John Nelson
Darby](#origin-of-the-theory) and was later popularized by [Hal
Lindsey](#origin-of-the-theory). While some interpret specific verses as proof
of a secret event, the context of [Matthew 24:31](#gathering-of-the-elect)
suggests a gathering after the tribulation. The warnings about the [days of
Noah](#days-of-noah) and the subsequent parables like the [Faithful and Wise
Servant](#faithful-and-wise-servant), the [Ten Virgins](#ten-virgins), and the
[Talents](#talents) emphasize the need for readiness and faithful service rather
than a secret escape. The linguistic analysis of [paralambano and
aphiemi](#days-of-noah) further supports the idea of being received or rejected
based on one's state of preparation. A [defense against critical
arguments](#potential-counter-arguments) clarifies that readiness remains
essential despite the perceived timing of events.

### Potential counter-arguments

Critics of the post-tribulational view often point to the imminent nature of
Christ's return described in the New Testament. They argue that if specific
events like the tribulation must occur first, then the return of Christ cannot
be "at any moment." However, the parables of the faithful servant and the ten
virgins suggest that while the return is unexpected, there may be a perceived
delay. The requirement for watchfulness applies regardless of the specific
timing. It focuses on the spiritual state of the believer rather than a
chronological countdown.
