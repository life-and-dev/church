# The Evolution of the Rapture Theory

The concept of the Rapture has become a cornerstone of modern evangelical
eschatology. However, its origins and scriptural foundations are often debated.
This article explores how the theory developed. It examines the key biblical
passages used to support it. It also offers a deeper look at the original Greek
context.

## The Origin of the Theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world. His development of these views was notably
influenced by a period of convalescence following a serious injury in 1827.

## Popularization in the 20th Century

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He is an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth." In the chapter titled "The Ultimate Trip," he
stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be a
> secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him. It will
> be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready. But
> for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and sorrow.
> The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a time
> unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet
> Earth, Chapter 12

Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly teleported to a secret
place. This place would be somewhere in the air to meet Jesus. He believed shock
and sorrow would fall upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical Arguments and Interpretations

The theory relies on specific interpretations of the Olivet Discourse and other
New Testament passages.

### The Gathering of the Elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
> Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

While some commentators have questioned various textual details, the verse is
firmly established in the major early manuscripts. These include Codex
Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the context shows that the angels gather
the elect after the Tribulation. This occurs at the return of the "Son of Man."
This challenges the Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture will happen
before the Tribulation.

### The Days of Noah and the Greek Context

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> For as were the days of Noah, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For as
> in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and they were
> unaware until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming of
> the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on what day your Lord is coming.
> But know this, that if the master of the house had known in what part of the
> night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let
> his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man
> is coming at an hour you do not expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus compared the "coming of the Son of Man" to the days of Noah because the
event will be unexpected. He did not focus on Noah escaping the flood. Instead,
he focused on the suddenness of the judgment.

Because Jesus says this event occurs unexpectedly, some believe this means the
elect would be teleported from Earth. They point to the words "one will be taken
and one left."

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It is a combination of
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm). "Pará" means from close or
alongside. "Lambánō" means to receive, admit, or acknowledge. It can be
explained as being received or accepted.

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20
> (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. This often implies rejection or being left to face consequences.

For example, the same word was also used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

This suggests that two persons would appear similar from a human perspective.
However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other will be rejected.

## Parables of Readiness

This theme of being ready for an unexpected return is confirmed by the parables
that immediately follow this passage.

### The Faithful and Wise Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
> whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
> set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
> ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
> drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
> does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
> and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
> gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

## Defense Against Common Critiques

Critics of the linguistic interpretation often argue that "taken" in the
context of the flood refers to being taken away in judgment. This is based on
Matthew 24:39. In that verse, the Greek word is *airō*. It means to sweep away.
However, Jesus switches to *paralambanō* in verses 40 and 41. This linguistic
shift suggests a distinction. Being swept away in judgment is different from
being received or taken along by the Lord. This supports the view that the
"taken" ones in verses 40 and 41 are being accepted. They are not being judged.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has evolved significantly from its 19th-century origins to
become a widely held belief today.

* The theory was first developed by John Nelson Darby in the 1830s following a
  period of intense study. ([Origin](#the-origin-of-the-theory))
* Hal Lindsey popularized the concept for a modern audience in the 1970s.
  ([Popularization](#popularization-in-the-20th-century))
* Biblical analysis of the Olivet Discourse suggests the gathering of the elect
  occurs after the tribulation period.
  ([Gathering](#the-gathering-of-the-elect))
* The original Greek context of "taken" and "left" emphasizes being received or
  rejected based on readiness. ([Greek
  Context](#the-days-of-noah-and-the-greek-context))
* The parables of Jesus emphasize the importance of spiritual preparation for
  an unexpected return. ([Readiness](#parables-of-readiness))
