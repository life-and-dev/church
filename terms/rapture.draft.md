# Rapture

The concept of the rapture has shaped modern Christian eschatology, yet its
historical development and biblical interpretation remain subjects of
significant debate. By examining the origins of pretribulational thought and the
specific linguistic context of the Gospels, one can better understand the
different perspectives on the return of Christ and the gathering of the elect.

## Theory origin

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture, and he convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers, and the movement spread
to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
>
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This interpretation suggests that followers of Christ will be unexpectedly
removed from the Earth to meet the Lord in the air. While these individuals
experience joy, those remaining on Earth face a period of distress.

Some proponents of this view interpret passages like 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 and
1 Corinthians 15:51-52 as indicating two distinct phases of Christ's return. The
first phase is seen as a secret gathering of believers before a period of
tribulation. The second phase is a visible return to establish a kingdom on
Earth.

## Scriptural analysis

### Gathering of the elect

One passage cited in discussions about the timing of this gathering is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some textual analyses suggest that variations exist for certain verses in this
chapter. However, major Greek uncials like Sinaiticus and Vaticanus include this
verse.

Even when considering this scripture as authentic, the text indicates that the
angels gather the elect after the period of tribulation. This sequence of events
contrasts with theories proposing a gathering prior to the tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Discussions regarding the "days of Noah" often center on the unexpected nature
of the flood. Jesus emphasized that the coming of the Son of Man would occur
when individuals are unaware. This comparison focuses on the suddenness of the
event rather than an escape from the flood.

Critics of this view sometimes argue that being "taken" refers to judgment,
citing how the flood "took" people away. However, the Greek text uses distinct
terms to differentiate these actions. In verse 39, the flood "swept away"
(*airo*) the people, a word associated with removal or destruction. In contrast,
verses 40-41 use *paralambano* for "taken," a term often used for receiving
someone into close fellowship or a personal relationship.

Some interpret the phrases "one will be taken" and "one left" as a physical
removal or teleportation. A linguistic review of the original Greek provides
another perspective. The word for "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This term combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from alongside, and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or
acknowledge. This word can describe being received or accepted into a position.

This usage appears in other passages:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The Greek word for "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word can mean to send away
or leave alone. This usage is found in other contexts relating to rejection or a
lack of forgiveness:

> But if you ***do not forgive*** others, then your Father will ***not
> forgive*** your transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

Under this analysis, the passage describes a situation where two people appear
similar from a human perspective. One is accepted, while the other is rejected
or sent away. This theme of being ready for an unexpected return is continued in
the following parables.

## Conclusion

Modern interpretations of a secret, pretribulational return find their
[historical roots in the 19th century](#theory-origin). While some point to the
gathering of the elect as proof, others note that this event is described as
occurring [after the tribulation](#gathering-of-the-elect). Linguistic analysis
of the Greek terms for "taken" and "left" suggests a theme of [acceptance and
rejection](#days-of-noah) rather than physical teleportation. This perspective
is further supported by parables emphasizing the necessity of [constant
readiness](#faithful-servant), [spiritual preparation](#ten-virgins), and
[faithful stewardship](#the-talents).
