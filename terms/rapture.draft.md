# Rapture

The Rapture is a widely discussed topic in modern Christianity, often depicted
in popular culture as a sudden disappearance of believers. This article explores
the history behind this theory and examines the biblical passages frequently
used to support or challenge it.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, [John Nelson Darby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Nelson_Darby)
argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational rapture. He convinced many other
Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and teachings attracted a growing
number of followers. The movement spread to England and other parts of the
world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular. He published a book in 1970 titled [*The Late
Great Planet Earth*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Late_Great_Planet_Earth)
that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
>
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12

Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a secret place
in the air to meet Jesus. This view claims that shock and sorrow will fall upon
those who remain on Earth.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major early
manuscripts such as Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. While some variations
exist in other ancient texts, the core verse is widely accepted as authentic in
modern biblical scholarship.

Either way, even if the scripture is authentic, the text indicates that the
angels gather the elect after the Tribulation. The gathering happens during the
return of the Son of Man. This sequence challenges the Pre-tribulationist theory
that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison of the coming of the Son of Man to the days of Noah emphasizes
the unexpected nature of the event. Jesus noted that people were unaware until
the flood came and swept them away.

Because Jesus said this event will occur unexpectedly, and used the words "one
will be taken and one left", some people believe this means that believers will
be removed from Earth.

A linguistic shift in the Greek text provides further clarity. The Greek word
used in Matthew 24:39 to describe the flood "sweeping away" the people is [αἴρω
(airo)](https://biblehub.com/greek/142.htm). In contrast, the word used in
verses 40-41 for those who are "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm).

The word *paralambanetai* is a combination of
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close or alongside,
and ["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or
acknowledge. This word can be explained as being received or taken to oneself.
It implies a welcoming action.

For example, the same word appears in these scriptures:

> Behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son
> of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which is conceived
> in her is from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" in verses 40-41 is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word means to send away or
leave alone. It can imply being rejected or remaining unforgiven.

For example, the same word is used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

The passage suggests a distinction between those who are swept away in judgment
and those who are received by Christ. From a human perspective, two people
appear equal. However, one is accepted while the other is rejected.

This theme of being ready for an unexpected return is confirmed by three
parables that follow this passage. A parable is a simple story used to
illustrate a moral or spiritual lesson.

### The faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
>   Truly, I say to you, he will **set him over all his possessions**.
>

>   begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
>   master of that servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him

>   with the hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of
>   teeth**.
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Talents

> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
> he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two
> talents more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the

>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled

>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
> more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made

> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been
> faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of

>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you

> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew

> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that

## Different Perspectives

The interpretation of these passages varies among different Christian groups.
Pre-tribulationists view being "taken" as a positive event where believers are
removed from Earth before a time of trouble. They point to the promise of being
gathered to meet the Lord in the air.

Other groups, such as Post-tribulationists or those with a different focus, see
the gathering as occurring at the very end of history. They may also emphasize
the ethical weight of the parables, focusing on the need for constant
faithfulness rather than the timing of the return.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory emerged significantly in the 19th century through the work of
[John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory). It was later brought into the
mainstream by authors like [Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory). While some
interpret specific biblical verses to support a [secret gathering of
believers](#the-gathering-of-the-elect), others point to the [timing of the
event](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) and [linguistic
evidence](#like-the-days-of-noah) to suggest a different meaning. The parables
that follow these passages emphasize the importance of [readiness and faithful
service](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) regardless of the specific timing or
nature of the return.
