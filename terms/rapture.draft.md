# Rapture

The concept of the rapture has become a central theme in many modern Christian
discussions about the end times. While often presented as a long-standing
tradition, its history and biblical interpretation are subjects of significant
debate among scholars and theologians. This article examines where the theory
came from and how specific biblical passages are used to support or challenge
the idea of a secret gathering of believers.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. This view suggests that Christians will be taken to heaven before a
period of great suffering on Earth. He convinced many other Christians of this
perspective. Darby's leadership and teachings attracted a growing number of
followers. The movement subsequently spread to England and other parts of the
world.

John Nelson Darby is also known for developing dispensationalism. This is a
theological system that divides history into different periods or dispensations.
In this system, God relates to humanity in different ways during each period.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the rapture theology popular. He was an
American author who published a book in 1970 titled "The Late Great Planet
Earth". The book stated the following:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

Hal Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly removed from the
Earth to meet Jesus in the air. This event would bring shock and sorrow to those
who remain.

In addition, some pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages to
suggest two separate comings of Christ. They use 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1
Corinthians 15:51-52 for this purpose. These authors see the rapture as a secret
event to gather believers before the tribulation. This would be followed by a
later visible second coming to establish a reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to support the rapture theory is found in the Gospel of
Matthew. It describes a gathering of the elect from across the heavens.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that this verse is present in major ancient
manuscripts. These include the Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. It is
widely accepted as an authentic part of the original text.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the timing of the event is significant. The
angels gather the elect after the tribulation and the return of the Son of Man.
This sequence appears to contradict the theory that the rapture occurs before
the tribulation.

## Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> > Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> > [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> > [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> >
> > Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> > [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> > [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is this event compared to the days of Noah?

Jesus does not say that it is because Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he
repeatedly emphasizes that the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It
will be like the flood that caught people by surprise.

Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly. He uses the words "one will be
taken and one left". Because of this, some people believe that the elect will be
unexpectedly removed from the Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is paralambanetai. This word is a
combination of "para" and "lambano". "Para" means from close or alongside.
"Lambano" means to receive or acknowledge. The term can be explained as being
received or accepted into a position.

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> ... behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph,
> son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife ... — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is aphietai. This word means to
send away or leave alone. It can imply being rejected or left unforgiven.

For example, the same word appears in another passage regarding forgiveness.

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus likely meant that two people might appear equal from a human perspective.
One will be accepted like a bride. The other will be rejected.

Some critics argue that being "taken" in this context refers to being taken away
in judgment. This would be similar to how the flood swept away the people in the
days of Noah. However, the use of paralambano elsewhere often refers to
receiving someone into a close relationship.

The theme of being ready at any time for an unexpected return is also present in
three parables. These stories follow the passage in the Gospel of Matthew. They
describe being accepted as stewards of a kingdom.

## Conclusion

The rapture theory, while popular today, [emerged relatively
recently](#the-origin-of-the-theory) in church history through the work of John
Nelson Darby and later Hal Lindsey. Modern interpretations often rely on
specific readings of the [gathering of the elect](#the-gathering-of-the-elect)
and the [days of Noah](#like-the-days-of-noah). However, linguistic analysis of
the original Greek terms suggests that these passages may instead describe the
[acceptance or rejection](#like-the-days-of-noah) of individuals based on their
readiness for the Lord's return. Parables of [stewardship and
watchfulness](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) further emphasize the importance
of being prepared for an unexpected arrival.
