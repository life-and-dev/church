# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has fascinated millions, appearing in bestselling
books and blockbuster films. Many Christians believe in a future event where
believers are suddenly taken from the earth before a period of great
tribulation. This article explores where this idea originated and examines the
biblical passages often used to support or challenge this widely held belief.

## Origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. He published a book in 1970 titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

This suggests that believers would be unexpectedly gathered to a place in the
air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow would fall upon those who
remain.

Some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1 Thessalonians
4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings of Christ.
They see the rapture as a secret event to gather believers before the
tribulation. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish
his reign on Earth.

## Evaluating the Evidence

### Gathering of the elect

One of the verses often cited to support the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Some sources suggest that verse 31 was a later addition to the scripture.
However, textual scholarship confirms that this verse is present in major
ancient manuscripts like Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. While Codex
Bezae contains some additional phrases, the core of the verse is widely
accepted as original to the text.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the context indicates that the angels
gather the elect after the tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This
sequence challenges the theory that the rapture occurs before the tribulation.
Proponents of pretribulationism often argue that this gathering refers
specifically to a Jewish remnant rather than the Church. However, this
distinction relies on an interpretation of "the elect" that is not universally
shared by all biblical scholars.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44
> (ESV)

Jesus used the days of Noah to illustrate his return. He emphasized that the
coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. It will be like the flood that
caught people unaware.

Because Jesus said this event will occur unexpectedly and mentioned that "one
will be taken and one left," some believe this refers to a sudden
disappearance of believers.

The Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). It combines "pará,"
meaning alongside, and "lambánō," meaning to receive or acknowledge. This can
be understood as being received or accepted.

For example, the same word appears in these passages:

> Joseph, son of David, do not fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife. — Matthew
> 1:20 (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. It can imply being rejected or left behind. The same word is used
regarding forgiveness:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

This suggests that while two people may appear equal, one is accepted while the
other is rejected. Some critics argue that being "taken" refers to judgment,
similar to how the flood "swept away" people. However, in the context of the
following parables, being received by the master is consistently portrayed as
the reward for readiness. This theme of readiness for an unexpected return is
further explored in subsequent passages.

#### Faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
>   Truly, I say to you, he will **set him over all his possessions**.
> * But if that wicked servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and
>   begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
>   master of that servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him
>   and at an hour he does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him
>   with the hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing
>   of teeth**. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the

>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet
> him.’
>
> Then all those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps.
>
> And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for our lamps are
> going out.’
> But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not be enough for us and for
> you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.’
>
> And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and **those who were
> ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the door was shut.
>
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’
> But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, **I do not know you**.’
>
> Watch therefore, for **you know neither the day nor the hour**. — Matthew
> 25:1-13 (ESV)

#### Parable of the Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he
> made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents
> more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and
> hid his master’s money.
>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled
> accounts with them.
>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
> more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made
> five talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been
> faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of
> your master**.’
>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you
> delivered to me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been
> faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of
> your master**.’
>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew
> you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you
> scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the
> ground. Here, you have what is yours.’
> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that I
> reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then you
> ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should
> have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from him and
> give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will more be
> given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not, even what
> he has will be taken away. And **cast the worthless servant into the outer
> darkness**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.
> — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory remains a significant and often debated topic within
Christian eschatology. It traces its modern roots back to the 19th-century
teachings of [John Nelson Darby](#origin-of-the-theory) and was later
popularized in the 20th century. While some find support for a secret return of
Christ in specific [biblical passages](#evaluating-the-evidence), others
interpret these same verses as emphasizing the need for [readiness and faithful
stewardship](#days-of-noah) rather than a physical removal from the earth.
Ultimately, the discussion centers on the interpretation of [key Greek
terms](#days-of-noah) and the [context of Jesus' parables](#faithful-and-wise-servant)
regarding his return.
