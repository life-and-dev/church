# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has fascinated and divided theologians for nearly
two centuries. While many modern believers anticipate a sudden, secret
disappearance of the faithful, others argue that this interpretation deviates
from historical Christian teaching. The origins of this doctrine, ancient Greek
texts, and biblical parables continue to shape this ongoing debate.

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world. This perspective is often called
dispensationalism. It suggests that God deals with humanity in distinct periods
or "dispensations."

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular with a book that
he published in 1970 titled "The Late Great Planet Earth." Hal Lindsey is an
American author and Christian theologian. His book stated:

> "The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are
> ready. But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock
> and sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at
> a time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
>
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Hal Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a secret
place in the air to meet Jesus. He also noted that shock and sorrow would fall
upon those who remained on earth.

Some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like
1 Thessalonians 4:13-17; 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret event to gather believers before
the tribulation. This event is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish his reign on Earth.

## Scriptural Evidence

### Gathering the elect

One of the verses that is quoted to support the Rapture is Matthew 24:31.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
>
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

However, some scholars suggest that verse 31 was added to the scripture. It is
missing from some ancient manuscripts.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the angels gather the elect after the
tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man." This sequence challenges the
theory that the rapture will happen before the tribulation. Critics of this view
argue that "the elect" in this context refers specifically to Jewish believers
rather than the entire Church. They suggest that the Church is removed in an
earlier, separate event.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm)
> and one [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm)
> and one [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is coming.
> But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what part of
> the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have
> let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of
> Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus compared his coming to the days of Noah. He did not say Noah escaped the
flood. Instead, he emphasized that the coming of the Son of Man will be
unexpected.

Some people believe that the phrase "one will be taken and one left" refers to
an unexpected departure from Earth. The original Greek word for "taken" is
paralambanetai. This word combines "para," which means from alongside, and
"lambano," which means to receive or acknowledge. It can be explained as being
accepted into a position.

For example, the same word appears in these scriptures:

> “Joseph, son of David, do not fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife...
>
> — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word for "left" is aphietai. This word means to send away or
leave alone. It can imply being rejected or unforgiven.

The same word is also used in this scripture:

> But if you ***do not forgive*** others, then your Father will ***not forgive***
> your transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

From a human perspective, two people might seem equal. However, one might be
accepted while the other is rejected. This theme of being ready for an
unexpected return is also found in three parables.

Critiques of this interpretation point out that in the days of Noah, it was the
wicked who were "swept away" by the flood. They argue that being "taken" might
actually refer to being taken in judgment. Conversely, others maintain that
being "taken" always refers to being received by Christ, citing the positive
use of "paralambano" elsewhere in the New Testament.

## Stewardship Parables

### The faithful servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time?
>
> * Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes.
> Truly, I say to you, he will **set him over all his possessions**.
>
> * But if that wicked servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and
> begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the
> master of that servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him and
> at an hour he does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him with the
> hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.
>
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept.
>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet
> him.’
>
> Then all those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps.
>
> And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for our lamps
> are going out.’
> But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not be enough for us and for
> you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.’
>
> And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and **those who were
> ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the door was shut.
>
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’
> But he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, **I do not know you**.’
>
> Watch therefore, for **you know neither the day nor the hour**.
>
> — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
> he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two
> talents more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the
> ground and hid his master’s money.
>
> Now after a long time the master of those servants came and settled accounts
> with them.
>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
> more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made
> five talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been
> faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of your

>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you

> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been
> faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of your

>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew

> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that
> I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then you
> ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should
> have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from him and

## Conclusion

The doctrine of the Rapture emerged in the 19th century through the teachings
of [John Nelson Darby](#historical-origins). It was later popularized by
authors like [Hal Lindsey](#historical-origins). Modern interpretations often
center on [ancient Greek terms](#days-of-noah) and [textual scholarship](#gathering-the-elect)
to determine the timing and nature of Christ's return. Ultimately, the biblical
emphasis remains on the need for [faithful stewardship](#stewardship-parables)
and constant readiness for an unexpected arrival.
