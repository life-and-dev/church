# The Rapture Theory

The concept of the Rapture is a belief that Christians will be suddenly removed
from the Earth before a period of great tribulation. It is a cornerstone of
many modern eschatological views. While widely discussed today, this specific
interpretation of biblical prophecy has a distinct historical beginning. It
also relies on particular linguistic choices. This article explores how this
theory emerged and how it was popularized. It also examines what the original
Greek texts suggest about the "taking" and "leaving" of individuals in the end
times.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. In 1970, he published a book titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth." The book stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be a
> secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him. It will
> be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready. But
> for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

In other words, Lindsey suggested that Christians would be unexpectedly
"teleported" to a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow would
then fall upon those left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish his reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
> Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some scholars note that the timing of this gathering is critical. Although some
have questioned the verse's manuscript history, it is present in major early
codices like Sinaiticus and Vaticanus. The angels gather the elect after the
Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man." This sequence challenges the
Pre-tribulationist theory that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them
> all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in
> the field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the
> mill; one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not
> know on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of
> the house had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would
> have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore
> you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not
> expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is the "Rapture" compared to "the days of Noah"? It is not because Noah
"escaped the flood." If that were the case, Jesus would have said so. Instead,
he repeatedly echoed that the "coming of the Son of Man" will be unexpected
like the flood.

However, Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly. Combined with the words
"one will be taken and one left," some people believe this means the elect
would be teleported from Earth.

The original Greek word translated to "taken" is *paralambanetai* (Strong's
3880). This is a combination of *para*, meaning from close or alongside, and
*lambano*, meaning to receive, admit, or acknowledge. It could be explained as
being "accepted into position."

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20
> (ESV)
>
> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated to "left" is *aphietai* (Strong's 863). This
means to send away or leave alone. It can imply being "rejected" or
"unforgiven."

For example, the same word was used in this scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:14-15 (ESV)

Jesus more likely meant that two persons would be considered "equal" from a
human perspective. However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other
will be rejected. This theme of being ready for the Lord's return to be
accepted as stewards is confirmed by the three parables that immediately follow
this passage.

### The faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that
> servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to
> you, he will set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant
> says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow
> servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will
> come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and
> will cut him in pieces and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there
> will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will
> not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But
> he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for
> you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me
> five talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him,
> ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
> I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who
> had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two
> talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had
> received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a
> hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no
> seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you
> have what is yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful
> servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I
> scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers,
> and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take
> the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone
> who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who
> has not, even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant
> into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of
> teeth. — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Defense

Critiques of the "accepted vs. rejected" interpretation often point to the
immediate context of the flood in Matthew 24:39. In that verse, the flood
"swept away" the people. Some argue that being "taken" in verses 40-41 mirrors
being "swept away" in judgment. However, the use of *paralambano* elsewhere in
Matthew strongly suggests a positive reception or being received alongside. For
instance, it is used when Joseph "takes" Mary as his wife. Furthermore, the
parallel in Luke 17:37 shows the disciples asking "Where, Lord?" and Jesus
responding with a proverb about vultures and a corpse. This suggests that while
one is received by the Lord, the other is left to a place of judgment.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory is a relatively modern development in Christian theology. It
was first articulated by [John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) in the
19th century and later popularized by [Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory).
Linguistic analysis of the Greek terms [paralambanetai and
aphietai](#like-the-days-of-noah) suggest a theme of acceptance and rejection
rather than physical teleportation. This interpretation is supported by the
[parables of readiness](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) that emphasize being
found faithful upon the Lord's unexpected return.
