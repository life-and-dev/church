# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture is a central theme in modern Christian eschatology,
particularly within evangelical circles. It refers to a future event where
believers are caught up to meet Christ. This article explores the historical
development of this doctrine and examines the biblical passages often used to
support it, providing a balanced look at different theological interpretations.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pre-tribulational
rapture. This view suggests that believers will be removed from the earth before
a period of global suffering known as the Great Tribulation. His teachings
influenced many other Christians, and the movement spread from Ireland to
England and other parts of the world. Notably, Darby's injury in 1827 provided a
period of convalescence that allowed him to focus extensively on these
theological developments.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This perspective suggests that Christians will be unexpectedly "teleported" to a
meeting with Jesus in the air. This event results in shock and sorrow for those
who remain.

Furthermore, pre-tribulationists often interpret passages such as 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 as indicating two distinct
comings of Christ. The Rapture is viewed as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers. This is followed by a visible second coming to establish a
reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One verse often quoted to support the Rapture is Matthew 24:31.

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

While some historical discussions have questioned the textual variants of this
passage, it is widely accepted in major manuscripts like Sinaiticus and
Vaticanus. Notably, the angels gather the elect after the Tribulation and the
return of the "Son of Man." This sequence challenges the pre-tribulational
theory that the Rapture occurs before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and **they were unaware** until the flood came and swept them
> all away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Therefore, stay awake, for **you
> do not know** on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the
> master of the house **had known** in what part of the night the thief was
> coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken
> into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an
> hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison to the days of Noah highlights the unexpected nature of the
"coming of the Son of Man." Noah did not escape the flood in the sense of being
removed from the earth. Instead, the flood came upon the unaware.

The phrase "one will be taken and one left" is sometimes interpreted to mean
that believers will be removed from the earth. However, the original Greek word
for "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This term combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from alongside, and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or
acknowledge. This can be understood as being accepted into a specific position
or relationship.

For example, the same word is used in these scriptures:

> “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife” — Matthew 1:20
> (ESV)

and

> “he took his wife” — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word means to send away or
leave alone, signifying rejection or remaining unforgiven.

For example, the same word is used in this scripture:

> “but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses” — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

This suggests that while two individuals may appear equal from a human
perspective, one is accepted while the other is rejected. The theme of constant
readiness for the Lord's return is further supported by three subsequent
parables.

### Defending the linguistic distinction

Critics may argue that "taken" and "left" simply refer to a physical separation
without theological weight. However, the consistent use of *paralambano* in the
New Testament often denotes a purposeful reception into a relationship, as seen
in the examples of Joseph taking Mary as his wife. Similarly, *aphiemi* is used
in contexts of release or abandonment, reinforcing the idea of rejection. This
linguistic shift from the word *airo* (sweeping away in judgment) used for the
flood to *paralambano* (taken in reception) for the elect further distinguishes
the two groups.

#### The faithful and wise servant

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory traces its modern roots back to the 19th-century teachings of
[John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) and was later popularized by
authors like [Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory). While some interpret [the
gathering of the elect](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) in Matthew 24:31 as
evidence for a pre-tribulational event, others point out its timing after the
tribulation. The interpretation of [being taken and
left](#like-the-days-of-noah) in the context of the days of Noah remains a point
of scholarly debate. Furthermore, the [subsequent
parables](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) in Matthew 24 and 25 emphasize a theme
of constant readiness for the Lord's return.
