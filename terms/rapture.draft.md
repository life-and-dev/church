# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture, an event where believers are gathered to meet Jesus
Christ in the air, has become a central theme in modern Christian eschatology.
While widely discussed today, the theory has a specific historical origin and
relies on particular interpretations of New Testament passages. This article
explores the development of the Rapture theory and examines the biblical context
of the verses often used to support it.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian,
popularized Rapture theology in his 1970 book titled "The Late Great Planet
Earth." In this work, he stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**.
> It will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are
> ready. But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible
> shock and sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take
> place at a time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The
> Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Lindsey suggested that believers would be suddenly removed from Earth to a
secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow would fall
upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some pretribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical interpretation and evidence

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
> gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
> Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

While some arguments have been made regarding the textual history of Matthew
24:31, the verse is present in major early manuscripts such as Codex Sinaiticus
and Codex Vaticanus.

Regardless of its textual history, the passage indicates that the angels gather
the elect **after** the tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man." This
sequence presents a challenge to pretribulational theories that place the
rapture before the tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of
> heaven, nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
> For as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking,
> marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark,
> and **they were unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so
> will be the coming of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) . Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) .
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew
> 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison to "the days of Noah" emphasizes the unexpected nature of the
event. Jesus did not explicitly state that Noah escaped the flood before it
began. Instead, he repeatedly noted that the "coming of the Son of Man" will be
as unexpected as the flood was to those in Noah's time.

Because Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly in combination with the
words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some interpret these verses
to mean that believers will be suddenly removed from Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) . This
word is a combination of ["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) , which
means from close or alongside, and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm) , which means to receive,
admit, or acknowledge. This term can be explained as being received or accepted
into a position.

For example, the same word appears in these passages:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him;
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is
[ἀφίεται (aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) . It means to send away
or leave alone. In certain contexts, it can imply being rejected or left behind
in judgment.

The same word is used in this verse regarding forgiveness:

> But if you do not forgive men, then your Father will not forgive your
> transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

In this view, Jesus may have meant that two persons who appear equal from a
human perspective will be separated. One will be **accepted like a bride**,
while the other will be **rejected**.

Critics of this interpretation often point to 1 Thessalonians 4:17, which
describes believers being "caught up... in the clouds to meet the Lord in the
air." However, others argue that this describes a "royal welcome." In ancient
times, citizens would go out to meet a visiting dignitary and escort them back
to the city. This would align the event with a single, visible second coming.

The theme of being ready for the Lord's unexpected return to be accepted as
stewards is supported by the three parables that immediately follow this
passage.

### The faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? * Blessed is that
> servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to
> you, he will **set him over all his possessions**. * But if that wicked
> servant says to himself, ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his
> fellow servants and eats and drinks with drunkards, the master of that
> servant will come on a day when he **does not expect** him and at an hour he
> does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him with the
> hypocrites**. In that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**.
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed,
> they all became drowsy and slept.
>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet
> him.’
>
> Then all those virgins rose and trimmed their lamps.
>
> And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for our lamps
> are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not be
> enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for yourselves.’
>
> And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and **those who were
> ready went in with him to the marriage feast**, and the door was shut.
>
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But
> he answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, **I do not know you**.’
>
> Watch therefore, for **you know neither the day nor the hour**. — Matthew
> 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
> he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two
> talents more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the
> ground and hid his master’s money.
>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled
> accounts with them.
>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
> more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made
> five talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful
> servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much.
> **Enter into the joy of your master**.’
>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you
> delivered to me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’ His master
> said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful
> over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of your
> master**.’
>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I
> knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering
> where you scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent
> in the ground. Here, you have what is yours.’ But his master answered him,
> ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown
> and gather where I scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my
> money with the bankers, and at my coming I should have received what was my
> own with interest. So take the talent from him and give it to him who has
> the ten talents. For to everyone who has will more be given, and he will
> have an abundance. But from the one who has not, even what he has will be
> taken away. And **cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness**. In
> that place there will be **weeping and gnashing of teeth**. — Matthew
> 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has specific historical roots in the 19th century through the
teachings of [John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) . It was further
popularized in the 20th century by authors such as
[Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory) . Biblical examination suggests that
the gathering of the elect described in
[Matthew 24:31](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) occurs after the tribulation rather
than before it. Furthermore, the context of being
[taken or left](#like-the-days-of-noah) in Matthew 24 may refer to a separation
for judgment and acceptance into the kingdom. The
[supporting parables](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) emphasize the necessity of
constant readiness and faithfulness for the Lord's return.
