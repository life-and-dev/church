# Rapture

The concept of a secret return of Christ to "snatch away" his followers has
captivated the imagination of many for nearly two centuries. Where did this idea
come from, and how does it align with the original Greek texts of the New
Testament? Exploring the history and biblical foundations of the rapture theory
reveals a fascinating journey through theology and linguistics.

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. This movement eventually
spread to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled "The
Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

This perspective suggests that Christians will be unexpectedly transported to a
secret location in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and sorrow will fall
upon those who remain behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers, followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his reign on
Earth.

## Biblical Analysis

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verse that is quoted to proof the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Textual scholarship confirms that Matthew 24:31 is present in major early
manuscripts. These include Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. While some
textual variants exist in the New Testament, this particular verse is widely
accepted as authentic.

The text indicates that the angels gather the elect after the tribulation. This
occurs following the visible return of the Son of Man. This sequence of events
challenges the pretribulational theory. That theory suggests the rapture happens
before the period of tribulation. Proponents of the pretribulational view argue
that the "gathering" in Matthew 24:31 refers to a separate event for ethnic
Israel. They contend that the church is removed earlier in a distinct event.
However, the context of Jesus' discourse suggests a singular climactic return.
The Bible describes the "day of the LORD" as a visible and awesome event rather
than a secret one. For example, the prophet Joel spoke of cosmic signs
accompanying this day.

> The sun shall be turned to darkness, and the moon to blood, before the great
> and awesome day of the LORD comes. — Joel 2:31 (ESV)

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Why is the *"Rapture"* like "the days of Noah"?

Not because Noah "escaped the flood", otherwise Jesus would have said so, but he
does not.  Instead, he repeatedly echoed that the "the coming of the Son of Man"
will be unexpected like the flood in the days of Noah.

However, because Jesus repeatedly says this event will occur unexpectedly in
combination with the words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some
people believe this means that the elect would unexpectedly be teleported from
Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This word combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close or alongside,
with ["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), which means to receive or
acknowledge. This term describes being accepted into a specific position or
relationship.

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> "Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which
> is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. In this context, it implies being rejected or left unforgiven.

For example the same word was also used in the scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

Jesus likely meant that while two people might appear similar from a human
perspective, one will be accepted while the other is rejected. The Greek word
*paralambano* means to "receive near" or "take to oneself". Conversely,
*aphiemi* means to "send away" or "leave alone". This theme of being ready for
the Lord's unexpected return is also confirmed by the three parables that
immediately follow this passage. These parables emphasize the importance of
being faithful stewards of his kingdom.

## Conclusion

The rapture theory is a relatively modern development in Christian theology
[Historical Origins](#historical-origins). Scriptural evidence indicates that
the gathering of believers is associated with the visible return of Christ after
the tribulation [The gathering of the elect](#the-gathering-of-the-elect). Greek
word studies suggest that the "taken" individuals are those accepted into a
relationship or position of service [Like the days of Noah](#like-the-days-of-noah).
The primary focus of Jesus' teachings on the end times was the necessity
of remaining faithful and prepared for his unexpected arrival [The faithful and
wise servant](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant).
