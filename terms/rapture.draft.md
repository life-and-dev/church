# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has become a cornerstone of modern Christian
eschatology, yet its origins and scriptural foundations are often the subject
of intense debate. This article explores where this theory began and examines
what the original biblical language suggests about the events described in the
New Testament.

## The origin of the theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular with a book
that he published in 1970. This book was titled *The Late Great Planet Earth*.
He wrote:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return for
> His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father.
> — Hal Lindsey, *The Late Great Planet Earth*, Chapter 12

Hal Lindsey described an event where Christians would be unexpectedly
"teleported" to a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, shock and
sorrow would fall upon those left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like
1 Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers. This would be followed by a later, visible second coming to
establish his reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

The text indicates that the angels gather the elect after the Tribulation and
the return of the "Son of Man". This sequence contradicts the
Pre-tribulationists theory that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is coming.
> But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what part of the
> night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would not have let
> his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man
> is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison between the Rapture and "the days of Noah" focuses on the
unexpected nature of the event.

Jesus does not say it is because Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he repeatedly
echoed that "the coming of the Son of Man" will be unexpected like the flood in
the days of Noah.

However, because Jesus repeatedly says this event will occur unexpectedly in
combination with the words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some
people believe this means that the elect would unexpectedly be teleported from
Earth.

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This
word is a combination of [*pará*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), which
means from close or alongside, and [*lambánō*](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm),
which means to receive, admit, or acknowledge. This suggests an interpretation
of being "accepted into position".

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> An angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David,
> do not fear to take Mary as your wife, for that which is conceived in her is
> from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
> he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "left" is
[ἀφίεται (aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). This word means to
send away or leave alone. It can imply being rejected or remaining unforgiven.

For example, the same word was also used in the scripture:

> but if you do not forgive others their trespasses, neither will your Father
> forgive your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

These linguistic connections suggest that from a human perspective, two persons
would be considered equal. One would be accepted like a bride while the other
would be rejected.

The theme of being ready for the Lord's unexpected return is also confirmed by
the three parables that immediately follow this passage. Believers are
encouraged to be ready to be accepted as stewards of his kingdom.

## Defense

Critics often argue that the Rapture is explicitly taught in 1 Thessalonians 4,
which describes believers being "caught up together" in the clouds to meet the
Lord. They point to this as evidence for a secret removal of the church.
However, others suggest that the language of meeting the Lord in the air was a
cultural expression of welcoming a returning king. In this view, believers meet
the Lord to escort him back to Earth, rather than being taken away to another
location.

The interpretation of being "taken" and "left" in Matthew 24 also faces
scrutiny. Proponents of the Rapture view being "taken" as a positive event of
being rescued. Yet, the context of the days of Noah suggests that those who
were "swept away" were those taken in judgment. This highlights the importance
of examining the linguistic and historical context of these passages.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory traces its origins to the 19th-century teachings of
[John Nelson Darby](#the-origin-of-the-theory) and was later popularized by
[Hal Lindsey](#the-origin-of-the-theory). However, an examination of the
[biblical sequence of events](#the-gathering-of-the-elect) and the
[original Greek language](#like-the-days-of-noah) suggests that the "taken" and
"left" passages may refer to being accepted or rejected in judgment rather than
a secret removal of believers. [Theological defenses](#defense) provide further
context by comparing these events to cultural practices and surrounding
parables.
