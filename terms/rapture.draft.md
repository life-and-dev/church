# The Rapture: Origins and Interpretations

The concept of the Rapture has captured the imagination of millions. It is often
depicted as a sudden, mysterious disappearance of believers from the Earth.
While it is a cornerstone of modern eschatological thought for many, its origins
and the biblical passages used to support it are subjects of intense study and
debate. This article explores where this theory came from. A closer look at the
original Greek texts might offer a different perspective on these well-known
scriptures.

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby, argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular with a book that
he published in 1970. The book is titled "The Late Great Planet Earth". It
states:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Hal Lindsey appears to suggest that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a
secret place in the air to meet Jesus. He notes that shock and sorrow would fall
upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages to
suggest two separate comings of Christ. These include 1 Thessalonians 4:13-17
and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52. They see the rapture as a secret event to gather
believers before the tribulation. This is followed by a later, visible second
coming to establish his reign on Earth.

## Scriptural Analysis

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

However, verse 31 is present in major early manuscripts like Codex Sinaiticus
and Codex Vaticanus.

Regardless of the authenticity, the angels gather the elect after the
Tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This contradicts the theory that
the rapture happens before the Tribulation.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
> concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
> the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
> coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
> eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
> entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
> away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
> field; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Two women will be grinding at
> the mill; one will be [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). Therefore, stay awake, for you
> do not know on what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master
> of the house had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he
> would have stayed awake and would not have let his house be broken into.
> Therefore you also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you
> do not expect. — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

Jesus repeatedly echoed that the coming of the Son of Man will be unexpected. He
compared it to the flood in the days of Noah. He did not say that the rapture is
like the days of Noah because Noah escaped the flood.

Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly. He uses the words "one will be
taken and one left". Some people believe this means that the elect would
unexpectedly be moved from Earth.

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "taken" is
[παραλαμβάνεται (paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This is
a combination of ["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm) and
["ambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm). It means from close or
alongside and to receive, admit or acknowledge. It could be explained as being
"accepted into position".

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear to take Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. This can imply being "rejected" or "unforgiven".

For example the same word was also used in the scripture:

> But if you do not forgive others, then your Father will not forgive your
> transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

Jesus likely meant that two persons would be considered equal from a human
perspective. However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other will be
rejected.

Critiques of this view often point to the immediate context of the flood. In the
flood, those "swept away" were the wicked. Some argue that being "taken" might
similarly refer to being taken in judgment. However, the use of *paralambano*
(to take to oneself) elsewhere in Matthew suggests a positive reception. This is
in contrast to *airo* (to sweep away) used for the flood in verse 39.

This theme is about being ready at any time for the unexpected return of the
Lord. It is about being accepted as stewards of his kingdom. This is confirmed
by the three parables that immediately follow this passage.

### The faithful and wise servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
> household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
> whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
> set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
> ‘My master is delayed,’ and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
> drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
> does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
> and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
> gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
> went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise.
> For when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the
> wise took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they
> all became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the
> bridegroom! Come out to meet him.’ Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
> their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, ‘Give us some of your oil, for
> our lamps are going out.’ But the wise answered, saying, ‘Since there will not
> be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
> yourselves.’ And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
> who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
> Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, Lord, lord, open to us.’ But he
> answered, ‘Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.’ Watch therefore, for you
> know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
> entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two,
> to another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who
> had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made
> five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more.
> But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
> master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
> settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
> forward, bringing five talents more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five
> talents; here, I have made five talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well
> done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
> set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ And he also who had the
> two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me two talents;
> here, I have made two talents more.’ His master said to him, ‘Well done, good
> and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you
> over much. Enter into the joy of your master.’ He also who had received the
> one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping
> where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was
> afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you have what is
> yours.’ But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You
> knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed?
> Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I
> should have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from
> him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone who has will
> more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who has not,
> even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant into the
> outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. —
> Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The theory of a pre-tribulational rapture was popularized by figures like [John
Nelson Darby](#historical-origins) and [Hal Lindsey](#historical-origins). It
relies on specific interpretations of the [Olivet
Discourse](#scriptural-analysis). However, a [linguistic
analysis](#scriptural-analysis) of the original Greek words for "taken" and
"left" suggests a theme of acceptance and rejection rather than physical
removal. This perspective is further supported by the [parables of
Jesus](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant), which emphasize the importance of being
spiritually prepared for an unexpected return.
