# Rapture

The concept of the Rapture has become a cornerstone of modern eschatology. It
is often depicted in popular culture as a sudden and mysterious disappearance
of millions of people from the earth. This article examines the historical
emergence of this doctrine and explores the scriptural passages frequently
cited by proponents and critics alike. By delving into the original Greek and
historical context, one can gain a deeper understanding of the different
perspectives regarding this significant theological topic.

## Historical Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership
and teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey made the Rapture theology popular. He was an
American author and Christian theologian. In 1970, he published a book titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
**a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
Planet Earth, Chapter 12

Hal Lindsey seemed to think that Christians would be unexpectedly teleported to
a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow would then fall upon
those who were left behind.

Some pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1 Thessalonians
4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings of Christ.
They see the rapture as a secret event before the tribulation to gather
believers. This is followed by a later visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Scriptural Analysis

### Gathering of the Elect

The Rapture theory often highlights specific scriptural passages as
foundational evidence. For example, 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17 (ESV) describes the
Lord descending from heaven with a cry of command. It states that the dead in
Christ will rise first, and then those who are alive will be caught up together
with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air.

> For the Lord himself will descend from heaven with a cry of command, with the
voice of an archangel, and with the sound of the trumpet of God. And the dead
in Christ will rise first. Then we who are alive, who are left, will be caught
up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air, and so we will
always be with the Lord. — 1 Thessalonians 4:16-17 (ESV)

In this passage, the Greek word for "meeting" is [*apantesin* (Strong's
529)](https://biblehub.com/greek/529.htm). In ancient times, this term
specifically referred to a formal delegation of citizens who would leave
their city to meet a visiting dignitary and then escort them back into the
city. This suggests that believers meet Christ in the air to escort him to the
earth, rather than to be taken away from it.

Another frequently cited verse is Matthew 24:31 (ESV):

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. —
Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

While some commentators have questioned the inclusion of this verse in certain
textual traditions, major uncials such as
[Sinaiticus](https://codexsinaiticus.org/en/manuscript.aspx?book=33&chapter=24&lid=en&side=r&verse=31)
and [Vaticanus](https://digi.vatlib.it/view/MSS_Vat.gr.1209) confirm its
authenticity. The verse indicates that the angels gather the elect after the
period of tribulation and the visible return of the Son of Man. This sequence
appears to contradict the pre-tribulation view that the rapture occurs before
the tribulation begins. Proponents also look to 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV),
which describes the "twinkling of an eye" transformation of believers.

> Behold! I tell you a mystery. We shall not all sleep, but we shall all be
changed, in a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet. For the
trumpet will sound, and the dead will be raised imperishable, and we shall be
changed. — 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 (ESV)

### Like the Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away. But
concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven, nor
the Son, but the Father only. For as were the days of Noah, so will be the
coming of the Son of Man. For as in those days before the flood they were
eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah
entered the ark, and they were unaware until the flood came and swept them all
away, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. Then two men will be in the
field; one will be taken and one left. Two women will be grinding at the mill;
one will be taken and one left. Therefore, stay awake, for you do not know on
what day your Lord is coming. But know this, that if the master of the house
had known in what part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed
awake and would not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must
be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect. —
Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison of the coming of the Son of Man to the days of Noah emphasizes
the unexpected nature of the event. Jesus does not state that Noah escaped the
flood in a way that parallels a secret rapture. Instead, he repeatedly
emphasizes that his coming will occur when people are unaware. Because Jesus
mentions that one will be taken and another left in this context, some believe
this refers to a sudden disappearance or teleportation of believers from the
earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [*paralambanetai* (Strong's
3880)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This is a form of the word
*paralambano*, which is derived from *para*, meaning alongside, and *lambano*,
meaning to take or receive. This term can be understood as being received or
accepted into a close relationship. For instance, the same word describes
Joseph taking Mary as his wife in Matthew 1:20 and Matthew 1:24. In the context
of the flood in Matthew 24:39, a different Greek word, [*airo* (Strong's
142)](https://biblehub.com/greek/142.htm), is used to describe how the people
were "swept away" in judgment.

In contrast, the Greek word translated as "left" in Matthew 24:40-41 is
[*aphietai* (Strong's 863)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm), which is a form
of *aphiemi*. This word can mean to leave alone or send away. It often carries
the nuance of being rejected or left behind in an unforgiven state. This usage
appears in Matthew 6:15 regarding the failure to forgive others.

The context suggests that two individuals may appear identical from a human
perspective. One is accepted into fellowship, while the other is rejected.

The theme of maintaining readiness for the unexpected return of the Lord is
further illustrated by the three parables that follow this passage. These
stories emphasize the rewards for faithful stewards and the consequences for
those who are found unprepared.

#### The Faithful and Wise Servant

> Who then is the faithful and wise servant, whom his master has set over his
household, to give them their food at the proper time? Blessed is that servant
whom his master will find so doing when he comes. Truly, I say to you, he will
set him over all his possessions. But if that wicked servant says to himself,
“My master is delayed,” and begins to beat his fellow servants and eats and
drinks with drunkards, the master of that servant will come on a day when he
does not expect him and at an hour he does not know and will cut him in pieces
and put him with the hypocrites. In that place there will be weeping and
gnashing of teeth. — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

#### The Ten Virgins

> Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and
went to meet the bridegroom. Five of them were foolish, and five were wise. For
when the foolish took their lamps, they took no oil with them, but the wise
took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they all
became drowsy and slept. But at midnight there was a cry, “Here is the
bridegroom! Come out to meet him.” Then all those virgins rose and trimmed
their lamps. And the foolish said to the wise, “Give us some of your oil, for
our lamps are going out.” But the wise answered, saying, “Since there will
not be enough for us and for you, go rather to the dealers and buy for
yourselves.” And while they were going to buy, the bridegroom came, and those
who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.
Afterward the other virgins came also, saying, “Lord, lord, open to us.”
But he answered, “Truly, I say to you, I do not know you.” Watch therefore,
for you know neither the day nor the hour. — Matthew 25:1-13 (ESV)

#### The Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to
another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away. He who had
received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and he made five
talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents more. But he
who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid his
master’s money. Now after a long time the master of those servants came and
settled accounts with them. And he who had received the five talents came
forward, bringing five talents more, saying, “Master, you delivered to me
five talents; here, I have made five talents more.” His master said to him,
“Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little;
I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.” And he also who
had the two talents came forward, saying, “Master, you delivered to me two
talents; here, I have made two talents more.” His master said to him, “Well
done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will
set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master.” He also who had
received the one talent came forward, saying, “Master, I knew you to be a
hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no
seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground. Here, you
have what is yours.” But his master answered him, “You wicked and slothful
servant! You knew that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I
scattered no seed? Then you ought to have invested my money with the bankers,
and at my coming I should have received what was my own with interest. So take
the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents. For to everyone
who has will more be given, and he will have an abundance. But from the one who
has not, even what he has will be taken away. And cast the worthless servant
into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of
teeth.” — Matthew 25:14-30 (ESV)

## Defense of Perspectives

The interpretation of being taken or left remains a subject of debate among
theologians. Critics of the view that being taken refers to acceptance often
point to the context of the flood. They argue that those who were taken by the
flood were the wicked who were destroyed. However, defenders of the acceptance
view highlight the linguistic shift in the Greek text. They note that the flood
"swept away" (*airo*) the people, whereas the individuals in the field are
"taken" (*paralambano*), a word often used for receiving someone into
fellowship.

Furthermore, the timing of the gathering in Matthew 24:31 is seen by some as a
challenge to the pre-tribulation theory. Proponents of a pre-tribulation
rapture may argue that Matthew 24 primarily addresses the Jewish people and the
Second Coming. They suggest that the rapture of the church is a separate
mystery revealed elsewhere in the New Testament.

## Conclusion

The Rapture is a complex theological concept with [historical
origins](#historical-origins) rooted in the 19th century. [Scriptural
analysis](#scriptural-analysis) reveals diverse interpretations of the timing
and nature of this event. The [gathering of the elect](#gathering-of-the-elect)
and the [parables of readiness](#the-faithful-and-wise-servant) underscore the
importance of maintaining spiritual vigilance. Ultimately, the debate
highlights the ongoing effort to understand the biblical promises regarding the
return of Christ.
