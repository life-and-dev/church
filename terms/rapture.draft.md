# Rapture

The concept of a "Rapture" has become a central theme in many modern discussions
about the end of the world. While some believe in a secret event where faithful
individuals are suddenly removed from Earth, others point to different
historical and biblical interpretations. This article examines the development
of the Rapture theory and explores the scriptural evidence often cited to
support or challenge these views.

## Theory Origins

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. He convinced many other Christians of this view. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement spread to
England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian, made
the Rapture theology popular. He published a book in 1970 titled *The Late Great
Planet Earth*. The book stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father. — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12

Some people believe that Christians would be unexpectedly "teleported" to a
secret place somewhere in the air to meet Jesus. They also believe that shock
and sorrow will be upon those who are left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to gather
believers. This is followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Biblical Evidence

### Gathering the Elect

One of the verses that is quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

However, according to [some
sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textual_variants_in_the_New_Testament)
verse 31 was added to the scripture and is missing from some texts.

Even if the scripture is authentic, the angels gather the elect **after** the
Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man". This challenges the Pre-
tribulationists theory that the rapture will happen before the Tribulation.

### Days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44
> (ESV)

The "Rapture" is often compared to "the days of Noah". Some interpret this
comparison to mean that Noah escaped the flood. However, Jesus repeatedly echoed
that "the coming of the Son of Man" will be unexpected like the flood in the
days of Noah.

Because Jesus says this event will occur unexpectedly in combination with the
words "one will be ***taken*** and one ***left***", some people believe this
means that the elect would unexpectedly be teleported from Earth.

A linguistic shift occurs in the Greek text of Matthew 24:39-41. The word for
"swept away" in verse 39 is *airo* (Strong's 142), which describes the flood
taking away the people in judgment. In contrast, verses 40-41 use the word
*paralambano* (Strong's 3880) for being "taken".

The word *paralambano* is a combination of *para* (alongside) and *lambano* (to
take). It literally means "to receive near" or "take to oneself". This suggests
an intimate act of receiving rather than a destructive removal. It could be
explained as to be "accepted into position".

For example, the same word was used in these scriptures:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
> him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear **to take** Mary as
> your wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. —
> Matthew 1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> **took** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "left" is *aphiemi*
(Strong's 863). This word means to send away or leave alone, which can imply
being "rejected" or "unforgiven".

The same word *aphiemi* is used in the context of forgiveness in the following
scripture:

> but if you **do not forgive** others their trespasses, neither will your
> Father **forgive** your trespasses. — Matthew 6:15 (ESV)

From a human perspective, two persons would be considered "equal". However, one
will be **accepted like a bride** while the other would be **rejected**.

This theme of being ready for the Lord's unexpected return is also confirmed by
three parables that immediately follow this passage.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory has its [origins in the 19th century](#theory-origins). It
was further popularized in the 20th century. However, [biblical
evidence](#biblical-evidence) suggests that the gathering of believers occurs
after the Tribulation. A linguistic analysis of the terms [taken and
left](#days-of-noah) indicates that the focus is on being accepted or rejected
by God. The [parables of Jesus](#faithful-servant) reinforce the importance of
being spiritually prepared at all times.
