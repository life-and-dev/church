# Rapture

The concept of a sudden, secret disappearance of believers from the Earth has
fascinated and puzzled many for generations. While often depicted in popular
media and modern literature, the origins and biblical foundations of this event
invite a deeper investigation into how these interpretations developed over
time.

## Origin of the Rapture Theory

In 1827, John Nelson Darby argued that the Bible taught a pretribulational
rapture. This view suggests that believers will be taken to heaven before a
period of global suffering known as the Tribulation. Darby's leadership and
teachings attracted a growing number of followers. The movement subsequently
spread to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, Hal Lindsey, an American author and Christian theologian,
made the Rapture theology popular with a book that he published in 1970, titled
"The Late Great Planet Earth" that stated:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
**a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
time unknown to anyone except God the Father."
>
> — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great Planet Earth, Chapter 12

This interpretation suggests that Christians would be unexpectedly moved to a
secret location in the air to meet Jesus. Meanwhile, those remaining on Earth
would experience shock and sorrow.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists often interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate
comings of Christ. They see the rapture as a secret, pre-tribulation event to
gather believers, followed by a later, visible second coming to establish his
reign on Earth.

## Examination of Scriptural Evidence

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verse that is quoted to proof the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will
gather his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other.
>
> — Matthew 24:31 (ESV)

Some have questioned the authenticity of Matthew 24:31. However, major uncials
such as Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus include this verse, providing
strong textual evidence for its inclusion. The scripture states that the angels
gather the elect after the Tribulation and the return of the "Son of Man." This
sequence challenges the pretribulational theory that the gathering happens
before the period of suffering.

### Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man.
For as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying
and giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they
were unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the
coming of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
[*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
[*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
[*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
[*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**.
>
> — Matthew 24:35-44 (ESV)

The comparison of the "coming of the Son of Man" to the "days of Noah"
emphasizes the unexpected nature of the event. Jesus did not explicitly state
that Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he highlighted that the flood came when
people were unaware. Because Jesus mentioned that this event occurs
unexpectedly and used the phrase "one will be taken and one left," some
interpret this to mean that the elect will be suddenly removed from Earth.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This word combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from alongside, and
["lambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or
acknowledge. This could be interpreted as being accepted into a specific
position.

For example the same word was used in these scriptures:

> But as he considered these things, behold, an angel of the Lord appeared to
him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not fear to take Mary as your
wife, for that which is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit.” — Matthew
1:20 (ESV)

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him:
he took his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word that was translated to the English "left" is
[ἀφίεται (aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm) which means to
send away or leave alone, i.e. "reject" or "unforgiven".

For example the same word was also used in the scripture:

> But if you ***do not forgive*** others, then your Father will ***not
forgive*** your transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

Jesus likely meant that two people might appear similar from a human
perspective, yet one is accepted while the other is rejected. This theme of
remaining ready for the Lord's unexpected return is supported by the three
parables that follow this passage.

#### The faithful and wise servant

>

at an hour he does not know and will **cut him in pieces and put him with the

>
> — Matthew 24:45-51 (ESV)

took flasks of oil with their lamps. As the bridegroom was delayed, they all

>
> But at midnight there was a cry, ‘Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet

>

#### Parable of the Talents

> For it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and
entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, to
another one, to each according to his ability. Then he went away.
>
> He who had received the five talents went at once and traded with them, and
he made five talents more. So also he who had the two talents made two talents
more. But he who had received the one talent went and dug in the ground and hid
his master’s money.
>
> Now **after a long time the master of those servants came** and settled

>
> And he who had received the five talents came forward, bringing five talents
more, saying, ‘Master, you delivered to me five talents; here, I have made
five talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of

>
> And he also who had the two talents came forward, saying, ‘Master, you
delivered to me two talents; here, I have made two talents more.’
> His master said to him, ‘Well done, good and faithful servant. You have
been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. **Enter into the joy of
your master**.’
>
> He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, ‘Master, I

> But his master answered him, ‘You wicked and slothful servant! You knew
that I reap where I have not sown and gather where I scattered no seed? Then
you ought to have invested my money with the bankers, and at my coming I should
have received what was my own with interest. So take the talent from him and

he has will be taken away. And **cast the worthless servant into the outer

>

## Defense of the Rapture View

While the sequence in Matthew 24 suggests a post-tribulation gathering,
proponents of a pretribulational rapture often point to other scriptures for
support. One key passage is 1 Thessalonians 4:17, which describes believers
being "caught up" to meet the Lord in the air.

> Then we who are alive, who are left, will be caught up together with them in
the clouds to meet the Lord in the air, and so we will always be with the Lord.
— 1 Thessalonians 4:17 (ESV)

Critics of the pretribulational view argue that the Greek word for "meeting"
(*apantesis*) used here specifically refers to a formal delegation meeting a
visiting dignitary to escort them back into their city. In this context,
believers would meet the returning King in the air and immediately escort Him
back to Earth, rather than being taken away to heaven for seven years.

## Conclusion

The doctrine of the Rapture, specifically the pretribulational view, is a
relatively modern development in church history. Its origins can be traced back
to the [19th-century teachings of John Nelson
Darby](#origin-of-the-rapture-theory) and were later popularized by authors
like Hal Lindsey. A close [examination of scriptural
evidence](#examination-of-scriptural-evidence) suggests that the passages often
used to support a secret disappearance may instead emphasize the importance of
[spiritual readiness and faithfulness](#like-the-days-of-noah) in anticipation
of Christ's return. Understanding the linguistic nuances of terms like "taken"
and "left" provides a different perspective on being [accepted or
rejected](#like-the-days-of-noah) based on one's relationship with God. The
[historical use of language](#defense-of-the-rapture-view) in the New Testament
also offers insights into how early readers might have understood the nature of
Christ's return.
