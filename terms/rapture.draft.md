# Rapture

Many Christians hold to a belief in an event where believers are suddenly taken
from the Earth. This concept, often called the Rapture, has become a significant
part of modern theological discussions about the end of the age. While widely
popular today, its origins and biblical basis are subjects of ongoing study and
debate. This article explores where this theory began and examines the primary
scriptures often used to support or challenge its claims.

## Origin of the theory

The modern concept of a pre-tribulation rapture emerged in the early 19th
century. In 1827, [John Nelson
Darby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Nelson_Darby) began to articulate the
view that the Bible taught a separate event where believers would be gathered
before the Great Tribulation. He convinced many other Christians of this
perspective during his leadership of the Plymouth Brethren. The movement
eventually spread to England and other parts of the world.

Many years later, [Hal Lindsey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hal_Lindsey) made
this theology widely popular in the 20th century. He published a book in 1970
titled *The Late Great Planet Earth*. The book states:

> The Bible teaches that there is coming a time when Jesus Christ will return
> for His church. But this will not be His final return to earth. This will be
> **a secret, invisible return in the air to meet those who believe in Him**. It
> will be a moment of unbelievable joy and excitement for those who are ready.
> But for those who are not ready, it will be a moment of terrible shock and
> sorrow. The Bible calls this event the 'rapture' which will take place at a
> time unknown to anyone except God the Father." — Hal Lindsey, The Late Great
> Planet Earth, Chapter 12.

Lindsey's writing suggests a belief that Christians would be unexpectedly taken
to a secret place in the air to meet Jesus. Shock and sorrow would then fall
upon those who were left behind.

In addition, some Pre-tribulationists interpret biblical passages like 1
Thessalonians 4:13-17 and 1 Corinthians 15:51-52 to suggest two separate comings
of Christ. They view the rapture as a secret event to gather the Church. This is
followed by a later, visible second coming to establish a reign on Earth.

## Proof of the theory

### The gathering of the elect

One of the verses quoted to prove the Rapture is:

> And he will send out his angels with a loud trumpet call, and they will gather
> his elect from the four winds, from one end of heaven to the other. — Matthew
> 24:31 (ESV)

Analysis of the context in Matthew 24 shows that this gathering occurs after the
Tribulation and the return of the Son of Man. This sequence challenges the
theory that the rapture must happen before the Tribulation.

Critics of this view sometimes argue that Matthew 24:31 refers specifically to
the gathering of Israel rather than the Church. They suggest that the "elect" in
this passage are distinct from the believers who would have been raptured
earlier. However, the term "elect" is used throughout the New Testament to refer
to all believers chosen by God. For example, Paul uses the same term for the
Church in Colossians 3:12 and Titus 1:1. The text does not provide an explicit
distinction between two different groups of elect ones.

## Like the days of Noah

> Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away.
>
> But concerning that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels of heaven,
> nor the Son, but the Father only.
>
> **For as were the days of Noah**, so will be the coming of the Son of Man. For
> as in those days before the flood they were eating and drinking, marrying and
> giving in marriage, until the day when Noah entered the ark, and **they were
> unaware** until the flood came and swept them all away, so will be the coming
> of the Son of Man.
>
> Then two men will be in the field; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
> Two women will be grinding at the mill; one will be
> [*taken*](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm) and one
> [*left*](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm).
>
> Therefore, stay awake, for **you do not know** on what day your Lord is
> coming. But know this, that if the master of the house **had known** in what
> part of the night the thief was coming, he would have stayed awake and would
> not have let his house be broken into. Therefore you also must be ready, for
> the Son of Man is coming at an hour **you do not expect**. — Matthew 24:35-44
> (ESV)

The comparison to the days of Noah highlights the unexpected nature of these
events. Jesus does not state that Noah escaped the flood. Instead, he repeatedly
emphasizes that the coming of the Son of Man will be sudden. It will be like the
flood in the days of Noah.

Some people believe that the phrase "one will be taken and one left" implies
that the elect would be teleported from Earth. This interpretation often relies
on the unexpected nature of the event.

The original Greek word translated as "taken" is [παραλαμβάνεται
(paralambanetai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/3880.htm). This word combines
["pará"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3844.htm), meaning from close or alongside,
and ["ambánō"](https://biblehub.com/greek/2983.htm), meaning to receive or
acknowledge. This term can be explained as being accepted into a position.

For example, the same word appeared in these scriptures:

> The Lord appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, do not
> fear ***to take*** Mary as your wife. — Matthew 1:20 (ESV)"

and

> When Joseph woke from sleep, he did as the angel of the Lord commanded him: he
> ***took*** his wife. — Matthew 1:24 (ESV)

The original Greek word translated as "left" is [ἀφίεται
(aphietai)](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm). It means to send away or leave
alone. This implies being rejected or left unforgiven.

For example, the same word appeared in this scripture:

> But if you ***do not forgive*** others, then your Father will ***not
> forgive*** your transgressions. — Matthew 6:15 (NASB 1995)

Jesus likely meant that two persons might appear equal from a human perspective.
However, one will be accepted like a bride while the other will be rejected.

A common critical argument is that "taken" refers to a physical rapture to
heaven. Proponents of this view often cite John 14:3, where Jesus uses the same
word *paralambano* to describe taking his disciples to himself. However, the
immediate context of Matthew 24 emphasizes a master returning to his house to
find his servants ready for service. In this setting, being taken alongside the
master implies being received into a position of responsibility or honor within
the household. This is consistent with the usage in Matthew 1:20 and 1:24, where
Joseph receives Mary into his home as his wife.

A central theme is being ready at any time for the unexpected return of the
Lord. Believers are encouraged to be accepted as stewards of his kingdom. This
theme is confirmed by the three parables that immediately follow this passage.

## Conclusion

The Rapture theory traces its primary development to the 19th century through
the teachings of John Nelson Darby [Origin of the
theory](#origin-of-the-theory). Popularized in the 20th century by authors like
Hal Lindsey, it often emphasizes a secret return of Christ [Origin of the
theory](#origin-of-the-theory). However, internal analysis of the gathering of
the elect in Matthew 24 suggests this event occurs after the Tribulation rather
than before [The gathering of the elect](#the-gathering-of-the-elect).
Furthermore, the Greek terms used in the "one taken and one left" passage may
refer to acceptance into position or rejection, a theme reinforced by the
subsequent parables of readiness [Like the days of
Noah](#like-the-days-of-noah).
