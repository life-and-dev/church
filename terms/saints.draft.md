# Saints

The term "saint" often brings to mind statues in cathedrals or historical
figures with halos. Many people associate sainthood with an official title
granted by a church to individuals of exceptional holiness. However, the
biblical use of the word tells a different story. This article explores the
original meaning of "saints" and how the term was applied to all believers in
the early church.

## Modern Ecclesiastical Definition

In contemporary religious contexts, a saint is typically seen as someone who
has reached a state of perfection. They may also be officially recognized after
death. The Cambridge Dictionary provides a common secular understanding of this
term.

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings: Saint Peter, St. Andrew's school, Saint Paul's Cathedral —
> [Cambridge Dictionary][1]

According to this definition, church leadership determines who receives this
title. This process of official recognition is known as canonization. This
practice began to be formalized in the 10th century. The first papal
canonization was Ulric of Augsburg in 993 AD. Later, in 1234 AD, the right to
canonize saints was reserved exclusively to the Holy See.

## The Biblical Greek Meaning

At the time the New Testament was written, the [Roman Catholic Church did not
exist yet][2] in its modern institutional form. Titles were not
handed out to [Christians][3]. The original Greek word translated as
"saint" is *hagios*. It carries a meaning quite different from an official
title of honor.

| Attribute | [Strong's Concordance][4] |
| :--- | :--- |
| Original Word | ἅγιος |
| Part of Speech | Adjective |
| Transliteration | hagios |
| Pronunciation | HA-gee-os |
| Phonetic Spelling | (hag'-ee-os) |
| Definition | [Holy, sacred][5] |
| Meaning | set apart by God, [holy][5] |

The word *hagios* literally means "holy one" or "set apart." It describes a
position or status before God. It does not refer to a degree of moral perfection
achieved by a human. No church has the right to choose what is holy. It is God
who determines what or whom are set apart as special to Him.

> You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy. — Leviticus 19:2 (ESV)

## Usage in the Early Church

In the New Testament, "saints" is almost always used in the plural. It refers
to the entire body of believers in a specific location. The apostles addressed
their letters to the "saints" in various cities. They did not single out an
elite group of exceptionally pious individuals.

For example, the Apostle Paul addressed the believers in Rome as "called to be
saints" (Romans 1:7 (ESV)). He used similar language for the church in Corinth.
He called them "those sanctified in Christ Jesus, called to be saints"
(1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)). This is striking because the Corinthian church
struggled with many moral and doctrinal issues. Yet, they were still called
saints because of their position in Christ.

Other examples include:

- The saints at Ephesus (Ephesians 1:1 (ESV))
- The saints in Christ Jesus who are at Philippi (Philippians 1:1 (ESV))
- The saints at Colossae (Colossians 1:2 (ESV))

Even the book of Acts describes the early believers this way. When Saul was
persecuting the church, he sought to do harm to the "saints" in Jerusalem
(Acts 9:13 (ESV)). Later, Peter visited the "saints" who lived in Lydda
(Acts 9:32 (ESV)).

## Conclusion

The biblical definition of a saint is any person who is set apart by God. This
differs from the later religious tradition of official canonization.

- A saint is fundamentally someone [set apart by God][6]
  rather than a person honored by a church.
- The New Testament applies the term to [all believers][7]
  within the community.
- The [modern definition][8] of a saint as a
  special title developed centuries after the apostolic era.
