# Saints

Many people recognize "Saints" as legendary figures with their names on
cathedrals or schools. Yet, the original meaning of this word in the first
century was far more inclusive. Discover how a term once used for every believer
became a prestigious title reserved for a select few.

## Modern definition

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "Saints" as:

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary][1]

According to this definition, church leadership determines who may receive this
special title. However, historical evidence suggests this institutional process
was not present in the earliest days of the church. At the time when the Gospel
of Matthew and the epistles of Paul were written, the Roman Catholic Church had
not yet emerged as a distinct institution. No such titles were handed out to
[Christians](christian.draft.md).

## Biblical meaning

The original Greek word translated as "saint" is *hagios*.

In the New Testament, this term refers to all believers rather than a select
group of exceptionally virtuous people. God is the one who determines who is set
apart for Him. For example, the apostle Paul addressed his letters to the
"saints" in various cities, referring to the entire community of believers.

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.
> — Romans 1:7 (ESV)
>
> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus,
> called to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the
> name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and ours.
> — 1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)

## Historical shift

The transition from the biblical use of "saints" to the modern ecclesiastical
title was a gradual process. In the first few centuries, the recognition of a
saint was often based on public acclaim or martyrdom. It was not until the 10th
century that the formal process of papal canonization began. The first recorded
instance of a pope officially canonizing a saint was Bishop Ulric of Augsburg in
993 AD. By 1234 AD, the right to canonize saints was reserved exclusively to the
Holy See.

Critics of the biblical view might argue that official canonization is necessary
to provide clear role models for the faithful. However, proponents of the
original meaning point to the scriptural precedent where the title applies to
every person "sanctified in Christ Jesus."

## Conclusion

The term "saints" has shifted from a universal label for all believers to a
formal title bestowed by church authority. This evolution is evident when
comparing the biblical use of [hagios](#biblical-meaning) with the [modern
definition](#modern-definition) of official canonization. History shows that the
formal process of [papal canonization](#historical-shift) was a later
development that redefined a once-common identity.
