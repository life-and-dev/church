# Saints

Have you ever wondered why some people are called "saints" while others are not?
The term often brings to mind stained-glass windows or ancient figures honored
with special titles. However, the original meaning of this word in the early
church might be quite different from how it is used today.

## Modern Definitions

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "Saints":

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/saint)

## Historical Context

However, at the time when the book of Matthew and the epistles of Paul were
written, [the Roman Catholic Church did not exist yet](../evolution.md) and no
titles were handed out to [Christians](christian.draft.md). According to
Cambridge's definition, the church leadership determines who or what may receive
this special "title."

## Biblical Usage

In the New Testament, the term "saints" (hagios) is used to refer to the
collective body of believers. For example, when Saul was persecuting the
church, Ananias mentioned how much evil he had done to the "saints at
Jerusalem" (Acts 9:13 (ESV)). Later, Peter visited the "saints who lived at
Lydda" (Acts 9:32 (ESV)).

The Apostle Paul frequently addressed his letters to all the believers in a
city as saints. He wrote to "all those in Rome who are loved by God and called
to be saints" (Romans 1:7 (ESV)) and to the church in Corinth as those "called
to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the name of
our Lord Jesus Christ" (1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)). This suggests that in the
early church, every believer was considered a saint because they were "set
apart" by God.

## Evolution of Canonization

The practice of official canonization by the church developed much later. The
formal process of papal canonization did not begin until the 10th century. For
instance, the first recorded papal canonization was that of Saint Ulric of
Augsburg in 993 AD. By 1234 AD, the right to canonize saints was reserved
exclusively to the Holy See. This shifted the definition from a shared
identity of all believers to a special status granted to a few by church
authority.

## Divine Selection

No church has the right to choose what is "holy". It is God who determines
what or whom are set apart (special) to Him.

## Conclusion

The term "saints" originally identified the
[collective body of New Testament believers](#biblical-usage) who were
[set apart by God](#the-greek-definition). While modern definitions focus on
[ecclesiastical titles and official honors](#modern-definitions), the early
church used the word to describe [every follower of Christ](#historical-context).
Over time, the [process of canonization](#evolution-of-canonization) shifted
the authority to designate saints to church leadership, moving away from the
[biblical principle of divine selection](#divine-selection).
