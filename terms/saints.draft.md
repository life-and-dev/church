# Saints

The concept of a "saint" evokes images of stained-glass windows and ancient
cathedrals. Many people recognize the title as an official honor bestowed upon
individuals who lived exceptionally holy lives. This understanding often aligns
with modern dictionary definitions that emphasize official ecclesiastical
recognition.

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings — [Cambridge
> Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/saint)

While this definition reflects current usage, the historical and biblical
origins of the term present a different perspective. At the time when the New
Testament was written, the institutional processes for handing out titles did
not exist.

## Biblical Origins of Hagios

The original Greek word translated as "saint" in the New Testament is *hagios*.
This term does not refer to a spiritual elite or a title granted by a committee.
Instead, it describes a status shared by all believers.

The term *hagios* emphasizes the idea of being "set apart" for God's use. In his
letters, the Apostle Paul frequently addressed his readers as saints, regardless
of their level of spiritual maturity. For instance, he wrote to the believers in
Rome as those "called to be saints" ([Romans 1:7 (ESV)][1]). He used the same
language for the church in Corinth, even while addressing their various internal
conflicts ([1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)][2]).

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. — Romans 1:7
> (ESV)

In the biblical context, holiness is not a rank to be achieved through human
effort. It is a position granted by God. He is the one who determines what or
whom is set apart as special to Him.

## Historical Evolution of Canonization

The transition from the biblical "set apart" status to the formal "title" of
saint occurred over several centuries. During the first few centuries of the
church, martyrs and exceptional figures were venerated locally without a
centralized process.

The formal process of papal canonization began to take shape in the 10th
century. Bishop Ulric of Augsburg became the first person known to have been
canonized by a pope when Pope John XV declared him a saint in 993 AD. This
marked a shift in authority from local bishops to the Holy See.

By 1234 AD, the Catholic Church reserved the right to canonize saints
exclusively for the Pope. This institutionalization transformed the term from a
common description of all Christians into a specific honor for a selected few.

## Addressing the Modern Practice

Critics of the biblical view might argue that the formal canonization process
serves an important role in providing role models for the faith. They believe
that honoring exceptional lives encourages the community and preserves the
history of the church.

However, a distinction remains between honoring history and redefining biblical
terminology. While recognizing the contributions of influential Christians is
valuable, the New Testament maintains that every believer is "set apart" by God.
This suggests that the status of a saint is an inherent part of the Christian
identity rather than a posthumous award.

## Summary of the Saint Identity

- The term "saint" originates from the Greek word *hagios*, which means "set
  apart" or "holy" [for God's use](#biblical-origins-of-hagios).
- In the New Testament, the title was applied to all believers as a description
  of their [standing before God](#biblical-origins-of-hagios).
- The formal process of canonization began in the [10th
  century](#historical-evolution-of-canonization) with Ulric of Augsburg.
- Centralized papal authority over the title was finalized in [1234
  AD](#historical-evolution-of-canonization).
- Every Christian is considered a saint in the biblical sense, as they are
  [called and set apart](#addressing-the-modern-practice) by God.
