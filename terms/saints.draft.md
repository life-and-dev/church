# Saints

Have you ever wondered what it truly means to be a "saint"? While many
associate the term with historical figures officially honored by a church, the
original biblical language suggests a much broader meaning. This article
explores the definition of "saints" and how it was used in the earliest
Christian writings.

## Modern Definitions

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "saints" as:

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings — [Cambridge Dictionary][1]

According to this definition, the church leadership determines who receives
this special title. Examples include Saint Peter, St. Andrew's School, and
Saint Paul's Cathedral.

## Historical Context

At the time when the book of Matthew and the epistles of Paul were written, the
institutional church as we know it today [did not exist yet][2].
No official titles were handed out to [Christians](christian.md). The original
Greek word translated as "saint" is *hagios*.

## Biblical Usage

The term *hagios* describes something or someone set apart by God. It is not a
right reserved for a church to choose what is "holy." It is God who determines
what or whom are special to Him. In the New Testament, the term was frequently
used to address all believers in a particular location.

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.
> >
> — Romans 1:7 (ESV)
> >
> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus,
> called to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the
> name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and ours
> >
> — 1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)
> >
> Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, To all the saints in Christ Jesus
> who are at Philippi, with the overseers and deacons
> >
> — Philippians 1:1 (ESV)
> >
> To the saints and faithful brothers in Christ at Colossae: Grace to you and
> peace from God our Father. — Colossians 1:2 (ESV)

Peter also used the term when traveling among believers.

> Now as Peter went here and there among them all, he came down also to the
> saints who lived at Lydda. — Acts 9:32 (ESV)

## Apostolic Authority vs. Universal Holiness

Some might argue that the church has the authority to officially recognize and
canonize individuals who have lived lives of exceptional holiness. This process
is intended to provide role models for the faithful. However, the New Testament
usage suggests that holiness is not a status achieved through exceptional
works. Instead, it is a state of being "set apart" by God's grace. While the
church may honor certain individuals, the biblical term *hagios* was
originally applied to all who were called by God to be part of His people.

## Conclusion

The term "saint" originates from the Greek word *hagios*, meaning [set apart by
God](#historical-context). In the early church, this [title was applied to all
believers](#biblical-usage) rather than being an [official honor bestowed by a
church institution](#modern-definitions).
