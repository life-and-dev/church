# Saints

Have you ever wondered if the title of "saint" is reserved only for a select
few who have lived extraordinary lives of virtue? While many people associate
the term with official church honors and stained-glass windows, the original
biblical context reveals a much broader and more inclusive meaning for all
believers.

## Modern Definitions

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "Saints" as:

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary][1]

According to this definition, church leadership determines who receives this
special title. This practice often involves a formal process of canonization.
The names of these individuals are frequently used to name schools, hospitals,
and cathedrals.

## Historical Context

The historical record indicates that the modern practice of official
canonization did not exist during the first century. When the book of Matthew
and the epistles of Paul were written, the Roman Catholic Church had not yet
been established. No formal titles were handed out to
[Christians](christian.md).

Historical scholarship confirms that the formal process of papal canonization
only began in the 10th century. Bishop Ulric of Augsburg was the first person to
be officially canonized by a pope in 993 AD. Later, in 1234 AD, the Holy See
reserved the exclusive right to canonize saints.

## Biblical Meaning

The New Testament was originally written in Greek. The word translated as
"saint" is *hagios*. This term has a specific meaning that differs from modern
ecclesiastical usage.

The biblical definition of a saint is someone who is set apart by God. It is
not a title granted by a human institution. Instead, it reflects a status
granted by God to those who follow Him. This concept of being "set apart" is
deeply rooted in the Old Testament as well.

> Oh, fear the LORD, you his saints, for those who fear him have no
> lack! — Psalm 34:9 (ESV)

## All Believers as Saints

In the New Testament, the term "saints" refers to all believers. It is not
reserved for a posthumous honor. For example, the Apostle Paul addressed his
letters to the entire congregation as saints.

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace
> to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. — Romans 1:7 (ESV)

He also addressed the church in Corinth and Ephesus in a similar manner.

> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ
> Jesus, called to be saints together with all those who in every place
> call upon the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and
> ours: — 1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)
>
> Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God, To the saints who
> are in Ephesus, and are faithful in Christ Jesus: — Ephesians 1:1 (ESV)

These verses demonstrate that sainthood was considered the common identity of
all Christians. It was not a reward for "heroic virtue" as defined by later
church councils.

## Addressing Critiques

Some may argue that the church needs a way to recognize exemplary lives to
provide models for the faithful. They might suggest that the term evolved to
distinguish those in heaven from those on earth.

However, critics of official canonization point out that the Bible describes
all believers as "set apart." They argue that creating a separate class of
"Saints" can lead to the veneration of humans. This practice might distract from
the worship of God. The biblical text emphasizes that God alone determines what
or whom is holy.

## Conclusion

The biblical term [saints](#biblical-meaning) refers to all believers who are
set apart by God. This stands in contrast to [modern
definitions](#modern-definitions) that treat it as an official title. Historical
evidence shows that [formal canonization](#historical-context) was a later
development in church history. Therefore, according to the [New
Testament](#all-believers-as-saints), every person who follows Christ is
considered a saint in the eyes of God.
