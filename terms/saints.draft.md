# Saints

Have you ever wondered what it truly means to be a "saint"? While modern culture
often associates the term with stained-glass windows and official church
decrees, the original biblical meaning was quite different. This article
explores the linguistic roots and historical evolution of the word, revealing a
designation that was once applied to every believer.

## Modern Definitions

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "Saints":

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/saint)

In modern usage, the term often refers to an official title bestowed by a church
hierarchy. However, historical records suggest that during the first century,
[the Roman Catholic Church did not exist
yet](../evolution/1st-c-christians.md#followers-of-the-way). At that time, no
formal titles were handed out to [Christians](christian.md). According to the
modern definition, church leadership determines who receives this special
recognition.

## Historical Development

The transition from the biblical use of "saints" to the formalized process of
canonization was a gradual one. In the early church, the term was used for all
members of the Christian community. Over time, particularly after the era of the
martyrs, certain individuals were singled out for special veneration. It was not
until around the 10th century that the [process of
canonization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonization) began to be reserved
to the papacy, leading to the structured system observed today.

## Biblical Roots

The original Greek word translated as "saint" is *hagios*. This term has a
broader meaning than the modern ecclesiastical title.

## Divine Designation

From a biblical perspective, the designation of "holy" is determined by God
rather than a church institution. In the New Testament, the term "saints" is
frequently used to address all believers in a particular location, regardless of
their level of "heroic virtue."

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. — Romans 1:7
> (ESV)
>
> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus,
> called to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the
> name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and ours: — 1 Corinthians 1:2
> (ESV)
>
> Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus, To all the saints in Christ Jesus
> who are at Philippi, with the overseers and deacons: — Philippians 1:1 (ESV)

These passages suggest that the status of a saint is a result of being "called"
or "sanctified in Christ Jesus," implying that God determines who is set apart
as special to Him.

## Conclusion

In summary, while [modern definitions](#modern-definitions) focus on official
honors bestowed by church hierarchies, the [biblical roots](#biblical-roots) of
the term reveal a different meaning. [Historical
development](#historical-development) shows that the status once held by all
believers was gradually transformed into a formal title. Ultimately, the
[divine designation](#divine-designation) of holiness rests with God rather
than any human institution.
