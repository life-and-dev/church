# The Biblical Meaning of Saints

The concept of a "saint" has shifted significantly over the past two millennia.
Many people today associate the term with stained-glass windows or individuals
who performed miracles and lived exceptionally holy lives. This modern
understanding suggests that sainthood is a high honor bestowed by church
authorities after a long process. However, the earliest followers of Jesus used
the term in a much broader and more personal way. Exploring the original meaning
of this word reveals a different perspective on who is considered "holy" in the
eyes of God.

## Modern definition

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "Saints":

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary][1]

[1]: https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/saint

However, at the time when the book of Matthew and the epistles of Paul were
written, the Roman Catholic Church did not exist yet. No titles were handed out
to [Christians](christian.draft.md). According to Cambridge's definition, the
church leadership determines whom or what may receive this special "title".

## Biblical definition

The original Greek word that was translated to "saint" in the New Testament is
*hagios*. It is used over 200 times and does not refer to a person who has
achieved a certain level of perfection. Instead, it describes someone who has
been set apart for God's purposes.

No church has the right to choose what is "holy". It is God who determines what
or whom are set apart (special) to Him.

## Biblical usage

In the letters written by the apostle Paul, he repeatedly addresses the entire
congregation as "saints". This indicates that the term applied to all believers
in the local church, not just a selected few.

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.
> — Romans 1:7 (ESV)

Paul uses similar language when writing to the Corinthians:

> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus,
> called to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the
> name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and ours.
> — 1 Corinthians 1:2 (ESV)

He also addresses the Ephesians in the same way:

> Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God, To the saints who are in
> Ephesus, and are faithful in Christ Jesus.
> — Ephesians 1:1 (ESV)

These examples show that "saints" was a common designation for all members of
the early Christian communities. They were considered "set apart" because of
their faith in Christ, not because of an official decree from a religious
institution.

## Historical evolution

The process of officially canonizing individuals as saints developed much later
in church history. For the first millennium of Christianity, the recognition of
saints was often a local and informal process. The first recorded papal
canonization occurred in 993 AD when Pope John XV declared Ulric of Augsburg to
be a saint.

The centralization of this process continued until 1234 AD. During that year,
Pope Gregory IX reserved the exclusive right to canonize saints to the Holy See.
This shifted the definition of "saint" from a common identity shared by all
believers to a prestigious title awarded by the church hierarchy. This evolution
reflects a broader trend where religious authorities began to regulate and
formalize aspects of faith that were previously more decentralized.

## Perspectives on canonization

Some may argue that the official canonization process serves an important
purpose. It provides a way for the church to recognize and honor individuals who
have demonstrated exceptional faith and virtue. By setting these individuals
apart, the church offers role models for other believers to follow.

However, a critique of this view suggests that it creates a two-tiered system of
Christianity. It implies that only a small number of people can truly be
considered "holy" or "set apart". This stands in contrast to the New Testament
view where every believer is called a saint. The biblical perspective
emphasizes that holiness is not based on human achievement but on being called
and set apart by God.

## Conclusion

The term "saint" originally identified every person who followed Jesus. While
modern language often treats it as an [official title](#modern-definition) given
by a church, the Bible defines it as being [set apart](#biblical-definition) by
God. New Testament writings consistently address [all
believers](#biblical-usage) as saints. This identity shifted over time due to
[historical changes](#historical-evolution) in church authority. Ultimately,
biblical sainthood is about [God's calling](#perspectives-on-canonization)
rather than human recognition.
