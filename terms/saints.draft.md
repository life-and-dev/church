# Saints

Have you ever wondered why some people are called "saints" while others are
not? Modern culture and church traditions often present sainthood as an official
title bestowed upon a select few for their exceptional piety. However, the
origin of this term reveals a much broader and more inclusive reality that
applies to every believer.

## Modern Definition

The Cambridge Dictionary defines "saints" as follows:

> (the title given to) a person who has received an official honor from the
> Christian, especially the Roman Catholic, Church for having lived in a good
> and holy way. The names of saints are sometimes used to name places and
> buildings:
>
> * Saint Peter
> * St. Andrew's school
> * Saint Paul's Cathedral
>
> — [Cambridge Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/saint)

According to this definition, the church leadership determines who may receive
this special title. This practice is common in many modern denominations today.

## Biblical Origin

At the time when the New Testament was written, no official titles were handed
out to followers of Jesus. These
[early Christians](../evolution/1st-c-christians.md) identified themselves
primarily by their shared faith and commitment to "the Way." The original Greek
word that was translated as "saint" is *hagios*.

No church has the right to choose what is holy. It is God who determines what
or whom are set apart as special to Him. The term *hagios* refers to being
different from the world because one is like the Lord.

Scripture uses this word to describe all believers. For example, Peter visited
the saints who were living in a specific town.

> Now as Peter went here and there among them all, he came down also to the
> saints who lived at Lydda. — Acts 9:32 (ESV)

Paul also addressed his letters to all the believers in a city as saints. He
did not reserve the term for a small group of leaders.

> To all those in Rome who are loved by God and called to be saints: Grace to
> you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ. — Romans 1:7
> (ESV)
>
> To the church of God that is in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus,
> called to be saints together with all those who in every place call upon the
> name of our Lord Jesus Christ, both their Lord and ours: — 1 Corinthians 1:2
> (ESV)

The term was even used as a general greeting for every individual believer.

> Greet every saint in Christ Jesus. The brothers who are with me greet you. —
> Philippians 4:21 (ESV)

## Evolution of Sainthood

The practice of officially canonizing individuals developed centuries after the
first century. The formal process of papal canonization began in the 10th
century. Pope John XV performed the first papal canonization in 993 AD for Ulric
of Augsburg. Before this time, local bishops or popular acclaim often recognized
saints.

The rights to canonize saints were eventually reserved exclusively to the Holy
See in 1234 AD. This shift moved the designation of sainthood from a shared
identity among all believers to a regulated honor granted by church authorities.

Critiques might argue that the church needs a way to recognize exceptional
examples of faith to inspire others. While such recognition can be valuable, it
differs from the original biblical application of the term. The New Testament
writers consistently used "saints" to refer to the entire community of believers
rather than a select few.

## Conclusion

The biblical term for saints describes all people whom God has set apart for
Himself [Biblical Origin](#biblical-origin). This stands in contrast to the
later ecclesiastical practice of bestowing an official title upon deceased
individuals [Evolution of Sainthood](#evolution-of-sainthood). Modern
dictionaries primarily focus on these official church honors [Modern
Definition](#modern-definition).
