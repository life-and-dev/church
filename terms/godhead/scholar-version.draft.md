# Godhead According to Scholars

Have you ever wondered how scholars attempt to reconcile the oneness of God with
the different members mentioned in the Bible? This article explores various
models used to explain the Godhead and examines the theological questions they
raise.

According to scholars, the Godhead has three members that each fulfill a
different function (Matthew 28:19 (ESV)). This leads to the question of what
these members of God actually are.

## Gods or One God?

Some argue that each member of the Godhead is a God by himself. They suggest
that these members are also one unified God or [Godhead](../godhead.md).

When the Bible says there is only one "God", they often interpret this to mean
the "Trinity". This word does not exist in the Bible. There are also no
scriptures that explain or prove the "Trinity" directly. The doctrine is built
upon various different scriptures scattered through the Bible. It relies on
complex interpretations and assumptions that must be correlated together to
support the theology.

## Incarnations

Some argue that God was first the Father. They suggest He later incarnated into
the Son. Finally, they believe He ascended into the invisible Holy Spirit to be
in our presence.

However, the Father and the Son interacted with each other as distinct
individuals. Furthermore, the Holy Spirit is not Jesus. This is because Jesus
received the Holy Spirit while he was a human among humans.

If God really incarnated or limited Himself to a human form, it raises the
question of who maintained the universe while Jesus was in his limited human
form.

Another potential flaw with this reasoning is the implication for humanity. If
God was Jesus and Jesus became truly human without another superior God, and he
was able to reincarnate back into a divine God by himself, then it could be
argued that humans should also be able to incarnate into competing gods by
themselves.

## Modes and Modalism

Some argue that God has different modes. In this view, He acts like a Father
when someone needs a father. He acts like the Son when someone needs a friend.
He acts like the Holy Spirit when someone needs spiritual power.

However, the Father and the Son interacted with each other as distinct
individuals. This suggests a relationship between two persons rather than a
single person changing roles.

## The Mystery View

Some argue that it is pointless to understand how the members relate to each
other. They suggest we should accept it as a [mystery](../mystery.md) that we
will never be able to solve.

While this approach avoids difficult questions, it can also discourage the
search for truth and deeper understanding.

## Parts and Partialism

Some argue that God has different parts, similar to how a person has a body,
soul, and spirit. Even human bodies have different parts like hands and feet.
This view suggests God has different components like the Father, Son, and Holy
Spirit.

However, in the case of Jesus, these parts interact with each other as if they
are different personalities. They can even be separated.

> And about the ninth hour Jesus cried out with a loud voice, saying, “Eli, Eli,
> lema sabachthani?” that is, “My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?” —
> Matthew 27:46 (ESV)

If they were different parts, this would imply that God was divided.

Critiques of this "divided God" argument suggest that Jesus' cry on the cross
was a relational separation rather than an ontological one. Some theologians
argue that Jesus was quoting Psalm 22 to point towards his eventual victory.
This would mean that the unity of God's essence remained intact even while the
Son experienced the human feeling of abandonment.

## Roles and Relationships

Some argue that the same person could have different roles, such as being a
father, son, husband, and friend at the same time. This view suggests God could
also have different roles like Father, Son, and Spirit.

However, Jesus' roles interact with each other as distinct individuals. Some of
these roles also seem incompatible. For example, some claims suggest he is:

* His own Father and also his own Son.
* Our God and also like one of us.
* The greatest God and also a servant of God.
* The Creator and also the firstborn.
* The one being worshipped and also a sacrifice.
* The one worthy of all prayer and also a mediator who prays.

## Shape-shifting

Some argue that the Almighty God can appear in any form He wishes. Examples
include a burning bush, a rock, bread, or water. Therefore, they suggest it is
no problem for Him to appear as a Heavenly Father, a human Son, and the Holy
Spirit.

However, the disciples often witnessed the Father and the Son interacting with
each other as distinct individuals.

## Summary of Models

Scholarly interpretations of the Godhead attempt to explain the relationship
between the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit through various models. These include
[viewing them as a Trinity](#gods-or-one-god), [different
incarnations](#incarnations), or [distinct modes of a single
God](#modes-and-modalism). Some see the Godhead as a [mystery beyond human
understanding](#the-mystery-view), while others propose
[parts](#parts-and-partialism), [roles](#roles-and-relationships), or
[different forms](#shape-shifting). Each of these models faces challenges when
compared with the biblical accounts of the distinct interactions between the
Father and the Son.

Instead of crafting clever theologies which try to explain the Godhead in such a
way to fit our doctrines, I would rather recommend to [study the words of
Jesus](jesus-version.md).
