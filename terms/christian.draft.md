# Christian

The label "Christian" is globally recognized today as an identifier for
billions of people. However, this term appears only three times in the entire
New Testament. By examining its biblical origins and the names used by early
followers of Jesus, one can uncover a complex history. This article explores
whether the title was originally an honor, an insult, or something else
entirely.

## Common Definitions

According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians):

> Christians (/ˈkrɪstʃən, -tiən/) are people who follow or adhere to
> Christianity, a monotheistic Abrahamic religion based on the life and
> teachings of Jesus Christ. The words Christ and Christian derive from the
> Koine Greek title Christós (Χριστός), a translation of the Biblical Hebrew
> term mashiach (מָשִׁיחַ) (usually rendered as messiah in English). While
> there are diverse interpretations of Christianity which sometimes conflict,
> they are united in believing that Jesus has a unique significance.
> — Wikipedia

Likewise, [the Cambridge Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/christian)
defines a "Christian" as:

> someone who believes in and follows the teachings of Jesus Christ
> — Cambridge Dictionary

## Biblical Alternatives

Paul does not refer to his followers as "Christian." Instead, he uses the term
"[saints](https://biblehub.com/greek/40.htm)" (Romans 1:7). This term refers to
people who are set apart for God.

So why are people called "Christians"?

Some quote 1 Peter 4:16 out of context to prove that being called a "Christian"
is a privilege that glorifies God.

## Historical Insult

However, the preceding verses suggest that the name was originally an insult.
Peter encouraged his followers not to be ashamed of being called a "Christian."
He urged them to use the opportunity to glorify God in that name.

> Beloved, do not be surprised at the fiery trial when it comes upon you to
> test you, as though something strange were happening to you. But rejoice
> insofar as you share Christ’s sufferings, that you may also rejoice and be
> glad when his glory is revealed. If **you are insulted for the name of
> Christ**, you are blessed, because the Spirit of glory and of God rests upon
> you. But let none of you suffer as a murderer or a thief or an evildoer or
> as a meddler. Yet if anyone suffers **as a Christian, let him not be
> ashamed, but let him glorify God in that name**. — 1 Peter 4:12-16 (ESV)

## Origins in Antioch

According to Acts 11:26, the term "Christian" originated in Antioch:

> And in Antioch the disciples were first called Christians.
> — Acts 11:26 (ESV)

The text does not state that the disciples chose this name for themselves.

## Royal Mockery

The only other biblical recording of someone being called a "Christian" occurs
during a legal defense:

> And as he was saying these things in his defense, Festus said with a loud
> voice, “Paul, **you are out of your mind**; your great learning is driving
> you out of your mind.” But Paul said, “I am not out of my mind, most
> excellent Festus, but I am speaking true and rational words. For the king
> knows about these things, and to him I speak boldly. For I am persuaded that
> none of these things has escaped his notice, for this has not been done in a
> corner.” “King Agrippa, do you believe the prophets? **I know that you
> believe**.” And Agrippa said to Paul, “**In a short time would you persuade
> me to be a Christian?**” — Acts 26:24-28 (ESV)

Paul challenged [Herod Agrippa II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herod_Agrippa_II)
to believe. In response, King Agrippa publicly denied Paul's faith. He mocked
Paul in front of the council to defend his own reputation. King Agrippa did
not honor Paul. It is likely that he used a label intended to be offensive.

If being called a "Christian" was an honor or a core identity for Paul, he
likely would have used the term to address his followers. However, he never
mentions the word in any of his letters. This absence is notable because he
was certainly familiar with the name.

## Other Designations

Another offensive name used to describe Paul's followers was "Nazarenes":

> And when he had been summoned, Tertullus began to accuse him, saying:
> “Since through you we enjoy much peace, and since by your foresight, most
> excellent Felix, reforms are being made for this nation, in every way and
> everywhere we accept this with all gratitude. But, to detain you no further,
> I beg you in your kindness to hear us briefly. For we have found this man a
> plague, one who stirs up riots among all the Jews throughout the world and
> is a ringleader of **the sect of the Nazarenes**.” — Acts 24:2-5 (ESV)

## Modern Context

> According to a 2011 Pew Research Center survey, there were 2.2 billion
> Christians around the world in 2010. ... Christians make up the majority of
> the population in 158 countries and territories. 280 million Christians live
> as a minority. About half of all Christians worldwide are Catholic, while
> more than a third are Protestant (37%). Orthodox communions comprise 12% of
> the world's Christians. Other Christian groups make up the remainder.
> — Wikipedia

Today, various religions, denominations, and sects are all labeled "Christian."
Even groups like the Crusaders fall under this name. This can be very
confusing. Depending on the audience, the label might be used to someone's
benefit. However, it can also be a hindrance when people harbor negative
feelings toward the name due to historical misunderstandings.

The Bible does not state that a person must convert to a "Christian" to be
saved.

## The Way

Instead, the original disciples often identified themselves as "the Way."
For example:

> But Saul, still breathing threats and murder **against the disciples of the
> Lord**, went to the high priest and asked him for letters to the synagogues
> at Damascus, so that if he found any belonging to
> **the [Way](https://biblehub.com/greek/3598.htm)**, men or women, he might
> bring them bound to Jerusalem. — Acts 9:1-2 (ESV)

This name was potentially a shortened form of "servants of the Most High God,
who proclaim to you the way of salvation" (Acts 16:17). Technically, the Way
refers to the proclamation of the way to salvation. This is known today as
the gospel.

Another example:

> About that time there arose no little disturbance concerning **the Way**.
> — Acts 19:23 (ESV)

The author describes a disturbance caused by the alternative teachings of the
Way. These teachings stood in contrast with other religions. The Ephesians
were not concerned about the presence of a new sect. Instead, they worried
that the teaching of the Way conflicted with their business interests. Their
business involved selling idols rather than destroying them.

It also would have made sense to call the disciples who proclaimed the Way by
that same name. Paul testified and summarized the core beliefs of the Way:

> And when the governor had nodded to him to speak, Paul replied:... But this
> I confess to you, that according to **the Way, which they call a sect**, I
> worship the God of our fathers, believing everything laid down by the Law
> and written in the Prophets, having a hope in God which these men
> themselves accept, that there will be a resurrection of both the just and
> the unjust. — Acts 24:14-15 (ESV)

and

> But Felix, having a rather accurate knowledge of **the Way**, put them off,
> saying, “When Lysias the tribune comes down, I will decide your case.”
> — Acts 24:22 (ESV)

This name aligns with the words of Jesus, who originally called himself the
Way:

> “I am **the way**, and the truth, and the life. — John 14:6 (ESV)

## Divine Calling

Some scholars argue that the name "Christian" was a divine appointment. They
point to the Greek word *chrematizo* used in Acts 11:26. This word often
describes a divine warning or revelation in other parts of the New Testament.
For example, it is used when the wise men were warned in a dream. These
scholars suggest that God Himself gave the name to the disciples.

However, *chrematizo* can also refer to taking a name from one's public
business or affairs. This would align with the view that the name was an
external label given by the people of Antioch. Regardless of its origin, the
apostles continued to use other terms like "saints" or "the Way" as their
primary identifiers.

## Conclusion

The term "Christian" is rarely used in the New Testament and likely originated
as an external label or insult. [Common Definitions](#common-definitions)
[Historical Insult](#historical-insult) Early followers of Jesus preferred to
identify themselves as "saints" or members of "the Way."

argue the name had a divine origin, it was not the primary identity used by the

the term has become broad and sometimes confusing due to the diversity of
