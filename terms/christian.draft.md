# Christian

The term "Christian" is globally recognized today as the primary designation for
followers of Jesus. However, this name was not the original identity of the
early disciples. This article explores the historical emergence of the term and
the alternative names used by the first believers. It examines whether the label
was initially an honor or an insult.

## External Definitions

According to [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians):

> Christians (/ˈkrɪstʃən, -tiən/) are people who follow or adhere to
> Christianity, a monotheistic Abrahamic religion based on the life and
> teachings of Jesus Christ. The words Christ and Christian derive from the
> Koine Greek title Christós (Χριστός), a translation of the Biblical Hebrew
> term mashiach (מָשִׁיחַ) (usually rendered as messiah in English). While there
> are diverse interpretations of Christianity which sometimes conflict, they are
> united in believing that Jesus has a unique significance. — Wikipedia

Likewise, [the Cambridge
Dictionary](https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/english/christian)
defines a "Christian" as:

> someone who believes in and follows the teachings of Jesus
> [Christ](../evolution/1st-c-jesus.md) — Cambridge Dictionary

## Biblical Designations

The apostle Paul does not use the term "Christian" to describe his followers. He
refers to them as "[saints](https://biblehub.com/greek/40.htm)" (Romans 1:7
(ESV)). This term describes people who are set apart for God.

## Origin in Antioch

The book of Acts records that the name originated in the city of Antioch.

> And in Antioch the disciples were first called Christians. — Acts 11:26 (ESV)

The text does not state that the disciples chose this name for themselves. The
Greek word for "called" in this verse is *chrematizo*. This word often signifies
a divine communication or warning in other parts of the New Testament. Some
theological perspectives suggest that the name was a divine appointment. Other
scholars argue that the passive voice indicates an external label given by the
local population. This label would distinguish the movement from traditional
Judaism and other religions.

## Suffering and Insult

Some interpretations suggest that 1 Peter 4:16 presents the name "Christian" as
a privilege. This view claims that the name glorifies God. However, the
preceding verses suggest the term may have been [an
insult](https://www.truthrightlydivided.com/why-are-we-called-christians/).
Peter encouraged his followers not to be ashamed. He urged them to glorify God
despite the label.

> Beloved, do not be surprised at the fiery trial when it comes upon you to test
> you, as though something strange were happening to you. But rejoice insofar as
> you share Christ’s sufferings, that you may also rejoice and be glad when his
> glory is revealed. If you are insulted for the name of Christ, you are
> blessed, because the Spirit of glory and of God rests upon you. But let none
> of you suffer as a murderer or a thief or an evildoer or as a meddler. Yet if
> anyone suffers as a Christian, let him not be ashamed, but let him glorify God
> in that name. — 1 Peter 4:12-16 (ESV)

The use of the term in legal contexts also suggests it was not a
self-designation. King Agrippa used the term during Paul's defense.

> And Agrippa said to Paul, “In a short time would you persuade me to be a
> Christian?” — Acts 26:28 (ESV)

Paul challenged [Herod Agrippa
II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herod_Agrippa_II) to believe. King Agrippa
responded by questioning Paul's attempt to convert him. The context suggests
that Agrippa may have been mocking Paul publicly. This would protect his own
reputation before the council. Agrippa did not honor Paul with the name.
Instead, the term served to label Paul with a name that might be offensive or
dismissive. Paul never used the word "Christian" in any of his letters. He was
familiar with the name but preferred other terms.

Another derogatory name used for the movement was "the sect of the Nazarenes."

> For we have found this man a plague, one who stirs up riots among all the Jews
> throughout the world and is a ringleader of the sect of the Nazarenes. — Acts
> 24:5 (ESV)

## Modern Usage

The term has evolved significantly over two millennia.

> According to a 2011 Pew Research Center survey, there were 2.2 billion
> Christians around the world in 2010. Christians make up the majority of the
> population in 158 countries and territories. 280 million Christians live as a
> minority. About half of all Christians worldwide are Catholic, while more than
> a third are Protestant (37%). Orthodox communions comprise 12% of the world's
> Christians. Other Christian groups make up the remainder. — Wikipedia

Today various religions and denominations use the label. This includes groups
with conflicting beliefs. The broad use of the term can lead to confusion.
Depending on the audience, the label can be beneficial or a hindrance. Some
people may have negative associations with the name based on historical events.

## The Way

There is no biblical requirement to convert to a "Christian" to be saved.
Instead, the original disciples often identified themselves as "the Way."

> But Saul, still breathing threats and murder against the disciples of the
> Lord, went to the high priest and asked him for letters to the synagogues at
> Damascus, so that if he found any belonging to the
> [Way](https://biblehub.com/greek/3598.htm), men or women, he might bring them
> bound to Jerusalem. — Acts 9:1-2 (ESV)

The name may have been a shortened form of "the way of salvation" (Acts 16:17
(ESV)). This concept is now commonly known as "the gospel." The presence of "the
Way" often caused local disturbances.

> About that time there arose no little disturbance concerning the Way. — Acts
> 19:23 (ESV)

In Ephesus, the teachings of the Way conflicted with local business interests.
These interests involved the sale of idols. Paul eventually testified about his
beliefs while identifying with this name.

> But this I confess to you, that according to the Way, which they call a sect,
> I worship the God of our fathers, believing everything laid down by the Law
> and written in the Prophets, having a hope in God which these men themselves
> accept, that there will be a resurrection of both the just and the unjust. —
> Acts 24:14-15 (ESV)

Other Roman officials were also familiar with this designation. Felix is
described as having "a rather accurate knowledge of the Way" (Acts 24:22 (ESV)).
This identity aligns with the words of Jesus.

> Jesus said to him, “I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to
> the Father except through me. — John 14:6 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The term "Christian" emerged as a prominent label for followers of Jesus, though
it was not their original identity ([Biblical
Designations](#biblical-designations)). The name first appeared in Antioch,
where it may have served as either an external designation or a divine
appointment ([Origin in Antioch](#origin-in-antioch)). While the term was often
used as an insult in legal and social contexts, believers were encouraged to
bear it without shame ([Suffering and Insult](#suffering-and-insult)). Today the
label encompasses a vast and diverse global population ([Modern
Usage](#modern-usage)). However, the earliest disciples primarily identified
themselves as "saints" or members of "the Way" ([The Way](#the-way)).
