# The Feast of Weeks: From Harvest to Pentecost

The Feast of Weeks, also known as Shavuot or Pentecost, stands as a central
pillar in the biblical calendar. This festival bridges the gap between the
physical harvest and spiritual fulfillment, inviting believers to explore the
deep connections between ancient agriculture and divine appointments.

## The Debate on Biblical Feasts

A common question in modern theology is whether the biblical feasts are still
applicable to believers today. Historical evidence shows that the apostles
continued to observe these days long after the resurrection. For instance, the
apostle Paul hastened to be in Jerusalem for the day of Pentecost (Acts 20:16
(ESV)). Furthermore, the New Testament encourages the continued celebration of
these festivals in a new light, as seen when Paul instructs believers to
celebrate the festival with the "unleavened bread of sincerity and truth" (1
Corinthians 5:7-8 (ESV)).

However, some scriptures are often used to argue against the continued
observance of these days. Paul warned the Galatians about returning to "weak and
worthless elementary principles" by observing days and months and seasons and
years (Galatians 4:8-10 (ESV)). Additionally, Colossians 2:13-17 (ESV) mentions
that God has forgiven our trespasses:

> ...by canceling the record of debt [cheirographon] that stood against us with
> its legal demands. This he set aside, nailing it to the cross. He disarmed the
> rulers and authorities and put them to open shame, by triumphing over them in
> him. Therefore let no one pass judgment on you in questions of food and drink,
> or with regard to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath.
> — Colossians 2:14-16 (ESV)

The "handwriting of requirements" or "record of debt" (Greek: *cheirographon*)
refers to the certificate of debt incurred by sin, rather than the feasts
themselves. The feasts are described as a "shadow of things to come," with the
"substance" belonging to Christ (Colossians 2:17 (ESV)). This suggests that the
manner of observance has changed from a burdensome obligation to a celebration
of Christ's presence. Keeping a feast without Christ is likened to slavery, but
observing it to honor Him brings a sense of divine blessing and presence
(Revelation 3:20 (ESV)).

## Old Testament Origins of Shavuot

The Feast of Weeks, known in Hebrew as *Shavuot* (meaning "weeks"), is also
called the Feast of Harvest or Pentecost (meaning "fiftieth"). It marks the
second of the three great annual pilgrimage feasts.

The Torah describes Shavuot as a time to celebrate "the Feast of Harvest, of the
firstfruits of your labor, of what you sow in the field" (Exodus 23:16 (ESV)). It
served as a reminder of the transition from slavery in Egypt to the abundance of
the Promised Land (Deuteronomy 16:12 (ESV)). The commandment was established as
a "statute forever throughout your generations in all your dwellings" (Leviticus
23:14 (ESV)).

## Instructions for Observation

The timing of the Feast of Weeks is calculated from the day of the offering of
the Firstfruits. Believers were to count seven full weeks from the day after the
Sabbath of the Passover week, totaling fifty days (Leviticus 23:15-16 (ESV)).

On the fiftieth day, several specific actions were commanded:

* A proclamation of the feast was to be made.
* The community was to gather for a holy convocation or sacred ceremony.
* No laborious work was permitted on this day.
* It was to be observed as a perpetual statute in all dwelling places (Leviticus
  23:21 (ESV)).

The feast also emphasized care for the marginalized. When reaping the harvest,
farmers were instructed not to reap to the very edges of their fields or gather
the gleanings. Instead, these leftovers were to be reserved for the poor and the
sojourner (Leviticus 23:22 (ESV)).

## The Shadow and the Substance

In the New Testament, the Feast of Weeks found its ultimate fulfillment. On the
day of Pentecost, the Holy Spirit descended upon the disciples. This event
marked a new kind of harvest. It was a harvest of souls (Acts 2:1-4 (ESV)).
This event transformed the "shadow" of the Old Testament festival into the
"substance" found in Christ. Just as the physical harvest sustained the body,
the spiritual harvest at Pentecost began the sustenance of the global Church.

The apostles' continued observance, such as Paul's desire to reach Jerusalem by
Pentecost (Acts 20:16 (ESV)) and his stay in Ephesus until the same feast (1
Corinthians 16:8 (ESV)), demonstrates that the day remained a significant marker
for the early Church. It shifted from an agricultural ritual to a spiritual
commemoration of the Spirit's arrival.

## Defense of Continued Observance

Critiques often argue that the feasts were part of the Old Covenant and are no
longer relevant for Christians. However, the New Testament does not explicitly
abolish the festivals but rather reinterprets them. Paul's instructions to
"celebrate the festival" in 1 Corinthians 5:8 (ESV) suggest an ongoing practice,
albeit focused on "sincerity and truth" rather than legalistic ritual.

Furthermore, the inclusion of all people in God's plan was foretold in the Old
Testament. The LORD promised to bring foreigners who join themselves to the LORD
to His "holy mountain" and make them joyful in His "house of prayer" (Isaiah
56:6-7 (ESV)). Jesus Himself declared that the "Sabbath was made for man,"
affirming that these divine appointments are intended for human benefit and are
under His authority as the Lord even of the Sabbath (Mark 2:27-28 (ESV)).

## Conclusion

The Feast of Weeks remains a profound testament to God's timing and provision.
It bridges the [Old Testament origins](#old-testament-origins-of-shavuot) of a
physical harvest with the [New Testament fulfillment](#the-shadow-and-the-substance)
at Pentecost. By understanding the [instructions for
observation](#instructions-for-observation), believers can appreciate how the
feast serves as a shadow of the greater reality found in Christ. While [the
debate on its application](#the-debate-on-biblical-feasts) continues, the
festival consistently points to the abundance of God's grace and the ongoing
harvest of His kingdom.
