# The Feast of Weeks

The Feast of Weeks, known to many as Pentecost, is one of the most significant
yet often misunderstood festivals in the Bible. It stands as a bridge between
the early harvest and the later ingathering, carrying deep spiritual meaning
from the foot of Mount Sinai to the upper room in Jerusalem. Why did the early
apostles continue to observe this day, and what does it reveal about the
relationship between ancient laws and the presence of Christ? This article
explores the rich history and enduring relevance of this sacred occasion.

## New Testament Observance

Evidence in the New Testament suggests that the early apostles continued to
observe the biblical feasts after the resurrection of Christ. The apostle Paul,
for example, demonstrated a commitment to these traditions. In his travels, he
expressed a desire to reach Jerusalem in time for the day of Pentecost (Acts
20:16 (ESV)). He also planned to remain in Ephesus until Pentecost, indicating
that the festival served as a significant marker in his ministry (1 Corinthians
16:8 (ESV)).

Furthermore, Paul encouraged the believers in Corinth to maintain the spiritual
essence of the festivals. He urged them to cleanse out the old leaven and
celebrate the festival with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth. In this
context, he identified Christ as the ultimate Passover lamb who has been
sacrificed.

> For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. Let us therefore celebrate
> the festival, not with the old leaven, the leaven of malice and evil, but with
> the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth. — 1 Corinthians 5:7-8 (ESV)

## Perspectives on Law and Tradition

There are different viewpoints regarding the continuing role of the biblical
feasts in the life of a believer. Some point to passages in the epistles that
seem to caution against a return to legalistic observance. Paul wrote to the
Galatians expressing concern that their observation of days, months, seasons,
and years might indicate a return to "weak and worthless elementary principles"
(Galatians 4:8-10 (ESV)). He emphasized that Christ came to redeem those under
the law so they might receive adoption as sons.

> But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman,
> born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might
> receive adoption as sons. — Galatians 4:4-5 (ESV)

Similarly, in his letter to the Colossians, Paul stated that the "record of
debt" and its legal demands were canceled and nailed to the cross.

> And you, who were dead in your trespasses and the uncircumcision of your
> flesh, God made alive together with him, having forgiven us all our
> trespasses, by canceling the record of debt that stood against us with its
> legal demands. This he set aside, nailing it to the cross. — Colossians
> 2:13-14 (ESV)

Paul also advised the community not to let anyone pass judgment on them
regarding food, drink, or the observance of a festival, new moon, or Sabbath
(Colossians 2:16 (ESV)). These practices are described as a "shadow of the
things to come," while the "substance belongs to Christ" (Colossians 2:17
(ESV)).

Objective analysis suggests that these warnings may be directed at the manner of
observance rather than the feasts themselves. If a festival is kept as a
burdensome ritual without the presence of Christ, it may lose its spiritual
value. Conversely, observing a feast to honor Christ can be seen as a source of
blessing. This emphasis on the presence of Christ is further illustrated by His
invitation to fellowship. He promises to enter and eat with those who open the
door to Him (Revelation 3:20 (ESV)). Furthermore, Jesus declared Himself to be
the "Lord of the Sabbath." He emphasized that sacred days were made for the
benefit of humanity rather than as a means of legalistic bondage (Mark 2:27-28
(ESV)). This is also reflected in the Old Testament, where foreigners who join
themselves to the LORD and keep His Sabbaths are promised a place in His house.

> And the foreigners who join themselves to the LORD, to minister to him, to
> love the name of the LORD, and to be his servants, everyone who keeps the
> Sabbath and does not profane it, and holds fast my covenant— these I will
> bring to my holy mountain, and make them joyful in my house of prayer. —
> Isaiah 56:6-7 (ESV)

## Defining the Feast of Weeks

The Feast of Weeks is known by several names in the biblical text, including
Shavuot, Pentecost, and the Feast of Harvest. The Hebrew name "Shavuot"
literally means "weeks." This refers to the seven-week period leading up to the
celebration. The Greek name "Pentecost" means "fifty." This signifies the fifty
days between the offering of the firstfruits and this festival.

The festival marks the firstfruits of the labor sown in the field.

> You shall keep the Feast of Harvest, of the firstfruits of your labor, of what
> you sow in the field. — Exodus 23:16 (ESV)

It serves as a reminder of the transition from slavery to freedom, calling the
people of Israel to remember their time in Egypt (Deuteronomy 16:12 (ESV)). The
Torah establishes this as a statute to be observed forever.

> it is a statute forever throughout your generations in all your dwellings. —
> Leviticus 23:14 (ESV)

## Biblical Instructions for Celebration

The timing of the Feast of Weeks is determined by counting seven full weeks from
the day after the Sabbath of the offering of Firstfruits, totaling fifty days
(Leviticus 23:15-16 (ESV)). On this day, specific actions are commanded:

* A proclamation is made to the people.
* A holy convocation is held, where the community gathers for ceremony.
* No ordinary or laborious work is to be performed.
* The day is to be kept as a perpetual statute.

## Support for the Needy

The biblical instructions for the harvest festivals include specific provisions
for the vulnerable members of society. When reaping the harvest, the people were
commanded not to reap the corners of their fields. They were also told not to
gather the gleanings left behind. Instead, these were to be left for the poor
and the sojourner.

> And when you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not reap your field
> right up to its edge, nor shall you gather the gleanings after your harvest.
> You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner: I am the LORD your
> God. — Leviticus 23:22 (ESV)

This practice ensures that the blessings of the harvest are shared with those in
need.

## Defense of Continued Observance

Critiques often argue that the "shadows" mentioned in Colossians 2:17 (ESV)
imply that the feasts are obsolete. The argument is that the "substance"
(Christ) has already arrived. However, a defense against this view suggests that
a shadow cannot exist without the light of the substance casting it. From this
perspective, the feasts continue to point toward and celebrate the reality of
Christ. The warning in Colossians 2:16 (ESV) may not be against the feasts
themselves. Instead, it may be against those who would judge believers for how
they celebrate them in light of their freedom in Christ.

The arrival of the Holy Spirit on the day of Pentecost (Acts 2:1 (ESV)) further
emphasizes the festival's ongoing significance. Rather than abolishing the day,
this event fulfilled a deeper layer of its meaning. It transformed the harvest
of grain into a harvest of souls.

## Conclusion

The Feast of Weeks is a significant
[biblical festival](#defining-the-feast-of-weeks) with deep historical and
spiritual roots. Evidence in the
[New Testament](#new-testament-observance) indicates that early apostles
continued to observe these appointed times. While
[perspectives vary](#perspectives-on-law-and-tradition) regarding the role of
these traditions today, they can be understood as
[enduring shadows](#defense-of-continued-observance) that point to the reality
of Christ. Ultimately, the festival’s
[instructions](#biblical-instructions-for-celebration) highlight the importance
of communal gathering and [caring for the vulnerable](#support-for-the-needy).
