# The Feast of Weeks (Shavuot)

The Feast of Weeks, also known as Shavuot or Pentecost, holds a central place in
the biblical calendar. It marks a significant transition from the early spring
harvest to the abundance of the summer crop. While many recognize it as the day
the Holy Spirit descended upon the apostles, its origins and requirements reveal
a deeper connection between God's provision and His people's response. This
article explores the history, observance, and spiritual meaning of this sacred
festival.

## Relevance of Feasts in the New Testament

The question of whether biblical feasts remain applicable today is a subject of
much discussion. The New Testament provides several examples of feast observance
and references among the early believers. For instance, the apostle Paul
intentionally planned his travels to accommodate feast days.

> For Paul had decided to sail past Ephesus, so that he might not have to spend
> time in Asia, for he was hastening to be at Jerusalem, if possible, on the day
> of Pentecost. — Acts 20:16 (ESV)

Paul also expressed his intention to remain in Ephesus until Pentecost in his
first letter to the Corinthians.

> But I will stay in Ephesus until Pentecost. — 1 Corinthians 16:8 (ESV)

The theological context of Christ's arrival also touches upon the relationship
of believers to the Law. Paul notes that Jesus was born under the Law to redeem
those who were under its jurisdiction.

> But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman,
> born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might
> receive adoption as sons. — Galatians 4:4,5 (ESV)

Furthermore, Paul encouraged the Corinthian church to keep the feast of
[Passover](passover.draft.md) and [Unleavened Bread](unleavened-bread.draft.md)
in a spiritual manner, identifying Christ as the
ultimate sacrifice.

> Cleanse out the old leaven that you may be a new lump, as you really are
> unleavened. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. Let us
> therefore celebrate the festival, not with the old leaven, the leaven of
> malice and evil, but with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth. — 1
> Corinthians 5:7,8 (ESV)

## The Origin of the Feast of Weeks

The Feast of Weeks is known by several names in Scripture, including Shavuot
(weeks), Pentecost (fiftieth), and the Feast of Harvest. It is one of the three
pilgrimage festivals where all males were required to appear before the LORD.

> You shall keep the Feast of Harvest, of the firstfruits of your labor, of what
> you sow in the field. You shall keep the Feast of Ingathering at the end of
> the year, when you gather in from the field the fruit of your labor. — Exodus
> 23:16 (ESV)

The name "Weeks" comes from the command to count seven full weeks from the day
of the wave offering during the Feast of Unleavened Bread. The fiftieth day is
the celebration of Shavuot. This festival served as a reminder of God's
provision in the land and the freedom He gave Israel after their slavery in
Egypt.

> You shall remember that you were a slave in Egypt; and you shall be careful to
> observe these statutes. — Deuteronomy 16:12 (ESV)

## Instructions for the Harvest Festival

The biblical requirements for observing the Feast of Weeks include specific
timing, gatherings, and labor restrictions. The count begins from the day after
the Sabbath of the week of [First Fruits](first-fruit.draft.md).

> You shall count seven full weeks from the day after the Sabbath, from the day
> that you brought the sheaf of the wave offering. You shall count fifty days to
> the day after the seventh Sabbath. Then you shall present a grain offering of
> new grain to the LORD. — Leviticus 23:15,16 (ESV)

On this same day, a holy convocation is held, and no laborious work is
permitted. It was established as a statute forever.

> And you shall make a proclamation on the same day. You shall hold a holy
> convocation. You shall not do any ordinary work. It is a statute forever in
> all your dwellings throughout your generations. — Leviticus 23:21 (ESV)

The feast also emphasized care for the poor and the stranger. Owners were
commanded not to reap the corners of their fields or gather the gleanings of
their harvest, leaving them for those in need.

> And when you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not reap your field
> right up to its edge, nor shall you gather the gleanings after your harvest.
> You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner: I am the LORD your
> God. — Leviticus 23:22 (ESV)

## New Testament Fulfillment and Pentecost

In the New Testament, the Feast of Weeks reached a significant milestone. The
book of Acts records that the disciples were gathered together when the Holy
Spirit was poured out.

> When the day of Pentecost arrived, they were all together in one place. — Acts
> 2:1 (ESV)

This event is often seen as the fulfillment of the feast, where the firstfruits
of the New Covenant church were gathered. Just as the firstfruits of the wheat
harvest were offered to God, the believers became the firstfruits of a greater
harvest. This spiritual gathering is also hinted at in other scriptures where
the Lord invites people into His presence.

> Behold, I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the
> door, I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me. — Revelation
> 3:20 (ESV)

The concept of believers as firstfruits is a recurring theme in the New
Testament, illustrating their unique position in God's plan.

> Of his own will he brought us forth by the word of truth, that we should be a
> kind of firstfruits of his creatures. — James 1:18 (ESV)

The concept of honoring God through these appointed times extends to all people,
including foreigners who choose to join themselves to the LORD. Isaiah describes
a blessing for those who keep His Sabbaths and hold fast to His covenant.

> And the foreigners who join themselves to the LORD, to minister to him, to
> love the name of the LORD, and to be his servants, everyone who keeps the
> Sabbath and does not profane it, and holds fast my covenant — these I will
> bring to my holy mountain, and make them joyful in my house of prayer. —
> Isaiah 56:6,7 (ESV)

## Defense of Feast Observance

Interpreting New Testament passages regarding the feasts often leads to
different conclusions. Some see these festivals as obsolete, while others find
ongoing value in their observance.

In Galatians 4:9,10, Paul rebukes the Galatians for observing "days and months
and seasons and years." One perspective is that Paul was concerned about a
return to legalistic observance as a requirement for salvation.

> But now that you have come to know God, or rather to be known by God, how can
> you turn back again to the weak and worthless elementary principles of the
> world, whose slaves you want to be once more? You observe days and months and
> seasons and years! — Galatians 4:9,10 (ESV)

However, some scholars suggest that these "elementary principles" refer to pagan
influences or a syncretism of Jewish and pagan practices that the Galatians were
returning to. In this view, Paul was not condemning the feasts themselves but
their misuse or the belief that they were necessary for justification.

The mention of festivals as a "shadow" in Colossians 2:16,17 is also a point of
debate.

> Therefore let no one pass judgment on you in questions of food and drink, or
> with regard to a festival or a new moon or a Sabbath. These are a shadow of
> the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ. — Colossians 2:16,17
> (ESV)

One interpretation is that the shadow is replaced by the substance, Christ,
rendering physical observance unnecessary. Another view suggests that the feasts
continue to point to Christ and His work. From this perspective, the "judgment"
Paul mentions refers to others criticizing believers for how they celebrate
these days. If the focus remains on honoring Christ, the specific manner of
observance becomes secondary to the presence of the Lord. Jesus Himself
emphasized this principle when He declared His authority over the Sabbath.

> And he said to them, "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath.
> So the Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath." — Mark 2:27,28 (ESV)

Furthermore, Colossians 2:14 mentions the "canceling the record of debt" (ESV).
The Greek term used here is *cheirographon*, which refers to a certificate of
debt or a handwritten account of sins. Some theologians argue that it is this
record of debt, rather than the Law or the feasts, that was nailed to the cross.
This distinction allows for the continued observance of the feasts as a way to
honor God's redemptive plan and enter into His presence.

## Conclusion

The Feast of Weeks serves as a bridge between the physical harvest and spiritual
fulfillment. It originated as a celebration of [God's
provision](#the-origin-of-the-feast-of-weeks) and included specific
[instructions for community care](#instructions-for-the-harvest-festival). In
the New Testament, it marks the [outpouring of the Holy
Spirit](#new-testament-fulfillment-and-pentecost). While there are
[varying views on its modern observance](#defense-of-feast-observance), its
significance in [New Testament
history](#relevance-of-feasts-in-the-new-testament) remains undeniable.
