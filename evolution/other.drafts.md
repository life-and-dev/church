# The Evolution of Protestant and Modern Christian Movements

The history of the Church is marked by periods of intense reform and the
emergence of diverse movements. This article explores how various leaders and
groups sought to return to biblical foundations. It also examines those who
introduced new interpretations. These events shaped the landscape of modern
Christianity.

## Jan Hus

Jan Hus was a former priest and university master from Bohemia. He was
influenced by the writings of John Wycliffe. Hus believed that the Bible took
precedence over church leaders and councils. This belief questioned the
authority of the Church at that time.

He argued that only Christ could be the head of the Church. He believed that
God, through His scripture, was the final authority. Hus also spoke against
simoniacal practices. These practices included selling pardons for sin or
positions in the church. He believed that Christians could communicate
directly with God.

Jan Hus was put on trial. On 6 July 1415, he was condemned as a heretic. He
was sentenced to death before the Council of Constance. His followers, known
as Hussites, continued to advocate for his reforms.

## Martin Luther

Martin Luther nailed 95 theses on the door of the Wittenberg Castle Church on
31 October 1517. This act is often cited as the spark for the Protestant
Reformation. Luther argued that the whole life of believers should be one of
repentance. He did not believe repentance was a sacramental act administered
by priests.

Luther taught that the pope cannot forgive sin against God. He argued that no
one can repent on behalf of the dead. He condemned priests who claimed the
power to forgive or reverse the forgiveness of sin. Luther stated that the
pope does not hold the keys of death. He also argued that no one can buy
souls out of purgatory.

Every Christian has the right to the forgiveness of sin without letters of
pardon. Every Christian has the right to the blessings of Christ and the
Church even without these letters. Luther believed that giving to the poor is
a better work than buying pardons. He taught that Christians should care for
their families instead of wasting money on pardons.

The Gospel is the true treasure of the Church. It is the most holy Gospel of
the glory and grace of God. Luther questioned why the pope did not empty
purgatory out of love rather than for money. He asked why the wealthy pope did
not build churches with his own money.

This movement led to several key reforms:

* Souls are saved by faith and the grace of God. This is based on the belief
  that "for by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your
  own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may
  boast" — Ephesians 2:8,9 (ESV).
* Scripture is the sole authority for faith and practice. This concept is
  known as Sola Scriptura. It relies on the teaching that "all Scripture is
  breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for reproof, for
  correction, and for training in righteousness" — 2 Timothy 3:16 (ESV).
* Scripture should be taught in the local language of the community.
* The church structure shifted focus toward local preachers.
* Two sacraments were kept but altered: baptism and communion.
* Five sacraments were downgraded or removed by many denominations.
* Seven books were rejected from the Old Testament canon. These books are
  Baruch, Judith, 1 and 2 Maccabees, Sirach, Tobit, and Wisdom.

The Reformation also influenced King Henry VIII. He declared the Church of
England independent of Rome. This created a national church with the
Archbishop of Canterbury as its head. Priests were allowed to marry. The
Anglican Church also rejected papal supremacy and the seven books.

## Lutheranism

Today, Lutheranism is the official religion in several Nordic countries.
These include Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, and Finland. Lutheran
theology teaches that the Eucharist involves the real presence of Christ's
body and blood. Bishops govern the church in many Lutheran traditions.
Lutherans generally reject the predestination theology associated with
Calvinism.

## Anabaptists

In 1525, Swiss reformers Conrad Grebel, Felix Manz, and George Blaurock
baptized themselves as adults. They used immersion for this act. This was a
radical break from the Catholic tradition of infant baptism. It marked the
beginning of the Anabaptist movement.

Anabaptist theology includes:

* Adult believer baptism. This means baptism is a conscious choice for those
  who have already come to faith. This follows the command to "go therefore
  and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father
  and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" — Matthew 28:19 (ESV).
* Separation of church and state. The church should not be involved in
  government or politics.
* Pacifism. Christians should not participate in war or violence.
* Community of goods. Some groups share all possessions and resources.

Anabaptists often take the Bible literally. They place a great emphasis on
discipleship, community, and social justice.

## Calvinism

In 1536, John Calvin published "Institutes of the Christian Religion". He
was a French theologian and reformer in Geneva. This book shaped the
Calvinist branch of the Reformation.

Calvinist theology includes:

* Sovereignty of God. Nothing happens without the will of God.
* Authority of Scripture. The Bible is the only source of religious
  authority.
* Predestination. God has predestined some people for salvation and others
  for damnation. This is often linked to the "once saved, always saved"
  doctrine.
* Justification by faith. People are saved by grace through faith, not by
  works.
* Total depravity. All people are born sinners and cannot save themselves.
* The Church is a community of believers who form the body of Christ.
* Infant baptism. Calvin believed everyone should be baptized. He
  acknowledged that the act itself does not save.
* Eucharist. It is a symbolic memorial of Christ's death and resurrection.
* Council of elders. The church is governed by elders instead of a bishop.

## Puritans

The Puritan movement gained momentum in 1547 under Edward VI. Their goal was
to purify the Church of England of remaining Roman Catholic elements.
Edward VI appointed Protestant reformers to key positions. He revised the
Book of Common Prayer. He also ordered the destruction of Catholic shrines
and images.

Puritan influences include:

* Rejection of the authority of the pope. They believed each congregation
  should be autonomous and governed by its own elders.
* Simplified church services. They removed elaborate rituals and ceremonies.
  They focused on preaching and prayer.
* Emphasis on preaching. They believed preaching was the most important part
  of the service.
* Promotion of education. They believed education was essential for
  understanding the Bible.
* A strict moral code. They condemned activities like dancing, gambling, and
  theatre-going.

## Socinianism

John Assheton was a former Anglican priest. He rejected the Trinitarian
doctrine. He was forced to recant in 1548. In 1553, Michael Servetus was
denounced as a heretic for also rejecting the Trinity. He was sentenced to
death by burning under the orders of John Calvin.

These ideas were spread by Italian humanists in Poland and Transylvania.
Lelio and Fausto Sozzini started the Socinianism movement. Fausto published
writings in the 1580s that influenced the Remonstrant movement.

## Unitarianism

The Unitarian movement began around the same time as the Protestant
Reformation. In 1568, the first Unitarian Church was founded in the Eastern
Hungarian Kingdom. Ferenc David was the founder. Unitarian theology rejects
the Trinity. Unitarians believe there is only one God who is one person.

## King James Bible

England was religiously divided in the early 17th century. In 1604, King
James I authorized 47 scholars to create a new translation. This became the
King James Version.

The project had several aims:

* To promote religious unity.
* To enhance the authority of the king.
* To make the Bible more accessible to the common people.

Many modern English translations are still influenced by this work.

## Baptist Reformation

In 1608, John Smyth fled to Amsterdam. He was a former Anglican priest. He
joined a Dutch Anabaptist group. Smyth became convinced that baptism was for
believers and not infants. He baptized himself and founded what is
considered the first Baptist church.

The Baptist church differed from Anabaptists in some ways. They did not
always believe in a strictly literal interpretation of every passage. They
did not practice the community of goods.

## Quakers

In 1647, George Fox experienced a spiritual awakening. He began preaching
about the "inner light" of God within each person. He founded the Religious
Society of Friends in 1652. They are also known as Quakers.

Quakers faced intense persecution in England. Many were jailed. In 1681,
William Penn founded Pennsylvania as a safe haven. Quakers played a role in
the American Revolution. They advocated for non-violence and the abolition
of slavery.

Quaker beliefs include:

* Inner Light. Each individual has an inner light of God to guide them.
* Simplicity. They avoid ostentatious displays and maintain plain meeting
  houses.
* Pacifism. They believe violence is never justified.
* Equality. All people are inherently equal.
* Direct Worship. They gather in silence and speak as moved by the Spirit.
* Consensus Decision-Making. All members participate in decisions.
* Social Activism. They work to address poverty and injustice.

## Methodism

John Wesley was an Anglican priest in the 1730s. He preached outdoors and
formed small groups called Methodist societies. Methodist theology includes
the doctrine of "Entire Sanctification". This is a state of holiness
achieved through the Holy Spirit.

Wesley taught that believers should strive to become like Christ. Some see
his teachings on prosperity as an early form of the prosperity gospel. His
brother, Charles Wesley, wrote thousands of hymns. Methodism broke away from
the Anglican Church in 1795.

## Plymouth Brethren

In 1825, a group in Dublin began meeting for prayer and fellowship. This
became the Plymouth Brethren movement. John Nelson Darby joined in 1827. He
introduced the concept of a "pretribulational rapture". This view suggests
Christians will be taken to heaven before a period of great tribulation.

In 1970, Hal Lindsey published "The Late Great Planet Earth". This book
popularized the idea of the rapture. It reached millions of readers
worldwide.

## Mormonism

Joseph Smith founded the Mormon Church on 6 April 1830. This is also known as
the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Smith claimed to receive
revelations from God. These are contained in the Book of Mormon.

Mormon beliefs differ from traditional Christianity:

* They include additional scriptures like the Book of Mormon.
* They believe God is a physical being with a body.
* They believe Jesus is the human son of God.
* They believe people were spirit children of God before birth.
* They practice baptism for the dead and temple sealing.
* They believe salvation comes through faith and keeping commandments.

## Seventh-day Adventists

William Miller predicted the second coming of Christ in 1844. When this did
not happen, it was called the Great Disappointment. A group studied the
Bible and formed the Seventh-day Adventist Church in 1863.

Their beliefs include:

* The seventh-day Sabbath. They observe the Sabbath as a day of rest.
* The imminent second coming of Christ.
* The state of the dead. They believe the dead are unconscious until the
  resurrection.
* The heavenly sanctuary. Christ ministers there as High Priest.
* The investigative judgment. Christ is currently determining who will be
  saved.

## New Apostolic Church

The New Apostolic Church was founded in 1863 in Hamburg. It split from the
Catholic Apostolic Church. They believe God still raises up apostles and
prophets today. They emphasize the sacraments of baptism, holy communion,
and sealing.

## Revised Bible Translations

In 1870, the Church of England appointed scholars to write the English
Revised Version. They used better understanding of Hebrew and Greek texts.
They also wanted to update the language from the King James Version.

Many modern translations are based on this work. These include the ASV, RSV,
NASB, NRSV, ESV, NET, and NIV.

## Jehovah's Witnesses

Charles Taze Russell founded this movement in 1872. They were first known as
the International Bible Students Association. Russell published the Watch
Tower magazine.

Jehovah's Witness beliefs include:

* Rejection of the Trinity. They believe God is one person named Jehovah.
* Jesus is a created being. He is not equal to God the Father.
* The death of Jesus was a ransom sacrifice to pay the debt of sin.
* Conditional immortality. The soul is mortal. Only the chosen will be
  resurrected.
* Rejection of holidays like Christmas and Easter. They consider them to
  have pagan origins.
* Door-to-door evangelism.
* Literal interpretation of certain prophecies. They believe 144,000 will
  live in heaven. Others will live on a restored paradise earth.

## Pentecostalism

William J. Seymour founded the first Pentecostal church in 1906. This was the
Apostolic Faith Church of Azusa Street. Pentecostals believe in the baptism
of the Holy Spirit. They believe this empowers believers to speak in
tongues and perform miracles.

They emphasize personal experience with God. They also place a strong
emphasis on evangelism and sharing the gospel.

## Modern Messianic Movement

Joseph Rabinovitz founded a Messianic congregation in 1887. He was a Russian
Jew who converted to Christianity. The movement grew with the establishment
of the State of Israel in 1948.

The movement uses various Bible translations. These include the Complete
Jewish Bible and the New Messianic Version. Messianic Jews generally
believe that Jesus is the Messiah. They often maintain Jewish traditions
and culture.

## Old Apostolic Church

Friedrich Wilhelm founded the Old Apostolic Church in 1921. It split from
the Catholic Apostolic Church. They believe they have preserved original
apostolic teachings.

OAC beliefs include:

* Exclusivity. They believe salvation is only possible through their church.
  Supporters argue this encourages total commitment to their community.
* Essential sacraments for salvation.
* Strict moral codes. These include prohibitions on alcohol and certain
  entertainment.
* A hierarchical leadership structure with apostles.

## Faith Movement

The Faith Movement began in the mid-20th century in the United States. It is
also known as the "Word of Faith" or "Prosperity Gospel". Key figures
include E.W. Kenyon and Kenneth Hagin.

The movement teaches that faith and positive confession can lead to
prosperity. Televangelism helped spread these ideas in the 1960s and 1970s.
Supporters argue that God desires the well-being of His people. They often
point to biblical examples of blessings. It remains a notable but
controversial part of modern Christianity.

## Universal Church

Bishop Edir Macedo founded the Universal Church of the Kingdom of God in
1977. He was a former Catholic. The church is known for its emphasis on the
prosperity gospel.

UCKG teachings include:

* Achieving financial prosperity through faith.
* The obligation to tithe.
* The Holy Spirit as a force for healing and solving problems.
* Encouraging members to speak in tongues.

Critiques of the movement often focus on its financial practices. Some argue
that it exploits the poor. Supporters believe it provides spiritual and
practical tools for success. They argue that teaching financial
responsibility and faith helps people improve their lives.

## Conclusion

The evolution of Christian movements shows a recurring desire for reform.
Many groups sought to return to [Scripture](#martin-luther) as the sole
authority. Others introduced new [revelations](#mormonism) or focused on
[personal experience](#pentecostalism). These movements have shaped the
modern [denominational](#lutheranism) landscape. They reflect a variety of
beliefs about [salvation](#old-apostolic-church) and the [nature of
God](#unitarianism).
