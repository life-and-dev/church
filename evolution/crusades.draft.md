# The Crusades

The Crusades were a series of religious and military campaigns initiated by the Latin Church during the Middle Ages. These events spanned several centuries and involved complex motivations ranging from spiritual devotion to economic gain. While often remembered for their intensity, the Crusades were deeply influenced by the political and social climate of both Europe and the Middle East.

## Historical Context

In 1071, the Byzantine Empire suffered a significant defeat at the [Battle of Manzikert](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Manzikert). This loss resulted in the Seljuk Turks gaining control over much of Anatolia. The Byzantine Empire sought military assistance from Western Europe to regain control of these territories. This request for help provided a catalyst for the first series of [campaigns](https://www.history.com/articles/crusades) aimed at reclaiming "the holy places" in the Holy Land.

## Religious Motivations

Religious fervor was a primary driver for many participants. Popes called for these Crusades as "God's will." They promised spiritual rewards for those who took up the cross.

* **Defending Christianity**: The campaigns were framed as a defense of Christian pilgrims and the Eastern Church.
* **[Securing Salvation](https://smarthistory.org/how-was-crusading-justified-2-of-4/)**: Participation was often presented as a form of penance. Crusaders were promised the remission of sins.

> All who die by the way, whether by land or by sea, or in battle against the pagans, shall have immediate remission of sins. — Pope Urban II

Participants often viewed their actions through the lens of scripture. They saw themselves as following the call to sacrifice.

> And whoever does not take his cross and follow me is not worthy of me. — Matthew 10:38 (ESV)

Some also identified with the laments of the Old Testament regarding the state of Jerusalem.

> O God, the nations have come into your inheritance; they have defiled your holy temple; they have laid Jerusalem in ruins. — Psalm 79:1 (ESV)

## Economic Factors

Beyond religious zeal, there were significant economic motivations. Merchants, particularly from Italian city-states like Venice and Genoa, sought to control trade routes in the Mediterranean. These campaigns provided opportunities to profit from transporting crusaders and supplying their armies. Additionally, the Crusades offered land and wealth to younger sons of European nobility who stood to inherit little in their home countries.

## Contrasting Worldviews

The Crusades were viewed differently by the opposing sides. For many Western Christians, the campaigns were a "Just War" intended to recover lands that had been part of the Christian world. They saw their actions as a fulfillment of divine duty. In contrast, the Muslim world viewed these incursions as an aggressive invasion by foreign "Franks." While Christians justified the wars as a recovery of sacred property and a path to salvation, Muslim leaders often invoked the concept of Jihad to defend their territories and religious identity.

## Critiques and Defenses

The Crusades have been the subject of intense debate throughout history. Critics often point to the violence and the contradiction between the campaigns and the peaceful teachings of Jesus.

> Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called sons of God. — Matthew 5:9 (ESV)

> Put your sword back into its place. For all who take the sword will perish by the sword. — Matthew 26:52 (ESV)

In defense, some historians argue that the Crusades were a delayed response to centuries of military expansion. They suggest that the campaigns were not unprovoked but were a reaction to the loss of Christian lands and the persecution of pilgrims. They also note that the concept of a holy war reflected the prevailing cultural norms of the Middle Ages.
