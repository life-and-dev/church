# Bible Ownership

Have you ever wondered if there was a time when owning a Bible could mark you
as a criminal? For centuries, the question of who had the right to read and
interpret the Scriptures was a central point of conflict in Europe. This
tension reached a boiling point in the thirteenth century, leading to official
decrees that fundamentally changed the relationship between the common person
and the Word of God.

## The Council of Toulouse (1229)

The Council of Toulouse was a local church council held in southern France. It
was called to address the spread of movements that the Roman Catholic Church
considered heretical. One of its most significant actions was the formal
restriction of Bible ownership among the laity.

Canon 14 of the council stated:

> We prohibit also that the laity should be permitted to have the books of the
> Old or New Testament; unless anyone from the motives of devotion should wish
> to have the Psalter or the Breviary for divine offices or the hours of the
> blessed Virgin; but we most strictly forbid their having any translation of
> these books. — Council of Toulouse, 1229

This decree meant that while a person might own a book of prayers or psalms in
Latin, they were strictly forbidden from possessing the actual text of the
Bible, especially in their own spoken language.

## The Albigensian Crusade Context

The restrictions at Toulouse did not emerge in a vacuum. They were part of a
larger effort to suppress the Albigensian (or Cathar) movement. This group held
beliefs that stood in stark contrast to Catholic orthodoxy. They often used
their own vernacular translations of the Bible to support their teachings.

The church responded with the Albigensian Crusade and later the establishment
of the Papal Inquisition. By controlling access to the Scriptures, the church
aimed to prevent the spread of "heretical" ideas and ensure that all religious
teaching remained under the strict supervision of the clergy. For more on the
centralized control of this era, see the article on the
[1233 Papal Inquisition](1233-papal-inquisition.md).

## The Council of Tarragona (1234)

The momentum from Toulouse continued into other regions. In 1234, the Council
of Tarragona in Spain issued an even stricter decree. It required that any
person in possession of a Bible in the "Romance" (vernacular) language must
hand it over to be burned.

> No one may possess the books of the Old and New Testaments in the Romance
> language, and if anyone possesses them he must turn them over to the local
> bishop within eight days after the promulgation of this decree, so that they
> may be burned. — Council of Tarragona, 1234

## Perspectives on the Prohibition

Critics of these decrees argue that they were a means of maintaining
institutional power by keeping the population in spiritual ignorance. By
ensuring that only the clergy could read the Bible, the church could position
itself as the sole mediator between God and man.

However, from the perspective of the medieval church, these measures were seen
as a necessary defense of the faith. They argued that "unauthorized"
translations often contained errors or deliberate distortions. They believed
that without proper training, common people would "boldly" misinterpret the
Scriptures to their own spiritual ruin. The goal, in their view, was to protect
the integrity of the divine message from being corrupted by those who lacked
the theological education to handle it correctly.

## The Biblical Call for Personal Study

In contrast to these medieval restrictions, the Bible itself often encourages
individuals to engage directly with the Scriptures. The authors of the New
Testament praised those who verified teachings by searching the Word for
themselves.

### Searching the Scriptures Daily

When the apostle Paul preached in the city of Berea, the local residents were
commended for their diligent study:

> Now these Jews were more noble than those in Thessalonica; they received the
> word with all eagerness, **examining the Scriptures daily** to see if these
> things were so. — Acts 17:11 (ESV)

### Equipped for Every Good Work

The apostle Paul also emphasized the sufficiency and importance of the
Scriptures for every believer:

> All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for
> reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of
> God may be complete, **equipped for every good work**. — 2 Timothy 3:16-17
> (ESV)

### A Lamp to the Path

The Psalmist described the Word of God as a personal guide for the individual:

> Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path. — Psalm 119:105 (ESV)

## Conclusion

The history of Bible ownership reveals a long struggle over religious authority
and personal access to truth. While councils like Toulouse and Tarragona
sought to restrict the Scriptures to protect church unity
[The Council of Toulouse (1229)](#the-council-of-toulouse-1229), the Bible
itself presents the Word as a gift intended for all people to study and follow
[The Biblical Call for Personal Study](#the-biblical-call-for-personal-study).
Ultimately, the tension between institutional control and individual
investigation remains a defining theme in the history of faith
