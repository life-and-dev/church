# The Prohibition of Bible Ownership

Why would a religious institution forbid the very book it claims to follow? For
centuries during the Middle Ages, the ownership of a Bible was not a basic right
for believers but a crime that could lead to severe punishment. This policy was
not an accident but a calculated effort to maintain control over religious
thought.

## The Council of Toulouse 1229

In the 13th century, the Roman Catholic Church faced a growing threat from the
Albigensian movement in southern France. To combat what they perceived as
heresy, local church leaders convened the Council of Toulouse. One of the most
significant decrees from this gathering targeted the accessibility of the
scriptures to common people.

> We prohibit also that the laity should be permitted to have the books of the
> Old or New Testament... we most strictly forbid their having any translation
> of these books. — Council of Toulouse, 1229

The church argued that the laity lacked the education to interpret the Bible
correctly. They believed that personal reading without priestly guidance would
lead to dangerous errors.

## Expansion of the Ban

The restrictions did not stop in Toulouse. In 1234, the Council of Tarragona in
Spain issued an even more severe warning to the public.

> No one may possess the books of the Old and New Testaments in the Romance
> language, and if anyone possesses them he must turn them over to the local
> bishop within eight days after the promulgation of this decree, so that they
> may be burned. — Council of Tarragona, 1234

Later, in England, the 1408 Constitutions of Oxford were enacted to suppress
the Lollard movement. These laws made it illegal to translate any text of Holy
Scripture into English without official church authorization.

## Reasoning for the Prohibition

Church leaders claimed that these measures were defensive. They wanted to
protect the simple minded from being misled by unauthorized translations or
heretical interpretations. From their perspective, the Bible was a complex and
sacred tool that only the ordained clergy were qualified to handle. This
centralized control ensured that the church remained the sole mediator between
God and the people.

## Biblical Support for Study

The prohibition of scripture ownership stands in contrast to the examples found
within the Bible itself. The New Testament often commends those who examine the
scriptures personally to verify the truth of what they are taught.

### The Noble Bereans

When the apostle Paul preached in Berea, the listeners did not simply accept
his words on his authority alone. Instead, they were praised for their
diligence in checking the scriptures.

> Now these Jews were more noble than those in Thessalonica; they received the
> word with all eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see if these
> things were so. — Acts 17:11 (ESV)

### Instruction for All

The scriptures themselves describe their own purpose as being profitable for all
believers. There is no indication in the text that this benefit was restricted
only to a specific class of priests.

> All Scripture is breathed out by God and profitable for teaching, for
> reproof, for correction, and for training in righteousness, that the man of
> God may be complete, equipped for every good work. — 2 Timothy 3:16,17 (ESV)

The Old Testament also encouraged the constant study of the law by the people
of Israel.

> This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you shall meditate
> on it day and night, so that you may be careful to do according to all that
> is written in it. For then you will make your way prosperous, and then you
> will have good success. — Joshua 1:8 (ESV)

## Contrasting Perspectives

Critiques of the church's actions during this period often focus on the
suppression of individual liberty and the prevention of spiritual growth.
However, some historians and Catholic apologists argue that the decrees were
local and specific to regions facing violent heretical uprisings. They suggest
the church was not against the Bible itself but against corrupt translations.
These translations were seen as being used to subvert social and religious
order.

Despite these defenses, the historical reality remains that for many centuries,
the common person was denied direct access to the word of God in their own
language. This created a barrier that was only broken down by the advent of the
printing press and the efforts of later reformers.

## Conclusion

The prohibition of Bible ownership was a powerful tool used by the medieval
church to maintain theological and social control. While the church claimed
these measures protected believers from error, the biblical record highlights
the importance of personal scripture study for every individual.
