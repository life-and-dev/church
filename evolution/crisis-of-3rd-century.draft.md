# The Crisis of the Third Century

The Crisis of the Third Century was a turbulent 50-year period from 235 to 284 AD, marked by intense political instability, civil war, and economic turmoil that nearly led to the collapse of the Roman Empire. During this time, at least 26 emperors rose and fell, many ruling for only a few months before being assassinated.

## Political Instability

The crisis began in 235 with the assassination of Emperor Severus Alexander and continued for five decades, with Roman generals fighting one another for control of the empire. By 268, the empire had fractured into three competing states: the Gallic Empire, the Palmyrene Empire, and the independent Roman Empire proper. By this time, emperors were no longer claiming to be divine, though they were still associated with divine authority.

## Religious Developments

In 274 AD, the Roman [emperor Aurelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurelian) established the cult of [Sol Invictus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sol_Invictus) ("Unconquered Sun") as an official religion alongside the traditional Roman cults. While tradition claims a temple dedication on December 25, 274, modern scholars dispute this specific date, noting limited evidence for a December 25 Sol festival before the mid-4th century.

## Diocletian's Reforms

The Crisis of the Third Century only [ended when Diocletian became emperor](https://www.thecollector.com/what-was-the-crisis-of-the-third-century/) in 284. Diocletian stabilized the empire with a co-emperor system and clear lines of succession and governance.

## Conclusion

The Crisis of the Third Century was a defining period in Roman history, marked by [intense political instability](#political-instability), civil war, and economic turmoil. The crisis began with the assassination of Emperor Severus Alexander in 235 and ended with the rise of Diocletian in 284, whose [reforms stabilized the empire](#diocletians-reforms). During this time, the cult of Sol Invictus was established as an official religion, reflecting the [changing religious landscape](#religious-developments) of the Roman Empire.
