# Clerical Marriages

The question of whether clergy should marry has been a point of debate for
centuries. In the medieval period, a significant shift occurred that redefined
the lives of priests and the structure of the church. This change was not
merely about personal lifestyle choices. It was a strategic move to ensure the
independence and purity of the ecclesiastical body.

## The Gregorian Reform

During the 11th century, the church faced internal and external pressures.
Many local lords controlled church offices and treated them as personal
possessions. This practice led to the appointment of relatives to high
positions regardless of their spiritual qualifications. The movement known as
the Gregorian Reform sought to reclaim the church's autonomy. Pope Gregory VII
became the leading figure in this effort. He aimed to purify the clergy and
eliminate secular interference in church appointments.

## Lenten Synod of 1074

In 1074, Pope Gregory VII held a Lenten Synod in Rome. During this council, he
issued a decree that strictly enforced clerical celibacy. The decree
commanded that those who were already married must leave their wives. Those
who were not yet married were forbidden from doing so if they wished to
receive holy orders. The Pope also instructed the laity to reject the
ministrations of priests who disobeyed these rules. This was a radical step.
It effectively used the power of the people to enforce church discipline.

## Motivations for Change

The enforcement of celibacy was tied to the fight against "Nicolaitism" and
"Simony". Nicolaitism referred to clerical marriage or concubinage. Simony was
the buying and selling of church offices. Pope Gregory VII believed that
married priests were too entangled in worldly affairs. Their primary focus
often shifted from the church to their own families. There was also a concern
about church property. Married clergy might attempt to pass on church lands and
wealth to their children. By enforcing celibacy, the church ensured that its
resources remained within the institution. This helped to maintain a clear
distinction between the clergy and the secular nobility.

## Biblical Perspectives

The debate over clerical marriage often turns to the scriptures. Proponents of
celibacy point to the words of Jesus and Paul.

> For there are eunuchs who have been so from birth, and there are eunuchs who
> have been made eunuchs by men, and there are eunuchs who have made themselves
> eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. Let the one who is able to
> receive this receive it. — Matthew 19:12 (ESV)

The Apostle Paul also spoke about the benefits of remaining unmarried to focus
on the work of the Lord.

> I want you to be free from anxieties. The unmarried man is anxious about the
> things of the Lord, how to please the Lord. But the married man is anxious
> about worldly things, how to please his wife, and his interests are divided.
> — 1 Corinthians 7:32-34 (ESV)

On the other hand, those who support a married clergy cite the requirements for
church leaders found in the pastoral epistles.

> Therefore an overseer must be above reproach, the husband of one wife,
> sober-minded, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach.
> — 1 Timothy 3:2 (ESV)
>
> Let deacons each be the husband of one wife, managing their children and
> their own households well. — 1 Timothy 3:12 (ESV)

These verses suggest that marriage was a common and accepted state for early
church leaders. The author of Hebrews also expressed a high regard for the
marital bond.

> Let marriage be held in honor among all, and let the marriage bed be
> undefiled, for God will judge the sexually immoral and adulterous.
> — Hebrews 13:4 (ESV)

## Contrasting Traditions

The enforcement of celibacy in 1074 primarily affected the Western Church. The
Eastern Orthodox tradition took a different path. In the East, priests and
deacons were allowed to marry before their ordination. Only bishops were
required to be celibate. Following the Reformation, many Protestant groups
rejected the requirement of celibacy entirely. They argued that marriage is a
divinely ordained institution for all people. This remains a significant
point of difference between the Roman Catholic Church and other Christian
denominations.

## Critique and Defense

Critics of the 1074 decree argued that it was an "unheard-of example" and
contrary to the traditions of the early church fathers. Some contemporary
accounts described the hardships faced by the families of priests who were
forced to separate. The clergy in regions like Germany and England resisted
the decree with great intensity.

The defense of the decree rested on the need for spiritual purity. Supporters
argued that a celibate clergy could serve as a more perfect reflection of the
heavenly kingdom. They maintained that the church is the "bride of Christ".
Therefore, its ministers should remain devoted solely to her. This helped to
solidify the role of the priest as a figure set apart from the rest of society.

## Conclusion

The 1074 decree against clerical marriages was a pivotal moment in church
history. It was driven by a desire for [independence from secular lords](#the-gregorian-reform).
The reforms aimed to [preserve church property](#motivations-for-change) and
[ensure spiritual focus](#biblical-perspectives). While it met with
[significant resistance](#critique-and-defense), the rule of celibacy became
a defining characteristic of the [Western clerical tradition](#contrasting-traditions).
