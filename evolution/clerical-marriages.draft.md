# The 11th Century Reform of Clerical Marriage

In the 11th century, the Western church underwent a radical transformation that
fundamentally altered the lives of its clergy. This shift moved the priesthood
from a state where marriage was common to one where celibacy was strictly
enforced. At the heart of this change was a [powerful decree](
https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gregory-VII#ref10212) that sought to
redefine the identity of the priesthood. The decision sparked a conflict that
would last for decades. It pitted the centralizing authority of the papacy
against the established customs of local priests across Europe.

## A Movement for Independence

The push for clerical celibacy was part of a larger movement known as the
Gregorian Reform. This movement was named after Pope Gregory VII. The reformers
sought to free the church from the control of secular lords and kings. One of
their primary targets was simony. This was the practice of buying or selling
church offices. The reformers believed that the church should be independent.
They argued that the clergy should be a distinct and holy class. They believed
this class should be separate from the entanglements of the world.

## The Lenten Synod of 1074

In 1074, Pope Gregory VII held a Lenten Synod in Rome. During this council, he
issued a series of strict decrees. These were aimed at eliminating simony and
enforcing clerical chastity. The most controversial part of these decrees was
the instruction to the laity. Gregory commanded the people to reject the
services of any priest who refused to obey the new rules.

> Nor shall clergymen who are married say mass or serve the altar in any way.
> We decree also that if they refuse to obey our orders, or rather those of
> the holy fathers, the people shall refuse to receive their ministrations,
> in order that those who disregard the love of God and the dignity of their
> office may be brought to their senses through feeling the shame of the
> world and the reproof of the people. — Pope Gregory VII

This move was unprecedented. It bypassed the traditional hierarchy of the
church. It appealed directly to the common people to enforce papal law.

## Arguments for Mandatory Celibacy

The reformers used both theological and practical arguments to support their
cause. They emphasized the concept of ritual purity. They argued that a priest
who handled the Eucharist should be completely free from the "pollution" of
sexual relations. They also looked to the writings of the Apostle Paul.

> The unmarried man is anxious about the things of the Lord, how to please the
> Lord. But the married man is anxious about worldly things, how to please
> his wife, and his interests are divided. — 1 Corinthians 7:32-34 (ESV)

Practically, the reformers wanted to prevent church property from becoming
hereditary. If a priest had children, there was a risk that he would try to
pass church lands and wealth down to his heirs. By enforcing celibacy, the
papacy ensured that church assets remained within the institution. This
strengthened the central authority of Rome over local dioceses.

## The Voices of Resistance

The 1074 decree met with fierce resistance, especially in Germany and France.
Many priests were already married and had families. They saw the decree as an
assault on their lives and a rejection of apostolic tradition. They pointed
to the biblical requirements for overseers and elders.

> Therefore an overseer must be above reproach, the husband of one wife,
> sober-minded, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach. —
> 1 Timothy 3:2 (ESV)

Some critics accused the papacy of falling into the very traps warned about in
scripture. They argued that mandatory celibacy could lead to greater moral
failings.

> Now the Spirit expressly says that in later times some will depart from the
> faith by devoting themselves to deceitful spirits and teachings of demons,
> through the insincerity of liars whose consciences are seared, who forbid
> marriage and require abstinence from foods that God created to be received
> with thanksgiving by those who believe and know the truth. —
> 1 Timothy 4:1-3 (ESV)

They also argued that the validity of the sacraments did not depend on the
personal holiness of the priest. This was a traditional view that the
reformers seemed to be challenging by encouraging people to boycott the masses
of married priests.

## Defending the Reform

The reformers provided a defense against these critical arguments. They
maintained that the prohibition of marriage in scripture referred to heretical
groups who condemned marriage as evil for everyone. In contrast, they argued
that the church honored marriage for the laity but required a higher standard
of "angelic" purity for those serving at the altar.

The reformers also clarified their stance on the sacraments. They did not
claim that a married priest's sacraments were invalid in themselves. Instead,
they argued that the faithful should avoid such priests as a form of
discipline. This was intended to shame the disobedient clergy into compliance.
They believed that the spiritual health of the church required a clergy that
was fully dedicated to God without the distractions of family life.

## A Lasting Division

The enforcement of clerical celibacy became a defining feature of the Roman
Catholic Church. It created a sharp distinction between the Western church and
the Eastern Orthodox traditions. In the East, marriage remained permitted for
priests who were married before their ordination. In the West, the Gregorian
Reform eventually succeeded in making celibacy the standard. This change
consolidated papal power. It also created a permanent shift in how the
relationship between the clergy and the world was understood.

## Conclusion

The [1074 decree](#the-lenten-synod-of-1074) was a pivotal moment that enforced
clerical celibacy to ensure church independence and purity. This move was part
of the broader [Gregorian Reform](#a-movement-for-independence) that sought to
separate the clergy from secular influences. The mandate faced [significant
resistance](#the-voices-of-resistance) from priests who defended their families
and traditional customs. Ultimately, this reform [reshaped the Western
priesthood](#a-lasting-division) and remains a central aspect of Roman Catholic
discipline today. A [defense of these changes](#defending-the-reform) was also
provided to address the theological concerns of the time.
