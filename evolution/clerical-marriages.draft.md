# The Decree Against Clerical Marriages

The eleventh century marked a dramatic shift in the structure of the Western
Church. One of the most significant changes was the enforcement of clerical
celibacy. This transition was not merely a spiritual reform. It was a
strategic move that redefined the relationship between the clergy and the
secular world. At the heart of this transformation was a controversial mandate
that would echo through the centuries.

## The 1074 Decree

In 1074, [Pope Gregory VII issued a decree](https://www.newadvent.org/cathen/06791c.htm)
**against clerical marriages** to enforce church discipline. He sought to
separate the clergy from secular influences and secular control. This mandate
was part of a broader movement known as the Gregorian Reform. The reform
aimed to purify the church and establish papal authority over secular rulers.

## Motives for Change

Several key motives drove the enforcement of clerical celibacy. These motives
were spiritual, hierarchical, and economic in nature.

One primary reason was the ascetic ideal. Proponents of the reform believed
that a priest of God should be superior to carnal passions. They argued that
an unmarried clergy could command more reverence from the people through
perceived angelic purity.

Another motive was the desire for hierarchical independence. Gregory VII
declared that he could not free the church from the rule of the laity unless
priests were freed from their wives. A married clergy was connected to the
world by social ties and family concerns. An unmarried clergy was independent
and could be more devoted to the interests of the church.

There was also a significant economic motive. The church wanted to prevent the
danger of a hereditary caste. If priests had families, there was a risk that
they might appropriate ecclesiastical property for private use. This could
impoverish the church over time. By enforcing celibacy, the church ensured
that its landed wealth remained intact.

## Scriptural Basis for Celibacy

The church supported the practice of celibacy with several scriptural
passages. These verses highlight the benefits of remaining unmarried for the
sake of ministry.

Jesus spoke about those who choose to remain single for the sake of the
kingdom.

> For there are eunuchs who have been so from birth, and there are eunuchs who
> have been made eunuchs by men, and there are eunuchs who have made
> themselves eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. Let the one who is
> able to receive this receive it. — Matthew 19:12 (ESV)

The Apostle Paul also discussed the advantages of being unmarried in his
first letter to the Corinthians.

> I want you to be free from anxieties. The unmarried man is anxious about the
> things of the Lord, how to please the Lord. But the married man is anxious
> about worldly things, how to please his wife, and his interests are divided.
> — 1 Corinthians 7:32-34 (ESV)

## Scriptural Basis for Marriage

Opponents of enforced celibacy pointed to other scriptures that appeared to
support clerical marriage. They argued that marriage was the original
institution of God and protected moral purity.

Paul gave specific qualifications for overseers in his letter to Timothy.

> Therefore an overseer must be above reproach, the husband of one wife,
> sober-minded, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach.
> — 1 Timothy 3:2 (ESV)

Paul also defended his right to take a wife, just as the other apostles did.

> Do we not have the right to take along a believing wife, as do the other
> apostles and the brothers of the Lord and Cephas? — 1 Corinthians 9:5 (ESV)

The Gospels also mention that Peter, often considered the first leader of the
church, was married.

> And when Jesus entered Peter's house, he saw his mother-in-law lying sick
> with a fever. — Matthew 8:14 (ESV)

## Contrasting Perspectives

The issue of clerical marriage continues to be a point of divergence among
different Christian traditions. Each group interprets the scriptural and
historical evidence differently.

The Roman Catholic Church maintains the requirement for celibacy in its
priesthood. This view holds that celibacy allows the priest to have an
undivided heart for the Lord. It is seen as a sacrificial gift for the sake
of the kingdom.

In contrast, the Eastern Orthodox Church allows priests to be married. This
permission only applies if they are married before their ordination. However,
bishops must remain celibate. They view marriage as a holy state that can
complement the ministry.

Protestant traditions generally allow and often encourage their clergy to
marry. They emphasize the biblical qualifications for bishops that include
managing their own households well. They believe that a married clergy can
better relate to the family lives of their congregations.

## Addressing Critical Arguments

Critiques of the 1074 decree often focus on the hardship it caused for
existing clergy and their families. Many priests were forced to choose
between their vocation and their loved ones. Some argue that this led to
widespread misery and social disruption.

Defenders of the reform argue that the survival of the church was at stake.
They claim that without enforced celibacy, the church would have become a
collection of hereditary fiefdoms. This would have compromised its spiritual
mission and independence. They view the reform as a necessary step to protect
the integrity of the church from secular and familial interests.

## Conclusion

The decree of 1074 was a pivotal moment in church history that established
mandated celibacy for the Western clergy. This policy was rooted in
[complex motives](#motives-for-change) and supported by
[specific scriptural interpretations](#scriptural-basis-for-celibacy). While
it aimed to protect the
[independence and property of the church](#addressing-critical-arguments), it
remains a subject of
[intense theological debate](#contrasting-perspectives) among various
Christian traditions.
