# 1233 (The Papal Inquisition)

How did a message of love and sacrifice transform into a system of
institutionalized terror? In 1233, the Roman Catholic Church transitioned
from localized efforts to a centralized, papal-controlled apparatus
dedicated to the "extirpation" of heresy. This system, known as the
[Papal Inquisition](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/papal-inquisition),
introduced systematic methods of investigation, interrogation, and
punishment that redefined the relationship between the church and dissent.

## Centralized Power

For centuries, the task of rooting out heretics belonged to local bishops
(the episcopal inquisition). However, this approach was often inconsistent
and ineffective against widespread movements like
[Catharism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catharism) in southern France.

In 1231, [Pope Gregory IX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Gregory_IX)
issued a decree in his *Statutes* that called for capital punishment for
those who persisted in heresy. In 1233, he followed this with a series of
papal bulls (such as *Ille humani generis*) that took inquisitorial power
away from the bishops and placed it directly under papal control. He
appointed members of the newly formed
[Dominican](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominican_Order) and
[Franciscan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franciscan) orders as
inquisitors. These men answered only to the pope, granting them nearly
unlimited authority to investigate anyone suspected of "heretical
depravity."

## Inquisitorial Process

The Inquisition was designed to be impossible to escape. Its methods were
revolutionary for the time:

* **Secret Trials:** Proceedings were held in secret, and suspects were
  often not told the names of their accusers.
* **Lack of Defense:** By 1254,
  [Pope Innocent IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV) had
  removed the right of the accused to have legal counsel, as providing a
  defense for a heretic was itself seen as an act of heresy.
* **Systematic Records:** Every testimony and confession was meticulously
  recorded and kept in archives. Suspects could be tracked across borders
  and arrested years later based on these records.
* **Self-Incrimination:** The goal was not just to find "truth" but to
  extract a confession. The accused were often forced to testify against
  themselves and their own families (a practice later restricted in
  modern law as "Fifth Amendment" rights).

## Authorized Torture

Initially, the church avoided physical violence, following the principle
*Ecclesia non sitit sanguinem* ("The Church does not thirst for blood").
However, as the fight against heresy intensified, this restraint was
abandoned.

On 15 May 1252, Pope Innocent IV issued the bull
[**Ad extirpanda**](http://www.documentacatholicaomnia.eu/01p/1243-1254,_Innocentius_IV,_Bullarium,_LT.pdf)
(PDF link to Latin text;
[English summary here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ad_extirpanda)). This
document officially authorized inquisitors to use **torture** as a tool for
extracting confessions. While the bull technically forbade torture that
caused the loss of life or limb, in practice, these limits were frequently
circumvented by treating repeated torture sessions as a single "continued"
session.

## Execution by Fire

For the church, a "heretic" was not just someone with a different opinion;
they were a "cancer" on the body of Christ that had to be removed. If an
accused person refused to recant their beliefs, or if they relapsed after a
first confession, they were "handed over to the secular arm" for execution.

The preferred method was **burning at the stake**. By using the state (the
"secular arm") to carry out the killing, the church maintained the legal
fiction that it was not directly responsible for shedding blood, even
though it was the church that passed the sentence.

> "The heretic is to be handed over to the secular power to be punished
> according to the law [execution by fire]." — Decree of Pope Gregory IX, 1231.

## Jesus' Teachings

The practices of the Inquisition stood in direct opposition to the path set
by Jesus. Where the church used fire to destroy its enemies, Jesus forbade
his disciples from doing the same.

### Rebuking the Use of Fire

When Jesus was rejected by a Samaritan village, his disciples suggested a
"holy" fire to consume them. Jesus' response was a direct rebuke of the
spirit that would later drive the Inquisition:

> When the days drew near for him *[Jesus]* to be taken up, he set his face to
> go to Jerusalem. And he sent messengers ahead of him, who went and entered
> a village of the Samaritans, to make preparations for him.
>
> But the people did not receive him, because his face was set toward
> Jerusalem.
>
> And when his disciples James and John saw it, they said, **“Lord, do you
> want us to tell fire to come down from heaven and consume them?”**
>
> But **he turned and rebuked them**.
>
> — Luke 9:51-56 (ESV)

### Gentle Correction

Jesus also taught that his servants should be kind and patient when dealing
with those who disagree, rather than resorting to force:

> And the Lord's servant must not be quarrelsome but kind to everyone, able
> to teach, patiently enduring evil, correcting his opponents with
> gentleness. God may perhaps grant them repentance leading to a knowledge of
> the truth, and they may come to their senses and escape from the snare of
> the devil, after being captured by him to do his will.
> — 2 Timothy 2:24-26 (ESV)

### Leaving Vengeance to God

Instead of taking vengeance upon heretics, followers of Jesus were commanded
to overcome evil with good:

> Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God, for it
> is written, “Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord.” To the
> contrary, “if your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him
> something to drink; for by so doing you will heap burning coals on his
> head.” Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good.
> — Romans 12:19-21 (ESV)

### Rejecting the Sword

Jesus also warned that those who resort to physical force in his name would
ultimately be destroyed by it:

> Then Jesus said to him, "Put your sword back into its place. For **all who
> take the sword will perish by the sword**." — Matthew 26:52 (ESV)

### Commandment of Love

Finally, Jesus established love as the identifying mark of his followers.
This love stood in stark contrast to doctrinal conformity enforced by
violence.

> "A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have
> loved you, you also are to love one another. **By this all people will know
> that you are my disciples**, if you have love for one another."
> — John 13:34-35 (ESV)

## Critique and Defense

Some might argue that the Inquisition was a necessary measure to maintain
social stability and protect the spiritual well-being of the majority. From
this perspective, heresy was viewed as a dangerous threat that could lead
souls to eternal damnation. Proponents of this view believe that the
preservation of religious unity justified the use of force.

However, the teachings of Jesus emphasize that faith cannot be coerced
through fear or physical pain. True conversion is a matter of the heart and
the spirit. The use of institutionalized violence to enforce doctrinal
conformity directly contradicts the example of gentleness and non-coercion
set by the Messiah.

## Lasting Legacy

The Papal Inquisition did not end with the Middle Ages; it evolved. In 1542,
it became the [Roman Inquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Inquisition),
and eventually, the **Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith** (which
still exists today). While the use of fire and torture has been abandoned,
the underlying claim of centralized authority to judge and punish
"unorthodoxy" remains a core part of the system's history.

## Conclusion

The 1233 Papal Inquisition established a system that prioritized doctrinal
conformity over the fundamental teachings of Jesus. By authorizing torture
and execution, the church institutionalized methods that were in direct
opposition to the path of love and gentleness. This legacy serves as a
reminder of the dangers of using centralized power to enforce belief
through coercion. More information can be found in the sections on the
[Inquisitorial Process](#inquisitorial-process),
[Authorized Torture](#authorized-torture), and
[Jesus' Teachings](#jesus-teachings).
