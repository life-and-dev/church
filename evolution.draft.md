# Evolution of the Church

The transformation of the Christian church from a small Jewish sect to a
dominant global institution is a complex journey. It involves shifts in
theology, authority, and culture over many centuries. By examining the key
influences and historical turning points, one can understand how the modern
religious landscape took shape. This exploration reveals the transition from
early spiritual practices to the structured systems that define major
denominations today.

## Jewish Influence

The foundation of the church began within the context of the Jewish faith.
During the [Jewish Era](evolution/-1445-jewish-era.md), a significant shift
occurred with the removal of the divine name, the Tetragrammaton, from common
use and eventually from many scriptural manuscripts. This tradition of using
titles like "Adonai" or "the LORD" instead of the four-letter name YHWH became
widespread by the Hellenistic period.

At the time of the Messiah, the ministry of [Jesus](evolution/1st-c-jesus.md)
brought further changes. His life involved the [fulfilment of messianic
prophecies](evolution/1st-c-jesus.md#chronology-and-the-calendar) and the
delivery of new [teachings](evolution/1st-c-jesus.md#teachings-of-jesus).
These teachings often provided a fresh perspective on the Mosaic Law. His
[crucifixion](evolution/1st-c-jesus.md#trial-and-crucifixion) served as a
pivotal moment for his followers.

Following his departure, the [Messianic Era](evolution/1st-c-messianic.md)
began. This period was characterized by both the intense persecution of
believers and the rapid spreading of the Gospel message across the region.

## Early Christian Development

As the movement grew into the 1st century, the [First Christians](
evolution/1st-c-christians.md) officially adopted the name "Christian" to
distinguish themselves. However, they faced significant external pressure from
the [Roman Imperial Cult](evolution/1st-c-roman-imperial-cult.md). This cult
intensified persecution and altered the calendar to honor Greek and Roman gods.

Internal challenges also arose during this time. The influence of [Gnosis](
evolution/1st-c-gnosis.md) introduced various forms of "wisdom" that began to
blend with Christian thought. Gnosis often emphasized secret knowledge over
traditional apostolic teachings. By 156 AD, [Montanism](
evolution/156-montanism.md) emerged. This movement encouraged more
authoritative church roles and pushed for the canonization of the Bible as a
response to its new prophetic claims.

In 206 AD, [Tertullian](evolution/206-tertullian.md) introduced significant
theological concepts. These included the early framework for Trinity theology,
the concept of apostolic succession, and the formalization of the Eucharist.
Tertullian was the first to use the Latin term "Trinitas" to describe the
Father, Son, and Holy Spirit. He also emphasized the importance of bishops as
successors of the apostles. The [Crisis of the Third Century](
evolution/235-crisis-of-3rd-century.md) in 235 AD further changed the
landscape as the "divine" status of Roman emperors began to fall.

## The Roman Church

A major turning point occurred in 312 AD under [Emperor Constantine](
evolution/312-constantine.md). During his reign, the Roman Catholic Church was
institutionalized. Historical evidence, such as depictions in the Vatican
necropolis, suggests that the sun god, Sol Invictus, was at times associated
with "Jesus Christ" during this transition. This association may have
influenced the solar symbolism sometimes used in early Christian art.

The [first Nicaea Creed](evolution/325-nicaea-creed.md) in 325 AD solidified
church authority with government support. It required Christians to confess
the divinity of Jesus. It also moved the calculation of Easter away from the
Jewish Passover. This led to Christianity becoming more mixed with traditions
that were once part of pagan festivals. Some argue that this mixture
compromised the original purity of the faith, while others see it as a
necessary step for the survival and expansion of the church in the Roman world.

In 337 AD, Constantius II and Bishop Ulfilas played a role in the further
spread of different beliefs. Constantius II relaxed some church creeds. This
allowed Bishop Ulfilas to [spread Arianism](evolution/337-arianism.md) in the
Gothic regions through his translation of the Bible. Arianism taught that
Jesus was a created being and subordinate to the Father. By 363 AD, the
Council of Laodicea [instituted Sunday rest](terms/sundays.md) and formally
prohibited "Judaizing" by resting on the Sabbath.

By 379 AD, under Theodosius I, [Nicene Christianity became a state religion](
evolution/380-state-religion.md) through the Edict of Thessalonica. Other
beliefs, labeled as "heresies" like Arianism, were criminalized. In 382 AD,
St. Jerome translated [the Vulgate Bible](evolution/382-vulgate.md). This was
the first Latin Christian Bible and was produced by a supporter of Nicene
orthodoxy.

## Medieval Transitions

The landscape shifted again in 395 AD during the period often called the Dark
Ages. During this time, [all Arian nations fell](evolution/395-dark-ages.md)
in various wars, such as the campaigns of Justinian. However, Nicene
Christianity survived these conflicts and became the dominant faith.

The church continued to institute new traditions and structures. In 494 AD,
Pope Gelasius suppressed the pagan Lupercalia and helped establish the Feast
of Saint Valentine. Later, in 731 AD, Pope Gregory III instituted [All Saint's
Day](evolution/609-halloween.md) on November 1, which eventually evolved into
Halloween.

The [Holy Roman Empire](evolution/800-holy-roman-empire.md) was established in
800 AD. Pope Stephen II formed alliances that strengthened papal involvement
in politics. Papal influence spread across Europe. The church worked to
suppress any religious practices against the Roman Catholic Church.
This centralized control aimed to maintain unity but also led to the
marginalization of minority viewpoints.

In 1074 AD, Pope Gregory VII introduced the [prohibition of clerical
marriages](evolution/1074-clerical-marriages.md) as part of the Gregorian
Reform. This was followed by [the Crusades](evolution/1095-crusades.md) in 1095
AD. These were military attempts by the Byzantine Empire to regain territory.
Many soldiers believed that participating in these wars could secure their
salvation.

The 13th and 14th centuries saw further consolidation of power. In 1215 AD,
the Fourth Lateran Council under Pope Innocent III decreed that [7 sacraments](
evolution/1215-7-sacraments.md) were necessary for salvation. This council
also dogmatized the doctrine of transubstantiation. Transubstantiation is the
belief that the bread and wine of the Eucharist become the actual body and
blood of Christ. By 1229 AD, the Council of Toulouse issued a [prohibition of
Bible ownership](evolution/bible-ownership.md) for laypeople to counter
regional "heresies." Finally, in 1302 AD, Pope Boniface VIII issued the bull
Unam Sanctam. He declared that [submission under the authority of the Pope is
necessary for salvation](evolution/papal-authority.md).

## Conclusion

The history of the church is marked by a steady transition from its [Jewish
roots](#jewish-influence) to a powerful, institutionalized state religion.
Early theological developments and [external pressures](#early-christian-development)
reshaped the beliefs and practices of the first believers. The support of the
Roman Empire [institutionalized the church](#the-roman-church), leading to
significant cultural and liturgical changes. By the medieval era, the church
had achieved [unprecedented authority](#medieval-transitions) over both
spiritual and temporal life.
