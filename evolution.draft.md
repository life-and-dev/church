# The Evolution of the Church

The history of the Christian church is a complex tapestry woven from diverse
cultural, political, and religious threads. From its origins within the Jewish
tradition to its institutionalization as a state religion under the Roman
Empire, each era introduced significant changes that shaped modern beliefs and
practices. This article explores the pivotal moments and influential figures
that contributed to the evolution of the church over more than a millennium.

## Jewish Influences

The early foundations of the faith were deeply rooted in Jewish tradition and
scripture. However, over time, certain practices were altered, including the
traditional pronunciation of the divine name.

| Period | Influencers | Influence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Before Messiah | [Jewish Era][1] | Removal of God's name |
| At the Messiah | [Jesus][2] | [Prophecies][3], [teachings][4], [death][5] |
| After Messiah | [Messianic Era][6] | Persecution and Gospel spreading |

> "You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain, for the LORD will
> not hold him guiltless who takes his name in vain." — Exodus 20:7 (ESV)

## Christian Influences

As the church expanded throughout the Roman Empire and beyond, it encountered
various cultural influences and political challenges. These interactions led to
the formalization of doctrines and the establishment of powerful ecclesiastical
structures.

| Period | Influencers | Influence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st century | [First Christians][7] | Adoption of the name "Christian" |
| 1st century | [Roman Cult][8] | Persecution and calendar changes |
| 1st century | [Gnosis][9] | Gnostic "wisdom" influences |
| 156 AD | [Montanism][10] | Church roles and canonization |
| 206 AD | [Tertullian][11] | Trinity, succession, and Eucharist |
| 235 AD | [3rd Century Crisis][12] | The fall of "divine" emperors |
| 312 AD | [Constantine][13] | Church institutionalized; sun god mix |
| 325 AD | [Nicaea Creed][14] | Government support and pagan mixing |
| 337 AD | Ulfilas | [Spread of Arianism][15] |
| 363 AD | Laodicea Council | [Sunday Rest][16] and Sabbath outlawed |
| 379 AD | Theodosius I | [State religion][17] and criminalization |
| 382 AD | St. Jerome | [The Vulgate Bible][18] |
| 395 AD | The Dark Ages | [Fall of Arian nations][19] |
| 494 AD | Pope Gelasius | [Valentine's Day][20] |
| 731 AD | Pope Gregory III | [All Saint's Day][21] and Halloween |
| 800 AD | [Holy Roman Empire][22] | Papal involvement in politics |
| 1074 AD | Pope Gregory VII | [Clerical marriage prohibition][23] |
| 1095 AD | [The Crusades][24] | Wars for territory and salvation |
| 1215 AD | Pope Innocent III | [Seven sacraments][25] for salvation |
| 1229 AD | Toulouse Council | [Bible ownership prohibition][26] |
| 1302 AD | Pope Boniface VIII | [Papal authority][27] and salvation |

> "Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days you shall labor, and do
> all your work, but the seventh day is a Sabbath to the LORD your God." —
> Exodus 20:8-10 (ESV)

## Conclusion

In summary, the Christian church transitioned from a faith rooted in [Jewish
traditions](#jewish-influences) to a powerful [Roman
institution](#christian-influences). Various councils and papal decrees
established new ecclesiastical requirements for salvation. These historical
developments permanently altered the theological landscape of Christianity.
